## Basic Info

## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel light and visual, but it must not become a generic
gardening lesson.

- Modern relevance:
  - Do not assume the student has a private garden.
  - Bridge early to school displays, craft projects, posters, LEGO/Minecraft
    builds, room/desk organization, or class decoration competitions.
- Story focus:
  - The key is not "Joe has flowers." The key is that different people can win
    or lose by different standards: size, effort, quantity, design, interest,
    beauty, and humour.
- Humour:
  - The ending is self-mocking. The narrator likes gardens but does not like
    hard work, so he wins a little prize for the worst garden.
  - Keep the tone playful, not mean.
- Student-facing display:
  - Prefer `乔·桑德斯` and `比尔·弗里斯` in Chinese narration rather than raw
    punctuation like `乔.桑德斯`.
  - Explain `pool` as a small garden pond / water feature in this context, not
    a swimming pool.
  - `enter for` means join/sign up for a competition, not "walk into".

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about comparison and judging criteria.

Joe Sanders has the most beautiful garden in town. Nearly everybody enters the
garden competition, but Joe wins every time. Bill Frith seems to have stronger
"numbers": his garden is larger, he works harder, and he grows more flowers and
vegetables. But Joe's garden is more interesting because it has neat paths, a
wooden bridge, and a pool.

Then the narrator turns the comparison into a joke. He likes gardens too, but he
does not like hard work. He also enters the competition every year and always
wins a little prize for the worst garden.

The courseware should make the student feel:

`bigger / harder / more things` is not always the same as `better / more interesting / best`.

## One-Sentence Hook

In one town, almost everybody joins the garden competition. One person has the
largest garden, another works harder, but Joe wins every time because his garden
is the most interesting.

## Story Punchline

The comparison chain is:

1. Joe has the most beautiful garden in town.
2. Nearly everybody enters the competition each year.
3. Joe wins every time.
4. Bill's garden is larger than Joe's.
5. Bill works harder than Joe.
6. Bill grows more flowers and vegetables.
7. But Joe's garden is more interesting.
8. Joe has neat paths, a wooden bridge, and a pool.
9. The narrator also likes gardens, but not hard work.
10. The narrator always wins a little prize for the worst garden in town.

The ending should land as: "I am also a regular winner, just not for the reason
you expected."

## Modern Bridge

Do not ask only "Do you have a garden?" Bridge to situations where children
compare and judge projects:

- school classroom decoration
- a poster, craft, or science-fair project
- a LEGO, Minecraft, Roblox, or model-building competition
- a tidy desk or bedroom contest
- a balcony plant, small pot, or mini garden
- a team display where one group has more things but another group has better
  design

Useful modern idea:

`The best project is not always the biggest one. It may be the neatest, clearest, most interesting, or best designed.`

## Vocabulary Strategy

Use exactly 3 formal vocab blocks, but explain the comparison words throughout
the story.

### Formal Vocab Blocks

Prefer:

1. `competition`
   - A contest or评比. Connect it to school/class/project competitions.
2. `neat`
   - Clean, tidy, and well-arranged. This is about order and design.
3. `path`
   - A small route people walk along; in a garden it gives structure.

### Required Supporting Vocabulary

Explain these in context:

- `enter for`
  - Join or sign up for a competition.
- `win a prize`
  - Get a prize because of a result.
- `larger than`
  - Bigger in size.
- `works harder than`
  - Puts in more effort.
- `more flowers and vegetables`
  - More in number/quantity.
- `more interesting`
  - Better in design or feeling, not just larger.
- `wooden bridge`
  - A bridge made of wood.
- `pool`
  - A small garden pond / water feature here.
- `the worst`
  - The lowest or poorest in quality; this is the final joke.

### Comparison Word Network

The script should build a word network:

1. **Best / worst**
   `the most beautiful`, `the best`, `the worst`
2. **More / larger / harder**
   `larger than`, `harder than`, `more flowers`, `more interesting`
3. **Design words**
   `neat paths`, `wooden bridge`, `pool`

Do not make students memorize rules first. Let them see what each comparison is
measuring.

## Language Focus

Use these language points:

- `the most beautiful garden`
  - Superlative: one garden stands out above all the others.
- `enters for ... each year`
  - A repeated annual action.
- `larger than Joe's`
  - Comparison by size.
- `works harder than Joe`
  - Comparison by effort.
- `grows more flowers and vegetables`
  - Comparison by quantity.
- `more interesting`
  - Comparison by design/feeling.
- `the worst garden`
  - Superlative used for the joke.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: comparison by different criteria.

Do not teach this as a spelling chart only. Teach it as a judging table:

- Size:
  - `Bill's garden is larger than Joe's.`
- Effort:
  - `Bill works harder than Joe.`
- Quantity:
  - `Bill grows more flowers and vegetables.`
- Interest/design:
  - `Joe's garden is more interesting.`
- Overall best:
  - `Joe has the most beautiful garden.`
- Comic worst:
  - `the worst garden in the town`

Student-friendly explanation:

`When we compare things, we must ask: compare what? Size, effort, number, design, beauty, or the final result?`

## Read Aloud Sentences

Use 7 guided sentences. The goal is to make comparison language sound natural
before the student moves into modern project/comparison scenes.

- `Joe Sanders has the most beautiful garden in our town.`
  - focus: `the most beautiful` as the top result.
- `Nearly everybody enters for 'The Nicest Garden Competition' each year, but Joe wins every time.`
  - focus: `enters for`, `competition`, `wins every time`.
- `Bill Frith's garden is larger than Joe's.`
  - focus: `larger than`.
- `Bill works harder than Joe and grows more flowers and vegetables, but Joe's garden is more interesting.`
  - focus: split the long comparison at `but`.
- `He has made neat paths and has built a wooden bridge over a pool.`
  - focus: `neat paths`, `wooden bridge`, `pool`.
- `I like gardens too, but I do not like hard work.`
  - focus: the narrator's honest self-description.
- `Every year I enter for the garden competition too, and I always win a little prize for the worst garden in the town!`
  - focus: `always win`, `little prize`, `the worst garden` as the punchline.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. A charming town garden competition feeling: Joe's beautiful garden stands out
   with a balanced, inviting design.
2. Bill's larger garden: more flowers and vegetables, more effort and quantity,
   but visually busier and less elegant than Joe's.
3. Joe's design details: neat paths, a small wooden bridge, and a garden pool /
   pond, showing why it is more interesting.
4. The narrator's messy or neglected garden with a small comic prize, warm and
   self-mocking rather than cruel.

No text, letters, captions, readable signs, or visible English words in images.
Keep the palette fresh and lively. This lesson can be visually prettier and
lighter than Lesson 7.

## Tone

Light, playful, and observant. This should feel like a comparison game with a
small English-humour twist at the end.

The script should help the student compare fairly: not "Bill is bad" or "Joe is
lazy," but "different criteria give different results."
