## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel like a witty shopping dialogue, not a lesson that mocks
women, clothes, or body shape.

- Explain the old hat-shop scene early.
  - A `hat shop` is a specialized shop for hats.
  - In older British / European settings, hats were often part of formal dress.
  - Women could spend time choosing hats for social events, weddings, church,
    races, or formal occasions.
- Do not turn the lesson into a gender stereotype.
  - The line about men having many ties and women having many hats is old-style
    comic symmetry.
  - Modern framing: every person has things they think are worth buying, while
    someone else may not understand them.
  - Examples today: sneakers, game gear, watches, hair accessories, phone cases,
    figures, books, bags, sports equipment, or collectible cards.
- Treat the wife respectfully.
  - She is trying on a dramatic hat and defending her taste.
  - Do not make her vain, foolish, or ridiculous.
- Treat the husband as witty but careless.
  - He is tired of waiting and says something rude.
  - His own terrible tie makes his criticism less convincing.
- Explain the title clearly.
  - `Do you call that a hat?` is not a neutral question.
  - It means: `You think that thing is a hat? It does not look like a proper hat
    to me.`
  - It is sarcastic and rude.
- Explain `lighthouse`.
  - A lighthouse is a tall tower by the sea with a light that guides ships.
  - A hat that looks like a lighthouse is high, strange, and very noticeable.
- Keep visuals playful but not cruel.
  - The hat can be tall and lighthouse-like.
  - The wife should still look dignified and confident.
  - The husband's terrible tie can be funny, but not visually nasty.
- Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about what happens when a shopping comment is too direct.

The narrator and his wife are in a hat shop. His wife is looking at herself in
the mirror while trying on a hat. The narrator waits on a modern chair with
holes in it. They have already been in the shop for half an hour. He says, `Do
you call that a hat?` and she says he needn't be so rude. Later he suddenly
remarks that they mustn't buy things they do not need. He regrets it at once.
His wife reminds him of the terrible tie he bought yesterday. He defends his
tie by saying a man can never have too many ties. She answers with perfect
symmetry: a woman can't have too many hats. Ten minutes later, she walks out
wearing a hat that looks like a lighthouse.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`hat shop -> wife at mirror -> husband waits -> rude title line -> modern chair -> half an hour -> mustn't buy unnecessary things -> regret -> terrible tie -> too many ties / hats -> lighthouse hat`

The ending should land as:

`He criticized her hat, but she used his own terrible tie to defeat the argument.`

## One-Sentence Hook

A man laughs at his wife's strange hat, but she quickly reminds him that his own
taste is not perfect either.

## Story Focus

The shopping-comedy chain is:

1. The narrator says: `Do you call that a hat?`
2. His wife says he needn't be so rude.
3. She looks at herself in the mirror.
4. The narrator sits on a modern chair with holes in it and waits.
5. They have been in the hat shop for half an hour.
6. His wife is still in front of the mirror.
7. He remarks that they mustn't buy things they do not need.
8. He regrets saying it almost at once.
9. His wife says he needn't have said that.
10. She reminds him of the terrible tie he bought yesterday.
11. He says he finds the tie beautiful.
12. He says a man can never have too many ties.
13. She answers that a woman can't have too many hats.
14. Ten minutes later, they leave the shop together.
15. She is wearing a hat that looks like a lighthouse.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`criticizing someone else's taste` versus `having questionable taste yourself`.

## Western Hat Culture Bridge

This bridge should appear early in the script so the hat-shop scene does not
feel random to a modern child.

Explain:

- In older Western social life, hats were not just for sun or cold.
- Hats could be part of proper dress when people went out.
- Women often wore hats for formal events such as weddings, church, races,
  garden parties, and public ceremonies.
- Some formal hats are decorative and dramatic. They are fashion statements,
  not just practical objects.
- Daily hat-wearing is less common now, but formal hats are still visible in
  some weddings, horse-racing events, church settings, royal/public events, and
  stage or costume occasions.
- This is why a `hat shop` and a long decision in front of the mirror make sense
  in the story.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课里的 hat shop 不是随便买一顶棒球帽。过去在英国和欧洲，帽子常常是正式穿着的一部分。参加婚礼、教堂、赛马会、正式聚会时，帽子可以很讲究，甚至很夸张。所以妻子在镜子前看很久，并不是只为了实用，而是在挑一件能代表审美和场合的配饰。`

Connect this bridge to the ending:

`The hat looked like a lighthouse` means the hat is very tall and eye-catching,
like a decorative formal hat taken to comic extreme.

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old hat-shop scene to present-day experiences:

- waiting while someone tries on clothes, shoes, glasses, or accessories
- disagreeing about whether something looks good
- buying things one person loves and another person cannot understand
- making a rude comment and regretting it immediately
- being reminded that your own taste may also be questionable
- learning to say disagreement politely

Modern equivalents:

- `Do you call that a hat?` -> `You call that a phone case?`
- `that terrible tie` -> `that weird hoodie / game skin / pair of sneakers / collectible figure`
- `too many ties / too many hats` -> `too many shoes / too many books / too many keycaps / too many cards`

The durable pattern is:

`someone chooses an item -> another person criticizes it -> hidden double standard appears -> comic comeback`.

Do not over-moralize. The scene is funny because both sides have a point.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is negative modal verbs and past criticism:

- `needn't`
- `mustn't`
- `needn't have done`

Teach these through the argument, not as isolated grammar.

### `needn't`

Meaning:

`There is no need to do this.`

Story examples:

- `You needn't be so rude about it.`
- `I needn't remind you of that terrible tie you bought yesterday.`

Student-facing explanation:

`needn't` means `don't need to`. It often sounds like: `You really don't have
to do that.`

In this story:

`You needn't be so rude` means `There is no need to speak so impolitely.`

### `mustn't`

Meaning:

`You must not / should not do this.`

Story example:

- `We mustn't buy things we don't need.`

Student-facing explanation:

`mustn't` is stronger than `needn't`. It sounds like a rule or warning. The
husband is not only saying they don't need the hat. He is saying they should not
buy unnecessary things.

### `needn't have done`

Meaning:

`You did it, but it was unnecessary.`

Story example:

- `You needn't have said that.`

Student-facing explanation:

This is the sharpest little grammar point in the lesson. The husband already
said the sentence. He cannot take it back. His wife says: `You needn't have said
that` = `That sentence was unnecessary, and you should not have said it.`

Use a compact board:

| Sentence | Core meaning | Story feeling |
| --- | --- | --- |
| You needn't be so rude. | You don't need to be rude. | Please speak more politely. |
| We mustn't buy things we don't need. | We should not buy unnecessary things. | Rule / criticism. |
| You needn't have said that. | You said it, but it was unnecessary. | Too late; now I am annoyed. |

Secondary language points:

- `Do you call that a hat?`
  - sarcastic question, not a real question.
- `remarked suddenly`
  - said suddenly, often as a comment.
- `I regretted saying it almost at once`
  - I quickly wished I had not said it.
- `I find it beautiful`
  - I think it is beautiful.
- `can never have too many...`
  - humorous exaggeration: no number is too many.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `rude`
   - Meaning: not polite; speaking or behaving in a way that hurts or offends.
   - Bridge: the title question is rude because it mocks the hat directly.
   - Example: `You needn't be so rude about it.`
2. `remind`
   - Meaning: make someone remember something.
   - Bridge: the wife reminds the husband of his terrible tie, so his criticism
     becomes weaker.
   - Example: `I needn't remind you of that terrible tie you bought yesterday.`
3. `lighthouse`
   - Meaning: a tall tower near the sea with a light that guides ships.
   - Bridge: the hat is so tall and dramatic that it looks like a lighthouse.
   - Example: `My wife was wearing a hat that looked like a lighthouse.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `hat shop` = shop that sells hats
- `wife` = the narrator's spouse
- `mirror` = object used to see yourself
- `look at herself` = check how she looks
- `modern chair` = new-style chair; here it has holes in it
- `holes` = empty spaces in something
- `waited` = stayed there until someone was ready
- `half an hour` = thirty minutes
- `remark` = say as a comment
- `suddenly` = quickly and unexpectedly
- `regret` = wish you had not done something
- `terrible tie` = a very bad-looking tie, from the wife's point of view
- `find it beautiful` = think it is beautiful
- `too many` = more than needed
- `looked like` = had the appearance of

## Title And Sarcasm Mini-Bridge

Make the title a small language moment.

Explain:

- `Do you call that a hat?` is a sarcastic question.
- The husband is not asking for information.
- He is saying the hat is so strange that he does not think it deserves to be
  called a hat.

Possible Chinese explanation:

`你把那个叫帽子吗？` 的真实语气不是好奇，而是吐槽：`这也能叫帽子？`

Give a modern parallel:

- `Do you call that music?`
- `Do you call that a haircut?`
- `Do you call that a plan?`

Warn the student:

These sentences can sound funny in stories, but they are rude in real life if
said directly to someone.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into polite disagreement
about taste and shopping.

Recommended scenario:

`You are shopping with a friend or family member. They like something that you do not like. You must disagree politely instead of saying something rude.`

Student should practice:

- avoiding direct sarcasm
- saying a softer opinion
- using `needn't` for unnecessary behavior
- using `mustn't` for a rule or strong warning
- using `needn't have done` to talk about something already done but
  unnecessary
- recognizing that different people like different things

Useful expressions:

- `It is not really my style.`
- `I wouldn't choose it for myself.`
- `You needn't worry about my opinion.`
- `We mustn't spend more than our budget.`
- `I needn't have said it that way. Sorry.`
- `If you like it, you can try it on.`

Ethical/social point:

The real skill is not pretending to like everything. The real skill is
disagreeing without humiliating the other person.

- `I needn't remind you of that terrible tie...`

- `And a woman can't have too many hats.`

Avoid teaching the modals as disconnected grammar. The student should feel why
each sentence appears at that exact moment in the quarrel.
