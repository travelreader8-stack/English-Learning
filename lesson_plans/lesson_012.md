# Lesson 12 Plan - Goodbye and Good Luck

thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 12 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 12
- Title: `Goodbye and good luck`
- Chinese title: `再见，一路顺风`
- Core bridge: `这不是单纯看小船，而是一次送行：一个熟人从英国港口城市 Portsmouth 出发，驾驶有名的小船去参加横渡大西洋的重要比赛，邻居们去港口表达祝福和骄傲。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson needs a small amount of geography and sailing context before the
student can understand the send-off.

- `Portsmouth`:
  - Explain early that Portsmouth is a port city on the south coast of England
    in the United Kingdom.
  - Do not over-teach British geography or naval history. The student only needs
    to know that it is a city strongly connected with ships, harbours, and
    setting out to sea.
  - A simple bridge is enough: "a place where ships can leave for the sea."
- `harbour`:
  - Explain it as the place where boats stop, prepare, and set out.
  - Do not let the student imagine a random riverside or park lake.
- `Atlantic`:
  - Explain it as the Atlantic Ocean, the large ocean between Europe and the
    Americas.
  - Crossing the Atlantic in a small boat is a serious challenge, not a casual
    sightseeing trip.
- `Topsail`:
  - Treat it as the boat's name.
  - Make clear that the boat has experience: it has crossed the Atlantic many
    times.
- Tone:
  - This is a send-off and blessing story. Keep it supportive and proud, not
    anxious or tragic.
- Student-facing display:
  - Prefer `查尔斯·艾利森船长` rather than raw punctuation like
    `查尔斯.艾利森`.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about saying goodbye before an important challenge.

Captain Charles Alison is the narrator's neighbour. Tomorrow he will sail from
Portsmouth. The narrator and others will meet him early in the morning at the
harbour. Captain Alison will be in his small boat, Topsail. Topsail is not an
ordinary little boat: it is famous and has sailed across the Atlantic many
times.

Captain Alison will set out at eight o'clock, so the neighbours have time to see
the boat and say goodbye. He will be away for two months. They are proud of him
because he will take part in an important race across the Atlantic.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`send-off = going to the starting place, seeing the person before departure, saying goodbye, wishing good luck, and feeling proud of the challenge.`

## One-Sentence Hook

Tomorrow morning, a neighbour will leave an English harbour in a small famous
boat to race across the Atlantic, and everyone goes to say goodbye and good
luck.

## Story Focus

The send-off chain is:

1. Captain Charles Alison is the narrator's neighbour.
2. He will sail from Portsmouth tomorrow.
3. People will meet him at the harbour early in the morning.
4. He will be in his small boat, Topsail.
5. Topsail is famous and has crossed the Atlantic many times.
6. Captain Alison will set out at eight o'clock.
7. There is plenty of time to see the boat and say goodbye.
8. He will be away for two months.
9. The neighbours are proud of him.
10. He will take part in an important race across the Atlantic.

The ending should land as: "This is not an ordinary trip. It is a difficult
challenge, and the people around him send him off with pride."

## Geography And Culture Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- Portsmouth is in England, in the United Kingdom.
- It is a port city on the south coast, so ships and boats are part of its
  natural background.
- A harbour is where boats stay before they leave and where people can come to
  see them off.
- The Atlantic is a huge ocean. Crossing it in a small boat is a major journey.
- The family/neighbours are not merely "looking at a boat"; they are attending a
  send-off before an important race.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`Portsmouth 是英国南部的港口城市。你不需要记住它的全部历史，只要知道：这是一个和船、海港、出海很有关系的地方。所以 Captain Alison 从这里启航去横渡大西洋，场景是合理的。`

## Modern Bridge

Do not center the modern bridge only on sailing. Most students will not have
that life experience. Connect the emotion and structure:

- going to the airport to send off a relative for an international competition
- seeing an older sibling leave for a summer camp, training program, or school
  trip
- saying goodbye at a train station before someone goes to a far-away contest
- classmates cheering for someone who will represent the school in an important
  event
- checking someone's equipment before a long challenge
- feeling proud because someone has prepared for a difficult task

The transferable idea is:

`important departure -> starting place -> equipment/vehicle -> goodbye -> good luck -> pride -> long challenge`

## Vocabulary Strategy

Use exactly 3 formal vocab blocks. Support the geography and race vocabulary in
retell, discussion, read-aloud, and living-scene practice.

### Formal Vocab Blocks

Prefer:

1. `sail`
   - To travel by boat or control a boat on water.
2. `harbour`
   - A safe place where boats stay, prepare, arrive, and leave.
3. `proud`
   - Feeling deep happiness and respect because someone is doing something
     difficult or meaningful.

### Required Supporting Vocabulary

Explain these in context:

- `Captain`
  - Here it means the person in charge of a boat, not mainly a military rank.
- `Portsmouth`
  - A port city in England, in the United Kingdom.
- `Topsail`
  - The boat's name.
- `Atlantic`
  - The Atlantic Ocean, a large ocean between Europe and the Americas.
- `set out`
  - Start a journey or task.
- `plenty of time`
  - More than enough time.
- `say goodbye`
  - Say farewell before someone leaves.
- `be away for two months`
  - Not be here for two months.
- `take part in`
  - Participate in a race or activity.
- `important race`
  - A serious event, not a casual boat ride.
- `good luck`
  - A phrase used to wish someone success before a challenge.

### Word Network

The script should build a word network:

1. **Sea and departure**
   `Portsmouth / harbour / sail / set out / boat`
2. **Challenge**
   `Atlantic / race / take part in / be away for two months`
3. **Support**
   `say goodbye / good luck / proud of him / plenty of time`

## Language Focus

Use these language points:

- `will sail from Portsmouth tomorrow`
  - A planned future departure.
- `We'll meet him at the harbour early in the morning`
  - The send-off plan.
- `He will be in his small boat, Topsail`
  - Where he will be and what boat he will use.
- `It has sailed across the Atlantic many times`
  - The boat's history and credibility.
- `will set out at eight o'clock`
  - The exact start time.
- `we'll have plenty of time`
  - There is enough time before departure.
- `He will be away for two months`
  - Future absence.
- `take part in an important race`
  - The purpose of the journey.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: future plan plus proven experience.

Do not teach `will` as a dry future-tense table. Teach it as a morning send-off
schedule:

- Tomorrow:
  - `Captain Alison will sail from Portsmouth tomorrow.`
- Early morning:
  - `We'll meet him at the harbour early in the morning.`
- Eight o'clock:
  - `Captain Alison will set out at eight o'clock.`
- Before departure:
  - `We'll see his boat and then we'll say goodbye to him.`
- The next two months:
  - `He will be away for two months.`
- The big purpose:
  - `He will take part in an important race across the Atlantic.`

Contrast with the boat's past experience:

- `Topsail has sailed across the Atlantic many times.`
  - The boat has a record. This makes the upcoming challenge feel credible.

Student-friendly explanation:

`This lesson uses will to build tomorrow's plan, and has sailed to show the boat's experience before tomorrow's race.`

Use 8 guided sentences. The goal is to make send-off, future plan, and sea-race
language easy to say before the student moves into modern departure examples.

- `Our neighbour, Captain Charles Alison, will sail from Portsmouth tomorrow.`
  - focus: `Captain`, `will sail`, `Portsmouth`.
- `We'll meet him at the harbour early in the morning.`
  - focus: `We'll meet him`, `harbour`, `early in the morning`.
- `He will be in his small boat, Topsail.`
  - focus: `small boat` and the boat name `Topsail`.
- `Topsail is a famous little boat. It has sailed across the Atlantic many times.`
  - focus: `famous little boat`, `has sailed across the Atlantic`.
- `Captain Alison will set out at eight o'clock, so we'll have plenty of time.`
  - focus: `set out`, `eight o'clock`, `plenty of time`.
- `We'll see his boat and then we'll say goodbye to him.`
  - focus: `we'll see`, `then`, `say goodbye`.
- `He will be away for two months. We are very proud of him.`
  - focus: `away for two months`, `proud of him`.
- `He will take part in an important race across the Atlantic.`
  - focus: `take part in`, `important race`, `across the Atlantic`.

## Living-Scene Exercise

Use `mode: "all_fills"` for Lesson 12, consistent with Lessons 1-11.

The exercise should transfer the story into a modern send-off / important
challenge scenario. The goal is to practice future-plan language and support
phrases, not sailing trivia.

Suggested word bank:

- `will set out`
- `harbour`
- `say goodbye`
- `good luck`
- `proud of`
- `take part in`
- `important race`
- `plenty of time`
- `be away for`
- `sail`

Suggested three fill scenes:

- Airport send-off:
  `My cousin ___ early tomorrow, so we will go to the airport to ___ and say ___.`
  Expected: `will set out`, `say goodbye`, `good luck`
- School competition:
  `My friend will ___ an ___ next month. Our class is very ___ him.`
  Expected: `take part in`, `important race`, `proud of`
- Long trip:
  `Dad will ___ three weeks, but we have ___ to see him before he leaves.`
  Expected: `be away for`, `plenty of time`

Production note: if the final scenario is not about boats, do not force
`harbour` or `sail` into every sentence. Keep them in the word bank or one
boat-related mini-scene if useful, but center the exercise on send-off,
departure, support, and challenge.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. Portsmouth as an English harbour city: a coastal harbour, boats, early
   morning light, and a small group preparing to send off Captain Alison. No
   readable signs or text.
2. Captain Alison and Topsail: a capable captain beside a small famous-looking
   sailing boat, presented as experienced rather than flashy.
3. The send-off before eight o'clock: neighbours/friends see the boat and say
   goodbye with good-luck energy. The scene should feel supportive, not sad.
4. The big challenge: Topsail heading out toward open water, with a visual sense
   that the Atlantic race is long and important.

No text, letters, captions, readable signs, readable boat names, or visible
English words in images. Avoid making the boat look like a luxury yacht or
cruise ship. It should read as a small but serious sailing boat.

## Tone

Supportive, proud, and outward-looking. This should feel like a send-off before
an important challenge.

The script should not become a geography lecture or sailing manual. Geography
supports comprehension; the emotional center is goodbye, good luck, and pride.
