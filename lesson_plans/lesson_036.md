## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel inspiring and carefully planned, not reckless or
overheated.

- Explain `the English Channel` early.
  - It is the sea channel between France and England.
  - It is not a TV channel, video channel, or app channel.
  - The route is from `the French coast` to `the English coast`.
- Use a simple map.
  - France on one side, England on the other, the English Channel between them.
  - Add an arrow: `France -> English Channel -> England`.
- Do not encourage children to copy extreme swimming challenges.
  - Debbie has trained for years.
  - Her father will go with her in a small boat.
  - Long-distance open-water swimming needs professional training, adults,
    safety support, weather judgment, and planning.
- Do not make Debbie look like a fantasy superhero.
  - She is strong and prepared, but still a child doing a difficult challenge
    with adult support.
- Keep the father's anxiety human.
  - `anxiously` means he supports her and worries about her safety at the same
    time.
- Treat the mother as the family-story reveal.
  - The ending is not a joke; it gives the challenge a warm sense of family
    tradition.
- Explain `record`.
  - It means world record here, not recording audio or video.
- Explain `train`.
  - It means to train / coach / practise here, not a railway train.
- Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production unless
  the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a young swimmer preparing to swim across the English
Channel with training, support, and a clear plan.

Debbie Hart is going to swim across the English Channel tomorrow. She will set
out from the French coast at five o'clock in the morning. She is only eleven
years old, but she hopes to set up a new world record. She is a strong swimmer,
and many people feel that she is sure to succeed. Her father will set out with
her in a small boat because he has trained her for years. Tomorrow he will be
watching anxiously as she swims the long distance to England. Debbie intends to
take short rests every two hours. She will drink, but she will not eat solid
food. Her school friends will be waiting for her on the English coast. Among
them will be her mother, who swam the Channel herself when she was a girl.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`English Channel -> French coast -> 5 a.m. -> eleven years old -> world record -> strong swimmer -> father in small boat -> trained for years -> rests every two hours -> drink / no solid food -> friends on English coast -> mother once swam the Channel`

The ending should land as: "This is a big challenge, but it is not a sudden
adventure. It is a trained, supported, family-linked plan."

## Geography And Safety Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- The English Channel is the sea between southern England and northern France.
- `across the Channel` means from one side to the other side.
- `the French coast` is the coast of France.
- `the English coast` is the coast of England.
- This is open-water swimming, not swimming in a pool.
- Open water has distance, cold water, waves, weather, boats, and safety risks.
- Debbie's father is in a small boat because she needs adult support and
  observation.
- The story is about trained challenge, not independent adventure.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`Channel 在这里不是电视台或视频频道，而是英吉利海峡。Debbie 要从法国海岸出发，横渡海峡，到英国海岸。这个目标听起来很燃，但课文里最重要的不是“她胆子大”，而是她训练了很多年，父亲坐小船陪着她，同学和妈妈在岸边等她。`

Modern safety bridge:

`今天如果进行这样的长距离公开水域运动，需要专业训练、安全船、成年人负责、天气判断和完整计划。我们可以欣赏 Debbie 的目标感，但不能把它当成普通孩子可以自己模仿的游泳挑战。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old record-attempt story to present-day experiences:

- sports training plans and coaches
- long-term practice before a public challenge
- safety support for outdoor sports
- parents supporting and worrying at the same time
- friends waiting at the finish line
- world records, personal records, and school sports goals
- GPS tracking, support boats, weather apps, and safety teams in modern
  endurance sports
- family examples: a parent once did something difficult and a child follows in
  the same spirit

The durable pattern is:

`big goal -> years of training -> support team -> clear plan -> people waiting -> family tradition`

Do not turn this into generic motivational content. Keep it grounded in route,
training, support, and safety.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is future plans, future support, and future-in-progress.

Teach these through the story:

- `is going to swim`
  - Debbie has a clear plan for tomorrow.
- `is going to set out`
  - The start time and place are arranged.
- `will set out with her`
  - Her father will accompany her.
- `will be watching her anxiously`
  - During the swim, he will be watching continuously.
- `intends to take short rests`
  - Debbie has a plan for how to manage the swim.
- `will be waiting`
  - Her friends will be waiting at the finish side.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson is full of future language because it is a planned challenge. Some
sentences tell us what Debbie is going to do. Some tell us what her father and
friends will do. `Will be watching` and `will be waiting` help us imagine the
action continuing during the swim.

Use a compact board:

| Future expression | Story meaning |
| --- | --- |
| is going to swim | Debbie's planned challenge |
| is going to set out | planned start |
| will set out with her | father's support |
| will be watching | father's continuous watching |
| intends to take rests | Debbie's plan |
| will be waiting | friends at the finish side |

Secondary language points:

- `set up a new world record`
  - create / establish a new record.
- `sure to succeed`
  - very likely to succeed.
- `has trained his daughter for years`
  - training started years ago and matters now.
- `every two hours`
  - once each two-hour period.
- `solid food`
  - food that is not liquid.
- `Among them will be...`
  - a formal inverted sentence meaning her mother will be among the waiting
    people.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `record`
   - Meaning in this lesson: the best officially known performance.
   - Bridge: world record, school record, personal record; not audio recording.
   - Example: `She hopes to set up a new world record.`
2. `train`
   - Meaning in this lesson: to practise and prepare someone for a sport or
     skill.
   - Bridge: her father has trained her for years; not a railway train.
   - Example: `Mr. Hart has trained his daughter for years.`
3. `anxiously`
   - Meaning: in a worried or nervous way.
   - Bridge: Debbie's father supports her but worries while watching.
   - Example: `He will be watching her anxiously.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `across` = from one side to the other
- `Channel` = the English Channel, the sea between England and France
- `set out` = start a journey
- `French coast` = coast of France
- `English coast` = coast of England
- `strong swimmer` = someone who swims very well
- `succeed` = do what one tries to do
- `small boat` = support boat beside Debbie
- `long distance` = very far distance
- `intend` = plan to do something
- `short rest` = short break
- `every two hours` = once after each two hours
- `something to drink` = drink / liquid
- `solid food` = food that is not liquid
- `school friends` = classmates / friends from school
- `among them` = in that group
- `herself` = by her own action; her mother also did it

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into planning a difficult
but safe goal.

Recommended scenario:

`You are preparing for a school sports challenge, performance, exam, or long project. You explain your plan, your training, who will support you, and how you will stay safe.`

Student should practice:

- saying what they are going to do
- saying when and where they will start
- explaining training or preparation
- naming support people
- using `will be watching / waiting`
- using `intend to` for a plan
- describing safety boundaries

Useful expressions:

- `I am going to... tomorrow.`
- `I am going to set out / start at...`
- `I have trained / practised for...`
- `My father / mother / teacher will help me.`
- `They will be watching me.`
- `My friends will be waiting for me.`
- `I intend to take short rests.`
- `I will stay safe and follow the plan.`

Keep the scene grounded: a sports day, swim meet, piano performance, long bike
ride with adults, exam plan, or school project.
