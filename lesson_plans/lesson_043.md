## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel adventurous and historically grounded, not like a modern
commercial flight.

- Explain the polar geography first.
  - `North Pole` is the northern end of the earth's axis.
  - `South Pole` is the southern end of the earth's axis, in Antarctica.
  - The South Pole is not a city or country. It is a geographic point.
  - Use a simple globe/map image: top = North Pole, bottom = South Pole.
- Explain why 1929 matters.
  - In 1929, long-distance polar flying was still dangerous and experimental.
  - Navigation, engine reliability, weather information, and rescue options were
    far weaker than today.
  - This is exploration by aircraft, not normal airline travel.
- Handle R.E. Byrd carefully.
  - The lesson text says Byrd flew over the North Pole three years earlier and
    then over the South Pole in 1929.
  - Do not expand into the later controversy about the 1926 North Pole claim
    unless the user explicitly asks. It is not the lesson's teaching focus.
  - Focus on the 1929 South Pole flight and the danger in the text.
- Explain the flight danger clearly.
  - The plane needs to rise to 10,000 feet to get over mountains.
  - It is too heavy to climb safely.
  - Throwing out two heavy food sacks reduces weight.
  - After that, the plane rises and clears the mountains by 400 feet.
- Explain measurements visually.
  - `10,000 feet` is about 3,000 meters.
  - `400 feet` is about 120 meters.
  - `300 miles` is about 480 kilometers.
  - Do not overdo unit conversion; use it only to help scale.
- Explain `photographs` and modern `photos`.
  - `photograph` is the fuller / more formal word.
  - `photo` is the modern everyday short form.
- Keep visuals thrilling but safe for a child.
  - No crash scene, fire, death, or panic.
  - Show danger through altitude, mountains, heavy plane, and tense faces.
- Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a high-risk early aviation expedition over Antarctica.

In 1929, three years after his flight over the North Pole, the American explorer
R.E. Byrd flew over the South Pole for the first time. At first, Byrd and his
men could take many photographs of the mountains below. Soon, however, they ran
into serious trouble. Their plane seemed likely to crash because it could only
get over the mountains if it rose to 10,000 feet. Byrd ordered his men to throw
out two heavy food sacks. The lighter plane was able to rise and cleared the
mountains by 400 feet. Byrd then knew they could reach the South Pole 300 miles
away, because no more mountains were in sight. The aircraft flew over endless
white plains without difficulty.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`1929 -> Byrd -> North Pole background -> South Pole flight -> photographs of mountains -> serious trouble -> plane too low/heavy -> throw out food sacks -> plane rises -> clears mountains by 400 feet -> endless white plains`

The ending should land as:

`A dangerous mountain crossing turns into a calm flight over the endless white
Antarctic plains.`

## One-Sentence Hook

Byrd's plane nearly crashed in Antarctica, but throwing out two heavy food sacks
helped it rise just high enough to clear the mountains.

## Polar Geography Bridge

This bridge should appear before the historical story begins.

Explain:

- Earth has two poles.
- The `North Pole` is at the top of the globe.
- The `South Pole` is at the bottom of the globe, in Antarctica.
- Antarctica is a continent covered by ice.
- The South Pole is a point on the continent, not a town or airport.
- Flying over it in 1929 meant crossing remote ice, mountains, and extreme
  conditions.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`South Pole 不是一个城市，也不是一个国家，而是地球最南端的点，在南极洲。今天我们听到飞机会觉得很普通，但 1929 年的飞机还很早期，飞到南极上空更像探险，不像旅行。`

Suggested visual:

`globe -> North Pole -> South Pole -> Antarctica -> mountains -> white plains`

Keep this clear and short. The goal is to give the student a map and a sense of
risk.

## Early Aviation Exploration Bridge

This lesson needs a modern-life contrast.

Explain:

- Today, commercial flights feel routine because planes, weather systems,
  navigation, radio, satellites, and rescue systems are advanced.
- In 1929, polar flight was much more uncertain.
- The crew could not assume that help was nearby.
- A mountain crossing was dangerous because the aircraft might not climb high
  enough.
- Taking photographs from the air was also part of exploration and mapping, not
  just casual sightseeing.

Suggested teacher line:

`这课里的 flight 不是“坐飞机去旅游”。它更接近“把飞机当作探险工具”。他们拍山脉的 photographs，是在记录一个当时很难到达、很难观察的地方。`

Add caution:

Do not make this a long biography of Byrd. Keep the focus on the flight problem
inside the text.

## Photograph / Photo Modern English Bridge

This is a useful small language expansion for this lesson.

Teach:

- `photograph` = full word, more formal / written / old-fashioned.
- `photo` = short form, more common in everyday modern English.
- `take a photograph` sounds like formal recording.
- `take a photo` sounds like normal daily speech.

Connect to the story:

`take a great many photographs` fits the explorer-report style of the text. The

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old exploration scene to present-day experiences:

- taking a plane today versus early aviation exploration
- using drones / satellites today versus aerial photography in 1929
- understanding why weight matters for flying machines
- making a fast decision under pressure
- using maps and measurements to understand risk
- recognizing how humans explored places before modern technology made them
  easier to observe

The durable pattern is:

`beautiful exploration -> sudden danger -> practical decision -> narrow escape -> open path ahead`.

Do not turn this into a full physics or history lecture. Use just enough science
and history to make the story exciting and understandable.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar / structure is ability and conditional possibility:

- `be able to`
- `could only ... if ...`
- `would be able to`
- `was able to`

Teach these through the danger sequence.

### `be able to`

Examples:

- `Byrd and his men were able to take a great many photographs`
- `The plane was then able to rise`
- `The aircraft was able to fly over the endless white plains`

Student-facing explanation:

`be able to` means `can`, but it often sounds more formal and is useful when we
talk about what someone managed to do in a situation.

In this lesson, `be able to` changes with the danger:

- At first, they were able to take photos.
- Then the plane was not high enough.
- After throwing out food sacks, the plane was able to rise.
- Finally, it was able to fly without difficulty.

### `could only ... if ...`

Key sentence:

`It could only get over the mountains if it rose to 10,000 feet.`

Meaning:

`Only if the plane rose to 10,000 feet could it get over the mountains.`

Student-facing explanation:

`could only ... if ...` gives a hard condition. It is not a suggestion. It is
the one thing that must happen.

Useful parallels:

- `You can only enter if you have a ticket.`
- `The drone can only fly higher if it has enough power.`
- `We could only finish if everyone helped.`

### `clear`

Explain:

`clear the mountains` does not mean clean the mountains.

It means:

`pass over the mountains without hitting them`.

`cleared the mountains by 400 feet` means the plane passed over the mountains
with about 400 feet of extra height.

### `lay below`

Explain briefly:

`the mountains that lay below` = the mountains were below the aircraft.

Do not confuse `lay` here with "put something down." In this sentence, it means
`were located`.

Secondary language points:

- `run into serious trouble`
  - suddenly meet a serious problem.
- `At one point`
  - at one moment in the story.
- `seemed certain`
  - looked almost sure.
- `at once`
  - immediately.
- `throw out`
  - throw away from the plane.
- `in sight`
  - visible.
- `without difficulty`
  - easily / with no problem.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `explorer`
   - Meaning: a person who travels to difficult or unknown places to learn about
     them.
   - Bridge: Byrd is not just a passenger; he uses aircraft for polar
     exploration.
   - Example: `The American explorer, R.E. Byrd, flew over the South Pole.`
2. `crash`
   - Meaning: to hit something violently, especially a vehicle or aircraft.
   - Bridge: the plane nearly crashes because it cannot rise high enough over
     the mountains.
   - Example: `It seemed certain that their plane would crash.`
3. `clear`
   - Meaning in this lesson: pass over an obstacle without hitting it.
   - Bridge: not "clean"; the plane cleared the mountains by 400 feet.
   - Example: `It cleared the mountains by 400 feet.`

## Measurement And Flight Physics Mini-Bridge

This section should make the emergency intuitive.

Explain:

- The plane is approaching mountains.
- It needs to climb high enough to get over them.
- A heavy plane has more difficulty climbing.
- Food sacks are heavy, so throwing them out reduces weight.
- Once the plane is lighter, it can rise.
- It clears the mountains by 400 feet, which is not a huge margin in a dangerous
  mountain crossing.

Suggested student-friendly line:

`飞机不是想飞多高就立刻飞多高。越重，爬升越困难。伯德扔掉两个沉重的 food sacks，不是浪费食物，而是在紧急时刻减轻重量，让飞机能爬过山。`

Suggested visual:

`heavy plane + mountains -> throw out sacks -> lighter plane rises -> 400 feet clearance`

Do not make this into equations. A simple weight-and-climb intuition is enough.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into solving a problem by
understanding a hard condition.

Recommended scenario:

`You are leading a team project, a drone flight, a hiking plan, or a game mission. You can only succeed if one condition is met, so you must make a practical decision.`

Student should practice:

- describing a goal
- using `be able to`
- using `could only ... if ...`
- explaining a problem
- making a quick decision
- using `clear` for passing an obstacle
- using `photo` versus `photograph` appropriately

Useful expressions:

- `We were able to... at first.`
- `Then we ran into serious trouble.`
- `We could only finish if...`
- `At once, we decided to...`
- `Then we were able to...`
- `We cleared the obstacle by...`

The exercise should make the polar flight pattern useful beyond geography.
