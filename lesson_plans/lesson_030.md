## Production Warnings

This lesson should be light, comic, and spatially clear.

- Explain the title joke early.
  - The children are playing with a football on the river bank.
  - The ball flies toward a boat on the river.
  - It is not real water polo; the title is joking because the football reaches
    the water/boat scene.
- Explain `bank` carefully.
  - Here `bank` means river bank / the land beside a river.
  - It does not mean a money bank.
  - This should be one of the first vocabulary clarifications.
- Explain the spatial layout with a simple diagram or narration.
  - river
  - bank
  - children on the bank
  - people rowing on the river
  - a passing boat
  - the ball going from bank to boat
- Treat `The Wayle` as a local river name.
  - The child does not need to know whether it is a famous real river.
  - It is enough to remember: the Wayle is the small river near the narrator's
    home.
- Explain `rowing`.
  - Rowing means moving a small boat with oars.
  - It is not a motorboat or a big ship.
- Keep the accident gentle.
  - The man is struck by the ball and nearly falls into the water.
  - He laughs and throws the ball back.
  - Do not make it look like a serious injury or danger scene.
- Do not make the children look malicious.
  - They are careless and scared, then run away.
  - The tone is mischievous, not cruel.
- Keep the final emotional note warm.
  - The man laughs when he realizes what happened.
  - The story ends with the ball returned to the bank.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a quiet riverside afternoon interrupted by a flying ball.

The Wayle is a small river that cuts across the park near the narrator's home.
The narrator likes sitting by the Wayle on fine afternoons. Last Sunday was
warm, so he sat on the river bank as usual. Children were playing games on the
bank, and people were rowing on the river. Suddenly, one child kicked a ball
very hard. It went toward a passing boat. People on the bank called out to warn
the man in the boat, but he did not hear them. The ball struck him so hard that
he nearly fell into the water. When the narrator turned to look at the children,
they were not in sight; they had all run away. The man laughed and threw the
ball back to the bank.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`river park -> narrator sitting on bank -> children playing -> rowing boat -> ball flies -> warning fails -> man nearly falls -> children disappear -> ball returned`

The ending should land as: "The man could have been angry, but he laughed and
threw the ball back."

## Culture And Scene Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A river can run through a park.
- The river bank is the land beside the river.
- On a fine afternoon, people may sit by the river, play games on the grass, or
  row small boats.
- Rowing is a calm small-boat activity using oars.
- A ball game near water can affect other people if the ball flies away.
- The title `Football or polo?` is a playful question, not a sports lesson.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课的场景其实很容易想象：一个公园，一条小河，岸上有人坐着、孩子玩球，河上有人划船。要先把空间摆清楚：bank 不是银行，而是河岸；children 在岸上，boat 在河上，ball 从岸上飞向船。标题 Football or polo? 是个玩笑，因为足球突然飞到了水边和船上，好像变成了水上运动。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the riverside scene to present-day experiences:

- playing football or frisbee near a path, lake, river, or road
- a ball flying into another person's picnic area
- children playing near people who are walking, cycling, or boating
- a playground next to a river or lake
- someone accidentally disturbing others during outdoor play
- apologizing or calling out when a ball goes toward someone
- checking surroundings before kicking or throwing hard

The durable pattern is:

`play space -> shared public space -> sudden accident -> responsibility -> kind response`

Do not make the lesson a heavy safety lecture. Keep it as a light public-space
story with one clear habit: look around before you kick hard.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is result and earlier past inside a past story.

Teach it through the story:

- `so hard that...`
  - `The ball struck him so hard that he nearly fell into the water.`
  - Meaning: the hit was very strong; the result was that he almost fell.
  - This is the main sentence pattern.
- `had all run away`
  - When the narrator turned to look, the children were already gone.
  - They ran away before he looked.
  - Past perfect explains the earlier action.

Student-facing explanation:

`so... that...` connects strength and result. `had run away` shows that the
children's running happened before the narrator looked back.

Use a compact board:

| Sentence | Function |
| --- | --- |
| so hard that he nearly fell | cause and result |
| there weren't any in sight | what the narrator saw |
| they had all run away | what had happened before he looked |

Secondary language points:

- `as usual`
  - as he often did.
- `towards`
  - moving in the direction of something.
- `called out`
  - shouted to warn or get attention.
- `in sight`
  - able to be seen.
- `realized what had happened`
  - understood the event after it happened.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `bank`
   - Meaning in this lesson: the land beside a river.
   - Bridge: river bank, lake bank; not a money bank.
   - Example: `I went and sat on the river bank as usual.`
2. `row`
   - Meaning: to move a boat with oars.
   - Bridge: rowing a small boat in a park lake or river.
   - Example: `There were some people rowing on the river.`
3. `sight`
   - Meaning: the area you can see; being visible.
   - Bridge: `in sight` = can be seen; `out of sight` = cannot be seen.
   - Example: `There weren't any in sight.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `polo` = a ball game; here part of the title joke, not the main sport lesson
- `Wayle` = name of the small river near the narrator's home
- `cut across` = go across / pass through
- `park` = public outdoor space
- `fine afternoons` = pleasant, clear afternoons
- `as usual` = as always / as he usually did
- `games` = play activities
- `kick` = hit with the foot
- `towards` = in the direction of
- `passing boat` = a boat moving past
- `call out` = shout
- `struck` = hit; past tense of strike
- `nearly` = almost
- `fall into` = drop into
- `run away` = leave quickly because of fear or guilt
- `throw back` = return by throwing

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the story into a shared public-space
scene.

Recommended scenario:

`You are playing football or throwing a ball in a park. Other people are nearby. The ball nearly hits someone. You need to call out, apologize, and get the ball back politely.`

Student should practice:

- warning someone
- apologizing after an accident
- explaining what happened
- saying the ball went toward someone or something
- asking for the ball back politely
- understanding that public space is shared

Useful expressions:

- `Watch out!`
- `I'm sorry.`
- `The ball went towards you.`
- `Are you all right?`
- `Could you throw the ball back, please?`
- `We should play farther from the river.`

The exercise should not shame the child. It should teach responsibility and a
polite repair after an accident.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to the action sequence.

Board structure:

`so... that...`

- Shows a strong cause and a result.
- In the story:
  - `so hard that he nearly fell into the water`

`had done`

- Shows an action that happened before another past moment.
- In the story:
  - narrator looked
  - children had already run away

`space words`

- `by the Wayle`
- `on the bank`
- `on the river`
- `towards a passing boat`
- `into the water`
- `back to the bank`

Use the final logic:

`The ball moved from land to boat.`

`The children moved out of sight.`

`The ball came back to land.`

Avoid a broad sports vocabulary lesson. The story's spatial movement is the
grammar and memory anchor.
