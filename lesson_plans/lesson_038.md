## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel lightly ironic and life-wise, not like a complaint
about England.

- Do not frame England as simply "bad weather."
  - The problem is Harrison's huge contrast after many years in the
    Mediterranean.
  - The lesson should say: England's weather was not what his retirement dream
    had imagined.
- Explain the Mediterranean as a region and a climate, not a single country.
  - Mediterranean climate generally suggests more sunshine, warmer weather, and
    outdoor living.
  - Do not turn this into a geography lecture; make it a lifestyle contrast.
- Make the climate-to-lifestyle link visible.
  - Mediterranean sunshine can mean walking outside, sitting in cafes, living
    near the sea, and planning days around good weather.
  - Continual rain and bitter cold change daily routines, mood, clothing,
    transport, and outdoor plans.
- Treat the three adverbs as the lesson's language-feel center.

  - `bitterly`: the cold has a bodily, unpleasant bite.
- Do not make Harrison merely silly.

    daily weather experience.
- Teach `except` through the title's punchline.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a man whose long retirement dream collapses because he
planned the place but forgot the climate.

Harrison had lived in the Mediterranean for many years before he returned to
England. He had often dreamed of retiring in England and planned to settle down
in the country. As soon as he returned, he bought a house and went to live
there. Almost immediately, he began to complain about the weather. Although it
was still summer, it rained continually and was often bitterly cold. After many
years of sunshine, Harrison got a shock. He acted as if he had never lived in
England before. In the end, the weather was more than he could bear. He had
hardly had time to settle down when he sold the house and left the country. The
dream he had had for many years ended there. He had thought of everything except
the weather.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`Mediterranean sunshine -> retirement dream -> England countryside -> buys house quickly -> complains immediately -> rain continually -> bitterly cold -> shock -> cannot bear it -> sells house -> leaves -> everything except weather`

The ending should land as: "He planned the dream picture, but forgot the daily
feeling of living there."

## One-Sentence Hook

Harrison planned his retirement, bought the house, and moved to the country, but
he forgot the one thing he would meet every day: the weather.

## Climate And Lifestyle Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- The Mediterranean is a region around the Mediterranean Sea, not one country.
- Mediterranean climate often means more sunshine, warmer days, outdoor meals,
  walking, sea views, and a lifestyle that can happen outside more easily.
- England can have cool, wet, changeable weather; rain and damp cold can shape
  how people plan their day.
- Harrison has lived for years in sunshine, so his body and habits have changed.
- Returning to England is not just moving to a place; it is returning to a very
  different daily climate.
- He imagined an English country retirement, but he forgot the feeling of living
  with continual rain and bitter cold.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`地中海不是一个国家，而是一片靠近地中海的地区。课文里的 Mediterranean 让人想到阳光、温暖、海边、户外散步、露天咖啡。Harrison 在这样的 sunshine 里住了很多年，再回到英国乡村时，遇到的不是梦里的安静生活，而是 continually raining 和 bitterly cold。问题不是英国不好，而是他的身体和生活习惯已经被另一种气候改变了。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect Harrison's mistake to present-day life:

- moving because a place looks beautiful in videos
- wanting to live by the sea, in the mountains, in another country, or in the
  countryside
- forgetting daily details: weather, transport, food, friends, school, medical
  access, internet, and routine
- liking a place as a tourist but not enjoying it as a resident
- climate affecting clothes, mood, outdoor exercise, commuting, and plans
- the difference between a dream picture and everyday life

The durable pattern is:

`dream location -> quick decision -> daily reality -> forgotten factor -> dream changes`

Do not turn this into "never move" advice. Keep it as: good planning includes
boring but daily details.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is past perfect for a long dream before a quick collapse, plus
paired "too fast" structures.

Teach these through the story:

- `had lived`
  - Harrison lived in the Mediterranean before he returned to England.
- `had often dreamed`
  - The dream existed for years before the move.
- `had planned`
  - The plan was already formed before he bought the house.
- `had no sooner returned than he bought a house`
  - He bought the house very soon after returning.
- `had hardly had time to settle down when he sold the house`
  - He sold it before he really had time to settle in.
- `the dream he had had`
  - A long-held dream from before the final disappointment.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson uses past perfect because Harrison's dream existed before the main
story action. He had lived in sunshine, had dreamed of retiring, and had planned
to settle down. Then reality arrived very quickly: he had no sooner returned
than he bought a house, and he had hardly settled down when he sold it.

Use a compact board:

| Structure | Story feeling |
| --- | --- |
| had lived / had dreamed / had planned | the dream had a long history |
| no sooner... than... | he bought the house very quickly |
| almost immediately | reality hit almost at once |
| hardly... when... | he sold it before settling in |
| had thought of everything except... | the final ironic gap |

Secondary language points:

- `retire`
  - stop working after a long career.
- `settle down`
  - start living steadily in one place.
- `complain about`
  - say that something is wrong or unpleasant.
- `got a shock`
  - was very surprised in an unpleasant way.
- `as if`
  - as though; like something were true.
- `more than he could bear`
  - too much for him to accept or endure.

## Teaching Adverbs As Language Feel

The three key adverbs should be taught as the emotional engine of the lesson.

### `immediately`

Core sentence:

`Almost immediately he began to complain about the weather.`

Language feel:

`几乎是一住进去，现实就开始打脸。`

Explain:

- `immediately` is not only "soon."
- It shows the gap between dream and reality was exposed very fast.
- It also echoes `no sooner... than...`: he bought quickly, then complained
  quickly.

### `continually`

Core sentence:

`It rained continually.`

Language feel:

`雨不是下一阵就走，而是一直下、反复下，没完没了。`

Explain:

- `continually` makes the rain feel like a daily pressure.
- One rainy day is not the problem; continual rain changes routine and mood.
- It affects walking, drying clothes, outdoor exercise, travel, and comfort.

### `bitterly`

Core sentence:

`It was often bitterly cold.`

Language feel:

`不是有点冷，而是冷得刺人、冷得发苦。`

Explain:

- `bitterly` is stronger and more bodily than `very`.
- It helps the student feel why Harrison could not bear the weather.
- It contrasts sharply with `sunshine`.

Use this compact contrast:

| Word | Not just... | Real feeling |
| --- | --- | --- |
| immediately | soon | almost at once, dream hit reality |
| continually | often | again and again, no relief |
| bitterly | very | painfully, unpleasantly cold |

These words should receive real teaching time in the script, not be treated as
minor supporting vocabulary.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `immediately`
   - Meaning: at once; almost no delay.
   - Bridge: Harrison begins to complain almost as soon as he starts living in
     the house.
   - Example: `Almost immediately he began to complain about the weather.`
2. `continually`
   - Meaning: happening again and again, or without stopping for long.
   - Bridge: continual rain changes daily life, not just one afternoon.
   - Example: `It rained continually.`
3. `bitterly`
   - Meaning: extremely and unpleasantly, especially with cold.
   - Bridge: bitterly cold feels sharp, damp, and hard to bear after years of
     sunshine.
   - Example: `It was often bitterly cold.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `except` = not including; the missing thing in the title
- `Mediterranean` = the region around the Mediterranean Sea
- `weather` = daily condition of rain, temperature, wind, sunshine
- `retire` = stop working after a long career
- `settle down` = begin living steadily in one place
- `country` = countryside in this lesson
- `no sooner... than...` = as soon as one thing happened, another happened
- `complain about` = say that something is bad or annoying
- `even though` = although
- `sunshine` = sunlight and sunny weather
- `got a shock` = was unpleasantly surprised
- `as if` = as though
- `bear` = accept or endure something difficult
- `hardly... when...` = almost no time had passed before something happened
- `dream ended` = the long hope was over

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into planning a real move,
trip, or life change with daily details.

Recommended scenario:

`You want to move to a beautiful place or join a new school / camp / activity. You explain the dream, then list the real daily factors you must check before deciding.`

Student should practice:

- saying what they have dreamed of doing
- naming what they have planned
- remembering one easily forgotten daily factor
- talking about weather and lifestyle
- using `except` to describe a missing factor
- using expressive adverbs if natural

Useful expressions:

- `I have dreamed of living near the sea.`
- `I have planned where to stay.`
- `But I must think about the weather.`
- `It may rain continually.`
- `It may be bitterly cold.`
- `I should not decide immediately.`
- `I thought of everything except...`

The exercise should make the student think: beautiful place first, real daily
life second.
