# Lesson 22 Plan - A Glass Envelope

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 22. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 22 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 22
- Title: `A glass envelope`
- Chinese title: `玻璃信封`
- Core bridge: `这不是单纯的“瓶中信很浪漫”，而是一次偶然的低效率随机连接，后来变成两个同龄女孩之间更可靠、更稳定的通信。瓶子像玻璃信封，但真正长期联系还是要靠邮局。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about chance connection and reliable communication, not
about encouraging children to throw bottles into the sea.

- Do not present throwing a bottle into the sea as a behaviour to copy today.
  - Explain it as an old story device with imagination.
  - Modern environmental awareness makes throwing glass into the sea
    inappropriate.
- Explain `Holland` carefully.
  - In the lesson, `Holland` points to the Netherlands in Europe.
  - Keep the explanation compact; do not turn it into a long geography lecture.
- Explain `the Channel` clearly.
  - Here it means the English Channel between England/Britain and mainland
    Europe.
  - The child needs this geography to understand how the bottle could travel.
- Use a simplified no-text map in the visuals.
  - The map should show England/Britain, the English Channel, mainland Europe,
    and the Holland/Netherlands direction.
  - Do not put readable labels or country names inside the image; narration and
    slide text can explain the geography.
- Do not make the story feel like modern social media only.
  - Modern random messages can be a bridge, but the original story is a paper
    note in a bottle and then letters by post.
- Keep the final turn practical.
  - The bottle is surprising, but the post office is faster and more reliable.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a random message that becomes a real correspondence.

Jane never dreamed of receiving a letter from a girl of her own age in Holland.
Last year, while travelling across the Channel, she put her name and address on
a piece of paper, put the paper into a bottle, and threw the bottle into the
sea. She forgot about it. Ten months later, a girl in Holland wrote to her.
Now the two girls write to each other regularly. However, they have decided to
use the post office. Letters cost a little more, but they travel faster.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`bottle message -> sea journey -> unexpected reply -> same-age friendship -> regular letters -> post office is faster`

The ending should land as: "The bottle made the connection, but the post office
keeps the connection going."

## One-Sentence Hook

Jane threw a paper message into the sea, forgot it, and ten months later it came
back as a real friendship.

## Story Focus

The chance-to-regular-communication chain is:

1. Jane never dreamed of receiving a letter from a girl of her own age in
   Holland.
2. Last year, her family travelled across the Channel.
3. Jane put her name and address on a piece of paper.
4. She put the paper into a bottle.
5. She threw the bottle into the sea.
6. She never thought of it again.
7. Ten months later, she received a letter from a girl in Holland.
8. The two girls now write to each other regularly.
9. They decide to use the post office.
10. Letters cost a little more, but travel faster.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`romantic chance` versus `reliable communication`.

## Geography And Culture Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- `Holland` in the lesson means the Netherlands, a country in Europe.
- `the Channel` means the English Channel, the sea between England/Britain and
  mainland Europe.
- Jane is travelling across the Channel when she throws the bottle into the sea.
- The bottle later reaches a girl in Holland, so the story needs a simple
  geography picture.
- A `post office` is where letters can be sent through an official mail system.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这篇课文如果只说“瓶子漂到了 Holland”，孩子脑子里可能没有方向感。我们可以先看一张非常简单的无文字欧洲方位图：左边是英国，右边是欧洲大陆，中间是英吉利海峡，荷兰在欧洲大陆靠海的一边。Jane 横渡海峡时扔下瓶子，瓶子后来漂到荷兰女孩那里。地图只是帮你看见路线，不需要背欧洲地理。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the bottle-message scene to present-day experiences:

- a message in a bottle as an old romantic story device
- a random postcard or pen-pal exchange
- a summer camp friend who becomes a regular correspondent
- a random online message that becomes a real friendship
- choosing a reliable contact method after a chance meeting
- email, messages, or postal letters as different communication channels

The durable pattern is:

`random message -> surprise reply -> real connection -> regular communication -> reliable channel`

Do not let the modern bridge erase the old charm. The bottle is the story's
magical accident; the post office is the sensible continuation.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is useful relationship-and-communication phrases:

1. `of one's own age`
   - `a girl of her own age` means a girl who is about the same age as Jane.
   - Useful patterns:
     - `a boy of my own age`
     - `a girl of her own age`
     - `students of our own age`
2. `write to each other`
   - Means both people write letters/messages to one another.
   - Useful patterns:
     - `They write to each other.`
     - `They help each other.`
     - `They talk to each other.`
3. `a little more / faster`
   - `Letters will cost a little more, but they will certainly travel faster.`
   - This shows a practical comparison: more cost, better speed.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson is not only about a bottle. It teaches how to describe a connection
between two people: same age, writing to each other, writing regularly, and
choosing a faster way to send letters.

Use a compact display:

| Phrase | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| a girl of her own age | a girl about the same age as Jane |
| write to each other | both girls write letters to one another |
| regularly | again and again, in a steady way |
| a little more | slightly more money |
| travel faster | arrive more quickly |

Secondary language points:

- `never dreamed of receiving`
  - Meaning: never imagined that she would receive.
  - `dream of + -ing` is useful but should not become a heavy grammar lecture.
- `thought of it again`
  - Meaning: remembered or considered it again.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `dream`
   - Meaning in this lesson: imagine something could happen.
   - Bridge: `never dreamed of receiving` = never imagined receiving.
   - Example: `Jane never dreamed of receiving a letter.`
2. `Channel`
   - Meaning in this lesson: the English Channel, the sea between
     England/Britain and mainland Europe.
   - Bridge: use a simple no-text map to show the crossing.
   - Example: `travelling across the Channel`
3. `regularly`
   - Meaning: again and again in a steady, repeated way.
   - Bridge: regularly write, regularly practise, regularly meet.
   - Example: `Both girls write to each other regularly now.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `Holland` = the Netherlands in this lesson
- `the Netherlands` = modern country name for Holland in broad student
  explanation
- `age` = how old someone is
- `of her own age` = about the same age as her
- `address` = where someone can receive letters
- `bottle` = container made of glass/plastic
- `throw / threw` = send something through the air by hand
- `receive` = get
- `each other` = one another
- `post office` = place/system for sending letters
- `cost` = require money
- `a little more` = slightly more
- `travel faster` = arrive more quickly

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a small sea-journey mystery that becomes a
friendship story.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "A bottle thrown into the sea disappears for ten months, then returns
   as a letter from another girl."
2. Give a simple geography bridge: England/Britain, the Channel, mainland
   Europe, Holland/Netherlands.
3. Add the environmental note: understand the old story device, do not copy it
   today.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: a simplified no-text Europe map showing Britain, the Channel,
     mainland Europe, and the Holland direction with a dotted bottle route.
   - Frame 2: Jane on a boat across the Channel putting her name and address
     into a bottle.
   - Frame 3: ten months later, a girl in Holland finds the bottle/message and
     writes back.
   - Frame 4: both girls write to each other regularly using letters and the
     post office.
5. Discussion: why the bottle is magical but the post office is practical.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `dream`, `Channel`, `regularly`.
8. Grammar/phrases: `of one's own age`, `each other`, `a little more / faster`.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals gentle, geographical, and correspondence-focused.

- Use one consistent Jane, one consistent Holland/Netherlands girl, and a clear
  bottle/letter motif.
- Suggested storyboard `visual_consistency`:
  `Keep the same two girls and bottle-letter motif across the four frames. Jane
  is a curious English girl around twelve years old, shoulder-length brown hair,
  blue coat, thoughtful and excited expression. The Holland/Netherlands girl is
  around the same age, blonde or light-brown hair, yellow raincoat or warm
  cardigan, friendly surprised expression. The bottle is a simple clear glass
  bottle with a rolled blank paper inside; letters are blank paper sheets with
  no readable words. Keep the palette soft sea-blue, cream, and warm daylight;
  no readable text anywhere.`
- Frame 1 should be a simplified no-text map, not a realistic labelled atlas.
  - Show Britain/England as an island shape on the left.
  - Show mainland Europe on the right.
  - Show the English Channel as blue water between them.
  - Show a dotted route or drifting bottle path toward the Netherlands/Holland
    direction.
  - Use icons, shapes, dots, and route lines only; no country names or labels.
- Avoid readable names, addresses, stamps, country labels, or map labels.
- Do not make the bottle-in-sea act look like an environmental recommendation.
  Keep it storybook-like and clearly old-fashioned.
- The final frame should feel more reliable and steady: desks, letters, mailbox
  or post office feeling without readable signs.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Simplified no-text Europe map showing Britain, the Channel, mainland Europe,
   and a dotted bottle route toward Holland/Netherlands.
2. Jane travelling across the Channel, putting a blank address paper into a
   clear bottle on a boat.
3. Ten months later, a same-age girl in Holland/Netherlands finds the bottle or
   receives the message near the coast.
4. The two girls write letters to each other regularly, with a post-office/mail
   motif showing reliable communication.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about a chance connection that becomes
regular communication through a reliable channel.

Good prompts:

- `你有没有偶然认识一个同龄朋友，后来变成经常联系？`
- `如果你收到一个来自远方同龄人的 message，你会怎么回复？`
- `你会选择随机留言、邮件、微信/短信，还是邮局？为什么？`

Expected scaffold:

`I never dreamed of receiving a message from ___. He/She is of my own age. Now we write to each other regularly. ___ costs a little more, but it travels faster.`

Add a modern version:

`We first met by chance, but now we use ___ because it is faster and more reliable.`

Keep the exercise safe and practical. Avoid encouraging anonymous contact with
strangers as a behaviour; frame it as supervised pen-pal, camp friend, class
exchange, or family-approved correspondence.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the sea route, chance reply, and regular
communication.

Recommended sentences:

1. `My daughter, Jane, never dreamed of receiving a letter from a girl of her own age in Holland.`
   - Focus: `never dreamed of receiving`, `of her own age`, `Holland`
2. `Last year, we were travelling across the Channel.`
   - Focus: `travelling across the Channel`
3. `Jane put a piece of paper with her name and address on it into a bottle.`
   - Focus: `name and address`, `into a bottle`
4. `She threw the bottle into the sea.`
   - Focus: `threw`, past of `throw`
5. `She never thought of it again.`
   - Focus: forgot / did not think about it
6. `Ten months later, she received a letter from a girl in Holland.`
   - Focus: long delay, surprise
7. `Both girls write to each other regularly now.`
   - Focus: `each other`, `regularly`
8. `They have decided to use the post office.`
   - Focus: practical decision
9. `Letters will cost a little more, but they will certainly travel faster.`
   - Focus: `a little more`, `travel faster`

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 1, 2, 3, 6, 7, and
9.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the chain:

`never dreamed -> across the Channel -> bottle into the sea -> ten months later -> write to each other regularly -> post office travels faster`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a tense chart. Start from useful phrases for connection.

Suggested explanation:

`这课最有用的不是某一个复杂语法，而是几组“人与人建立联系”的表达。a girl of her own age 是和 Jane 同龄的女孩；write to each other 是两个人互相写信；regularly 是后来变成固定联系；a little more... faster 是比较：多花一点钱，但快得多。`

Use a compact display:

| Phrase | Student use |
| --- | --- |
| of my own age | a friend about my age |
| write to each other | we both send messages/letters |
| regularly | again and again, not just once |
| a little more | slightly more money or effort |
| travel faster | arrive faster |

Then connect to the title:

`The bottle is like a glass envelope for the first message. But once the girls
become real correspondents, they choose a better channel: the post office.`

## Tone

Wonder, sea-distance, and practical friendship.

The child should feel:

- "I understand where the Channel and Holland are in the story."
- "I know Holland means the Netherlands in this lesson."
- "I can see why the bottle is surprising but not reliable."
- "I can use `of my own age` and `each other`."
- "I understand why the post office is less romantic but faster."

Avoid:

- encouraging children to throw bottles into the sea
- making the geography too detailed
- putting readable English or country labels inside images
- turning the story into generic internet chat
- skipping the practical ending about the post office
