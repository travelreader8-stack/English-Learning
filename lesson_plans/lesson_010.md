# Lesson 10 Plan - Not for Jazz

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 10. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 10 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 10
- Title: `Not for jazz`
- Chinese title: `不适于演奏爵士乐`
- Core bridge: `这不是说 jazz 不好，而是说一个 1681 年的古老键盘乐器太脆弱，不适合被当成现代键盘大力弹；老物件需要合适的使用方式和边界。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson needs careful framing so the student does not misunderstand the
title.

- `Not for jazz`:
  - Do not imply that jazz is bad, noisy, or low quality.
  - The problem is mismatch: an old, delicate clavichord is not suitable for a
    strong jazz playing style.
- `clavichord`:
  - Do not translate it only as `古钢琴` and move on.
  - Explain it as an early keyboard instrument: a historical instrument that
    looks a little like an old small piano, but is quieter and more delicate.
- Old object boundary:
  - The clavichord is in the living room, but it is not a toy or an everyday
    keyboard.
  - Treat it like a family heirloom or museum-like object: interesting, valuable,
    and fragile.
- Student-facing tone:
  - Do not blame the visitor as stupid or bad. She used the wrong playing style
    on the wrong instrument.
  - The lesson should teach respect for old objects and correct use, not fear of
    touching anything.
- Adult context:
  - If mentioning jazz, explain it simply as a music style with strong rhythm
    and expressive playing; do not turn the lesson into music history.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about old valuable objects and matching the tool to the use.

The family has an old musical instrument called a clavichord. It was made in
Germany in 1681 and has belonged to the family for a long time. It was bought by
the narrator's grandfather and is kept in the living room.

Recently a visitor damaged it because she tried to play jazz on it. She struck
the keys too hard and two strings were broken. The father was shocked. Now the
children are not allowed to touch it, and a friend of the father's is repairing
it.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`A thing can be beautiful and useful, but still not suitable for every style of use.`

## One-Sentence Hook

Imagine your family has a very old instrument from 1681. A visitor treats it
like a modern keyboard, plays too hard, and breaks two strings.

## Story Punchline

The object-history chain is:

1. The family has an old musical instrument.
2. It is called a clavichord.
3. It was made in Germany in 1681.
4. It is kept in the living room.
5. It has belonged to the family for a long time.
6. It was bought by the narrator's grandfather.
7. Recently it was damaged by a visitor.
8. The visitor tried to play jazz on it.
9. She struck the keys too hard and broke two strings.
10. The father was shocked.
11. Now the children are not allowed to touch it.
12. It is being repaired by a family friend.

The ending should land as: "The instrument was not against music; it was simply
not for that kind of playing."

## Modern Bridge

Do not assume the student knows old keyboard instruments. Bridge to modern
objects with clear usage boundaries:

- a grandfather's old camera that cannot be treated like a phone
- an old record player that cannot be tapped like a Bluetooth speaker
- a museum object that can be looked at but not touched
- a finished LEGO model, figure, or handmade project that should not be pulled
  apart casually
- an old guitar, piano, game console, or mechanical watch that needs careful use
- a delicate model or collectible that is displayed in the living room but is
  not a toy

Useful modern idea:

`The problem is not that the music is wrong. The problem is using the wrong method on a delicate object.`

## Vocabulary Strategy

Use exactly 3 formal vocab blocks, but explain the instrument words throughout
the story.

### Formal Vocab Blocks

Prefer:

1. `instrument`
   - A tool for making music. Connect to piano, guitar, violin, keyboard, and
     drums.
2. `damage`
   - To harm or break something, especially something that should be kept safe.
3. `allow`
   - To let someone do something. `not allowed to touch it` means there is a
     rule and a boundary.

### Required Supporting Vocabulary

Explain these in context:

- `clavichord`
  - An old keyboard instrument, earlier and more delicate than a modern piano.
- `jazz`
  - A music style with strong rhythm and expressive playing; not the bad thing
    in the story.
- `musical`
  - Related to music.
- `recently`
  - Not long ago.
- `key`
  - The part of a keyboard instrument that you press.
- `string`
  - A stretched wire/string inside an instrument that makes sound.
- `struck`
  - Hit or pressed with force. In this story, she struck the keys too hard.
- `shocked`
  - Very surprised and upset.
- `touch`
  - Put your hands on something; here it is forbidden.
- `repair`
  - Fix something that is damaged.
- `belonged to`
  - Has been owned by someone or a family.

### Word Network

The script should build a word network:

1. **Music object**
   `musical instrument / clavichord / key / string / jazz`
2. **Old object history**
   `was made / has belonged to / was bought / is kept`
3. **Damage and rules**
   `damaged / struck too hard / broken / shocked / not allowed / repaired`

## Language Focus

Use these language points:

- `It is called a clavichord`
  - Naming an object.
- `It was made in Germany in 1681`
  - Its origin and age.
- `Our clavichord is kept in the living room`
  - Where the family keeps a valuable object.
- `It has belonged to our family for a long time`
  - Long family history up to now.
- `The instrument was bought by my grandfather`
  - Who brought it into the family.
- `It was damaged by a visitor`
  - The event that changed the object.
- `We are not allowed to touch it`
  - A new rule after the damage.
- `It is being repaired`
  - The repair is happening now.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: passive voice as an object's life story.

Do not teach passive voice as a formula first. Teach it as a way to tell what
has happened to an object:

- Name:
  - `It is called a clavichord.`
- Origin:
  - `It was made in Germany in 1681.`
- Place:
  - `It is kept in the living room.`
- Family history:
  - `The instrument was bought by my grandfather.`
- Accident:
  - `It was damaged by a visitor.`
- Rule:
  - `We are not allowed to touch it.`
- Current repair:
  - `It is being repaired by a friend of my father's.`

Student-friendly explanation:

`Passive voice is useful when the object is the main character. In this story, the clavichord is the main character, so many sentences tell what was done to it.`

## Read Aloud Sentences

Use 8 guided sentences. The goal is to make the object-history and passive
voice pattern easy to say before the student moves into modern old-object
examples.

- `We have an old musical instrument.`
  - focus: `old musical instrument` as one phrase.
- `It is called a clavichord.`
  - focus: `clavichord`; pronounce it slowly and clearly.
- `It was made in Germany in 1681.`
  - focus: `was made`, `Germany`, and the year `1681`.
- `It has belonged to our family for a long time.`
  - focus: `has belonged to` and `for a long time`.
- `Recently it was damaged by a visitor.`
  - focus: `Recently`, `was damaged`, and `by a visitor`.
- `She tried to play jazz on it!`
  - focus: the surprise; jazz is a style, not the villain.
- `She struck the keys too hard and two of the strings were broken.`
  - focus: `struck the keys too hard`, `strings were broken`.
- `Now we are not allowed to touch it. It is being repaired by a friend of my father's.`
  - focus: `not allowed to touch it`, `is being repaired`.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. A warm living room with an old clavichord displayed as a treasured family
   object. It should look like a historical keyboard instrument, not a modern
   piano or electronic keyboard.
2. A gentle object-history frame: the clavichord's old age and family history,
   with a grandfather memory or old photograph feeling, but no readable text.
3. A visitor playing too forcefully, with keys struck too hard and two strings
   broken. Keep it clear but not slapstick or cruel.
4. The father looks shocked; the instrument is now protected and being repaired
   carefully by a knowledgeable adult/friend.

No text, letters, captions, readable signs, or visible English words in images.
Avoid making jazz look bad or chaotic; the visual problem is force and mismatch,
not the music itself.

## Tone

Curious, respectful, and lightly dramatic. This should feel like a family-object
story: a treasured old thing has a history, a guest uses it wrongly, and the
family learns a clearer boundary.

The script should be sympathetic to the visitor but firm about the rule:
delicate old objects need the right kind of use.
