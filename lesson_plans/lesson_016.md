# Lesson 16 Plan - A Polite Request

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 16. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 16 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 16
- Title: `A polite request`
- Chinese title: `彬彬有礼的要求`
- Core bridge: `这不是单纯的停车规则课，而是同一个规则可以用不同语气表达：冷冰冰的罚单让人害怕，礼貌的提醒反而让人更愿意遵守。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about polite rule enforcement, not about car ownership.

- Do not center the lesson on driving knowledge.
  - The student may not have any driving or parking experience.
  - Use parking only as the story's original surface.
- Explain `ticket` carefully.
  - Here it means a parking ticket / fine notice, not a train ticket or cinema
    ticket.
- Explain Sweden only as the place where the narrator received the polite note.
  - Do not over-teach Swedish culture or make broad claims.
  - The contrast is between punishment tone and reminder tone.
- Do not make the police look frightening.
  - The story says traffic police can sometimes be very polite.
  - The note should feel calm, civic, and persuasive.
- The raw source has lowercase `sir`; student-facing script can normalize this
  to `Sir` without editing `web/data/lessons.json`.
- Do not make "polite" mean "unclear".
  - The note is polite, but the rule is still clear: this is a `No Parking`
    area.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a polite reminder that is stronger than a rude order.

The narrator explains that if you park in the wrong place, a traffic policeman
will soon find your car. You are lucky if he lets you go without a ticket. But
traffic police are sometimes polite. During a holiday in Sweden, the narrator
finds a note on his car. The note welcomes him to the city, says the place is a
`No Parking` area, and gently reminds him to pay attention to street signs. The
narrator says that if you receive a request like this, you cannot fail to obey
it.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`wrong parking -> expected ticket -> polite note -> clear rule -> gentle reminder -> willing obedience`

The ending should land as: "The request works because it is both polite and
clear."

## One-Sentence Hook

He parked in the wrong place and expected a fine, but found a polite note so
well written that he could not help obeying it.

## Story Focus

The persuasion chain is:

1. If you park in the wrong place, traffic police will find the car.
2. If the policeman lets you go without a ticket, you are lucky.
3. This does not always happen.
4. Traffic police are sometimes very polite.
5. During a holiday in Sweden, the narrator finds a note on his car.
6. The note welcomes him to the city.
7. The note says this is a `No Parking` area.
8. The note says he will enjoy his stay if he pays attention to street signs.
9. The note says it is only a reminder.
10. A request like this is hard not to obey.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`rude order / cold fine` versus `polite request / clear reminder`.

## Rule And Culture Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- `traffic policeman` means a police officer who manages road traffic and
  parking rules.
- A parking `ticket` here means a fine notice.
- `No Parking` area means you cannot leave your car there.
- `street signs` tell people the rules of the road or street.
- A `reminder` is a note that helps you remember or notice something; it is not
  the same as a punishment.
- Sweden is simply the holiday setting. The student only needs to understand
  that the note came from a city trying to guide visitors politely.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这篇课文里的车和停车罚单，对小孩子可能有点远。你可以把它换成学校或小区里的提醒：不要乱停共享单车、不要在走廊跑、图书馆里请安静。同样是让你守规则，有人会写“严禁！违者处罚！”也有人会写“欢迎你来这里，请注意这些标志，这只是一个提醒。”这篇讲的就是第二种表达。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old parking scene to present-day experiences:

- shared-bike parking reminders in an app
- school hallway signs such as "Please walk" instead of "Don't run"
- library quiet signs
- apartment or community notices
- museum or subway reminders
- classroom rules written in a polite tone
- game or app prompts that guide behavior without scolding

The durable pattern is:

`rule -> reminder -> tone -> willingness to obey`

Do not turn the lesson into a legal lecture. Keep it about language, tone, and
how a request can be both polite and effective.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is the `if... will...` conditional pattern.

Teach it as a rule-and-result chain:

- `If you park your car in the wrong place, a traffic policeman will soon find it.`
- `You will be very lucky if he lets you go without a ticket.`
- `You will enjoy your stay here if you pay attention to our street signs.`
- `If you receive a request like this, you cannot fail to obey it.`

Student-facing explanation:

`if` gives the condition; the other part tells what will happen or what the
result will be. This lesson uses `if` to explain rules and consequences.

Use a compact chain:

- If you park wrongly -> police will find it.
- If there is no ticket -> you are lucky.
- If you pay attention -> you will enjoy your stay.
- If the request is this polite -> you will obey it.

Do not over-teach all conditional types. This lesson only needs practical
`if + present, will + verb`.

Secondary language point:

- `cannot fail to obey it`
  - Meaning: you certainly will obey it; it is hard not to.
  - Explain without making the double-negative structure too heavy.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `ticket`
   - Meaning in this lesson: a fine notice for parking wrongly.
   - Bridge: not a movie ticket or train ticket here.
   - Example: `without a ticket`
2. `reminder`
   - Meaning: a note or message that helps you remember or notice something.
   - Bridge: phone reminders, teacher reminders, app reminders.
   - Example: `This note is only a reminder.`
3. `obey`
   - Meaning: follow a rule, order, or request.
   - Bridge: obey traffic signs, school rules, game rules.
   - Example: `You cannot fail to obey it.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `park` = leave a car somewhere
- `traffic policeman` = 交通警察
- `No Parking area` = 禁止停车区域
- `note` = a short written message
- `street signs` = street rule signs
- `pay attention to` = notice carefully
- `request` = a polite ask
- `fail to` = not succeed in doing something

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a small lesson in social intelligence.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "What kind of note would make you obey a rule more willingly than a
   fine?"
2. Bridge from parking to everyday reminders.
3. Explain the original scene: wrong parking, traffic police, ticket.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: a car parked in a `No Parking` area.
   - Frame 2: narrator expects a ticket or punishment.
   - Frame 3: narrator finds a polite note welcoming him to the city.
   - Frame 4: narrator reads the reminder and decides to obey the street signs.
5. Discussion: why the polite note works.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `ticket`, `reminder`, `obey`.
8. Grammar: `if... will...` as rule and result.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals clean, civic, and friendly.

- City street in Sweden or a generic European city, but do not overdo travel
  postcards.
- A visible `No Parking` sign and a small note on the car.
- The note should look polite, not angry.
- Traffic police can appear calm and professional, not threatening.
- Use warm daylight and clear street signs.
- Avoid crash scenes, towing scenes, or harsh punishment imagery.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Car parked in a clearly marked `No Parking` area.
2. Narrator looking worried, expecting a ticket.
3. A polite note on the windshield: welcome + reminder.
4. Narrator reading street signs carefully and moving/obeying calmly.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about writing a polite reminder.

Good prompts:

- `如果你要提醒同学不要在走廊跑，你会怎么说得既清楚又礼貌？`
- `如果共享单车停错地方，App 可以怎样提醒你，而不是只吓唬你？`
- `写一个 polite request：欢迎对方，然后说明规则，再给一个温和提醒。`

Expected scaffold:

`Welcome to ___. This is a ___ area. You will ___ if you pay attention to ___. This note is only a reminder.`

Keep the exercise productive. The student should learn to express rules in a
clear but polite way.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the rule-result chain and polite request.

Recommended sentences:

1. `If you park your car in the wrong place, a traffic policeman will soon find it.`
   - Focus: `If you park`, `will soon find it`
2. `You will be very lucky if he lets you go without a ticket.`
   - Focus: `without a ticket`
3. `Traffic police are sometimes very polite.`
   - Focus: contrast word `polite`
4. `During a holiday in Sweden, I found this note on my car.`
   - Focus: setting + `note`
5. `Sir, we welcome you to our city.`
   - Focus: polite opening
6. `This is a "No Parking" area.`
   - Focus: clear rule
7. `You will enjoy your stay here if you pay attention to our street signs.`
   - Focus: `if you pay attention to`
8. `This note is only a reminder.`
   - Focus: `only a reminder`
9. `If you receive a request like this, you cannot fail to obey it!`
   - Focus: final conclusion

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 8,
and 9.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the polite note as a single
well-structured request:

`welcome -> rule -> helpful reason -> reminder`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a full conditional-grammar chart. Start from rule and result.

Suggested explanation:

`这篇课文一直在用 if：如果你这样做，会有什么结果。if 后面给条件，另一半句子给结果。`

Use a compact display:

| Condition | Result |
| --- | --- |
| If you park in the wrong place | a policeman will find it |
| if he lets you go without a ticket | you will be very lucky |
| if you pay attention to signs | you will enjoy your stay |
| If you receive a request like this | you cannot fail to obey it |

Then connect to tone:

`同样是规则，语气可以很硬，也可以很礼貌。这篇的英语重点不只是 if 条件句，也是怎样把规则说得让人愿意听。`

## Tone

Clear, polite, and a little witty.

The narration can compare two versions:

- Hard version: "No parking. Pay a fine."
- Polite version: "Welcome to our city. This is a No Parking area. This note is
  only a reminder."

The child should feel:

- "I understand what a parking ticket is."
- "I can see why the polite note works."
- "I can use if to explain rules and results."
- "Polite language can still be strong and clear."

## Acceptance Criteria For Production

Lesson 16 production is successful only if:

- The script clearly frames the lesson as polite rule enforcement, not a driving
  lesson.
- The modern bridge connects parking to everyday reminders, school/community
  rules, apps, or shared-bike notices.
- The 4 retell frames show wrong parking, expected ticket, polite note, and
  willing obedience.
- The 3 formal vocab blocks are `ticket`, `reminder`, and `obey`.
- Supporting words include `park`, `traffic policeman`, `No Parking area`,
  `note`, `street signs`, `pay attention to`, `request`, and `fail to`.
- The grammar section teaches `if... will...` through rule and result.
- The polite note is shown as both gentle and clear.
- The living-scene exercise asks the student to write a polite reminder.
- The final line `you cannot fail to obey it` lands as the lesson's conclusion.
- The outro includes the full practice order and the concrete read-aloud steps:
