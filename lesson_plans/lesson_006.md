## Production Warnings

This lesson needs careful context and tone. Do not present it as a simple
"beggar joke."

- Housing context:
  - The story is set in an older British street where a house door can open
    directly toward the street.
  - Do not make the student imagine a modern Chinese high-rise apartment with
    security gates, elevators, and corridor doors.
  - Explain early that this is an old-style street-life situation.
- Safety context:
  - In a modern Chinese setting, a child should not open the door to a stranger
    or handle the situation alone.
  - If a modern bridge mentions someone at the door, include an adult, parent,
    guard, or neighbour as the safe responder.
- Human dignity:
  - Do not make poverty or homelessness the punchline.
  - The humor is the routine and the information gap: the narrator is new, but
    the whole street already knows Percy Buttons.
- Student-facing display:
  - Prefer `珀西·巴顿斯` rather than the raw data punctuation
    `珀西.巴顿斯`.
  - `call at` means "visit / stop at a place", not "make a phone call".
- Adult detail:
  - `a glass of beer` is part of the original adult scene. Do not turn it into a
    child-facing action or modern recommendation. Mention it only as story
    detail if needed.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

For a present-day Chinese child, the hardest part is not the vocabulary. It is
the scene itself: why would a beggar knock at someone's home?

The courseware should bridge this first:

- In many older British streets, houses are close to the road, and the front
  door is part of street life.
- The narrator has just moved into Bridge Street, so he does not yet know the
  local routines or familiar faces.
- Percy Buttons is a stranger to the narrator, but not to the street. The
  neighbour says everybody knows him.

The real story is about a new resident's information gap. What feels strange and
unexpected to a newcomer is already a regular monthly event to the community.

## One-Sentence Hook

You move into an old British street house. The next day, a man knocks at your
door, asks for a meal, performs a strange little show, and leaves. Later your
neighbour tells you: everybody here knows him.

## Story Punchline

The comic chain is:

1. The narrator has just moved into a house in Bridge Street.
2. A beggar knocks at the door the next day.
3. He asks for a meal and a glass of beer.
4. In return, he stands on his head and sings songs.
5. The narrator gives him a meal.
6. Percy eats, drinks, puts a piece of cheese in his pocket, and leaves.
7. Later a neighbour explains who he is.
8. Everybody in the street knows him.
9. His name is Percy Buttons.
10. He visits every house in the street once a month and always asks for the
    same thing.

The ending should land as: "I thought this was a strange one-time event, but it
was actually a local routine."

## Modern Bridge

Do not ask the student to imagine a beggar walking into a modern apartment
building. Instead, connect the emotional and social pattern:

- moving into a new neighbourhood and not knowing the local people yet
- a new student entering a class where everyone else already knows a routine
- a person who often appears near a gate, small shop, school entrance, or
  neighbourhood street
- a neighbour, guard, parent, or classmate explaining, "Everybody knows him"
- learning local rules from people who have lived there longer

Also make the safety line explicit: in real life today, if a stranger knocks at
the door, a child should call an adult instead of opening the door alone.

## Language Focus

Use only the strongest points. Recommended focus:

- `I have just moved...`
  - This explains the information gap: the narrator is new here.
- `knocked at my door`
  - A physical knock at the door, natural in an old street-house context.
- `in return for this`
  - "As payment / as thanks / as an exchange." He gives a performance in return
    for food and beer.
- `stood on his head`
  - The strange performance detail. Use as story color rather than a core vocab
    block.
- `calls at every house`
  - Means visits every house; it is not a telephone call.
- `once a month` and `always asks`
  - These reveal the routine.

## Vocab Blocks

Use exactly 3 vocab blocks. Prefer:

1. `beggar`
2. `in return for`
3. `call at`

If `pocket`, `meal`, or `neighbour` needs explanation, include it in the retell
or discussion rather than adding a fourth vocab block.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: new situation, yesterday's event, regular habit.

Contrast:

- `I have just moved...`
  - New status now: I am new in this street.
- `Yesterday a beggar knocked... / asked... / stood... / sang...`
  - One chain of past events yesterday.
- `He calls at every house... once a month and always asks...`
  - A regular habit, not a one-time event.

Keep this as story logic, not a tense chart.

## Read Aloud Sentences

Use 7 guided sentences. The goal is to make the old street scene and the final
routine easy to say before the student moves into modern neighbourhood examples.

- `I have just moved to a house in Bridge Street.`
  - focus: `have just moved` as "I am new here now."
- `Yesterday a beggar knocked at my door.`
  - focus: `knocked at my door` as one phrase.
- `He asked me for a meal and a glass of beer.`
  - focus: `asked me for` and the two objects.
- `In return for this, the beggar stood on his head and sang songs.`
  - focus: pause after `In return for this`; read the performance detail clearly.
- `Then he put a piece of cheese in his pocket and went away.`
  - focus: `a piece of cheese` and `in his pocket`.
- `Later a neighbour told me about him. Everybody knows him.`
  - focus: the reveal; `Everybody knows him` should sound like the punchline.
- `He calls at every house in the street once a month and always asks for a meal and a glass of beer.`
  - focus: `calls at`, `once a month`, and `always asks`.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The exercise should transfer the story into a modern, safe "new neighbourhood /
local routine" situation. It should not ask the student to open the door to a
stranger or to give food or beer.

Suggested word bank:

- `have just moved`
- `knocked at my door`
- `in return for this`

- `calls at`
- `once a month`
- `always asks`

Suggested three fill scenes:

- New neighbourhood:
  `I ___ to this building, so I do not know the people near the gate yet.`
  Expected: `have just moved`
- Safe door response:
  `Someone ___, so I asked my dad to answer it.`
  Expected: `knocked at my door`
- Local regular:
  `My neighbour ___: "Don't worry. ___; he ___ this street ___."`
  Expected: `told me about him`, `everybody knows him`, `calls at`, `once a month`

Adjust wording if needed so the expected phrases appear exactly in the word
bank and fit naturally.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. An old British street with small houses close to the road; the narrator has
   just moved in, with a few moving boxes visible.
2. A respectful doorway scene: an adult narrator opens the door of a street
   house and sees Percy Buttons outside. Make clear this is an old street-house
   setting, not a modern apartment corridor.
3. Percy performs his strange exchange: he stands on his head and sings while
   the adult narrator looks surprised. Keep it warm and observational, not
   mocking.
4. A neighbour explains the routine; the street feels like a small community
   where people recognize Percy as a regular monthly visitor.

No text, letters, captions, readable signs, or visible English words in images.
Avoid making Percy frightening, dirty in a dehumanizing way, or cartoonishly
ridiculous.

## Tone

Humane, curious, and lightly comic. This should feel like a neighbourhood story
about a newcomer learning local routines. The humour comes from the reveal and
the repeated routine, not from poverty.
