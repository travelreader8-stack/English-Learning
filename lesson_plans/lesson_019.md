# Lesson 19 Plan - Sold Out

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 19. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 19 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 19
- Title: `Sold out`
- Chinese title: `票已售完`
- Core bridge: `这不是单纯的“没买到票”，而是一次买票过程里的三次转折：演出快开始了，票卖完了，突然有人退票，结果那两张票却是下周三的。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about ticketing, timing, and a disappointed reversal, not
about a generic theatre visit.

- Explain `ticket office` as a physical ticket counter.
  - Modern students may mostly know app booking, QR codes, and online tickets.
  - The original story happens at a traditional theatre ticket office.
- Explain that one `play` can have many `performances`.
  - The returned tickets are real tickets, but not for tonight's performance.
  - They are for next Wednesday's performance.
- Do not make the returned-ticket moment feel like a happy ending.
  - The narrator thinks he has found a solution, but the dates are wrong.
- Do not make `return` mean only "come back".
  - Here `return these two tickets` means give the tickets back / get a refund.
- Treat `sold out` as a core phrase even though it is not a single keyword in
  the source keyword list.
  - The title and whole story depend on this phrase.
- Keep `I might as well have them` mildly sad and resigned.
  - It means "It is not what I wanted, but I may as well take them now."
  - Do not present it as excited acceptance.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about one ticket-buying attempt that keeps changing direction.

The narrator and Susan are late for a play. The play may begin at any moment, or
may have begun already. The narrator hurries to the ticket office and asks for
two tickets. The tickets are sold out. Just then, another man comes to return
two tickets. The narrator quickly asks for those tickets, thinking the problem
is solved. But the girl explains that the tickets are for next Wednesday's
performance. The narrator accepts them sadly because there is not much else he
can do.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`late for play -> sold out -> returned tickets -> hope returns -> wrong date -> resigned ending`

The ending should land as: "He gets tickets, but not for the performance he came
to see."

## One-Sentence Hook

He finally gets two theatre tickets, but only after discovering that they are
for the wrong day.

## Story Focus

The reversal chain is:

1. The play may begin at any moment.
2. Susan thinks it may have begun already.
3. The narrator hurries to the ticket office.
4. He politely asks for two tickets.
5. The girl says the tickets have sold out.
6. Susan says, "What a pity!"
7. A man hurries to the ticket office with two tickets to return.
8. The narrator immediately asks for those two tickets.
9. The girl says they are for next Wednesday's performance.
10. The narrator says he might as well have them.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`no tickets` -> `sudden hope` -> `wrong performance`.

## Culture And Scene Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A `ticket office` is the place where people buy or return tickets at a
  theatre, station, cinema, or venue.
- In the original story, tickets are handled at a window by a person, not inside
  an app.
- A `performance` is one showing of a play, concert, or show.
- The same play can have many performances on different days.
- `next Wednesday's performance` means the ticket is not for tonight.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`现在买电影票、演唱会票，常常是在 App 里选日期、选场次、选座位。可是以前很多人会直接到剧院门口的 ticket office 买票。Lesson 19 的笑点就像你在 App 里看到今晚 sold out，突然有人退了两张票，你赶紧点进去，结果发现不是今晚，是下周三那一场。票是真的，可救不了今天这场。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the theatre ticket scene to present-day experiences:

- buying movie tickets in an app
- trying to get concert tickets before they sell out
- waiting for someone to return tickets or release seats
- choosing the wrong date or wrong performance in an app
- buying train or event tickets when there are only a few seats left
- seeing "sold out" on a popular product, game item, or event page

The durable pattern is:

`urgent plan -> sold out -> second chance -> hidden detail -> disappointment`

Do not over-teach theatre culture. Keep the focus on dates, performances,
availability, and the emotional turn from hope to disappointment.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is modal verbs for possibility, polite requests, and resigned
choice.

Teach these through function, not a full modal chart:

- `The play may begin at any moment.`
  - Meaning: It is possible that it will begin very soon.
  - Function: present/future possibility.
- `It may have begun already.`
  - Meaning: It is possible that it has already begun.
  - Function: present guess about an earlier event.
- `May I have two tickets please?`
  - Meaning: Please sell me two tickets.
  - Function: polite request.
- `Could I have those two tickets please?`
  - Meaning: Please let me buy those tickets.
  - Function: polite request, slightly softer than direct `Can I`.
- `I might as well have them.`
  - Meaning: They are not what I wanted, but I may as well take them.
  - Function: resigned acceptance.

Student-facing explanation:

`may` is not always the same meaning. At the start, `may` means "maybe". At the
ticket office, `May I...` is a polite way to ask. At the end, `might as well`
means "not ideal, but I will do it anyway."

Use a compact display:

| Expression | Function | Story meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| may begin | possibility | The play may start soon. |
| may have begun | guess about the earlier past | Maybe it has already started. |
| May I have...? | polite request | Please sell me tickets. |
| Could I have...? | polite request | Please let me buy those tickets. |
| might as well | resigned choice | I may as well take them. |

Secondary language point:

- `at any moment`
  - Meaning: very soon; possibly in the next second.
  - Good for urgent timing.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `ticket`
   - Meaning: proof that you can enter a theatre, cinema, train, concert, or
     other event.
   - Bridge: paper ticket, QR code ticket, app ticket.
   - Example: `May I have two tickets please?`
2. `return`
   - Meaning in this lesson: give tickets back, often because you cannot use
     them.
   - Bridge: return a product, return a library book, return tickets.
   - Example: `Can I return these two tickets?`
3. `pity`
   - Meaning: something disappointing or unfortunate.
   - Bridge: when a plan fails, you can say `What a pity!`
   - Example: `What a pity!`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `sold out` = all tickets/products have been sold
- `ticket office` = place/window where tickets are sold
- `play` = theatre story performed by actors
- `performance` = one showing of a play/show/concert
- `next Wednesday` = the Wednesday after this one
- `hurry` = move quickly because there is little time
- `exclaim` = say something suddenly and strongly
- `sadly` = in a sad way
- `at once` = immediately
- `at any moment` = very soon
- `might as well` = may as well; not ideal, but acceptable

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a small ticketing comedy with clear reversals.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "They are late, the play may have started, and the ticket window is the
   only chance."
2. Bridge from physical ticket office to modern app ticketing.
3. Explain `sold out`, `return tickets`, and different `performances`.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: narrator and Susan hurry toward a theatre because the play may
     begin at any moment.
   - Frame 2: narrator asks at the ticket office and hears that tickets have
     sold out.
   - Frame 3: another man returns two tickets, and hope suddenly comes back.
   - Frame 4: the girl explains the tickets are for next Wednesday; narrator
     accepts them sadly.
5. Discussion: why the returned tickets are a false rescue.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `ticket`, `return`, `pity`.
8. Grammar: `may`, `may have`, `May I`, `Could I`, `might as well`.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals theatrical, lightly comic, and focused on ticketing.

- Use one consistent narrator, one consistent Susan, one ticket-office girl,
  and one returning man across frames.
- Suggested storyboard `visual_consistency`:
  `Keep the same four theatre-ticket characters across all frames. The narrator
  is a polite adult man in his early thirties, slim build, short brown hair,
  wearing a navy coat and looking increasingly worried. Susan is the same adult
  woman in her early thirties, shoulder-length dark hair, warm scarf, expressive
  face, disappointed but not angry. The ticket-office girl is a calm young woman
  behind a small theatre ticket window, neat hair, simple uniform jacket. The
  returning man is a hurried middle-aged man with a tan overcoat holding two
  plain blank tickets. Keep the old theatre entrance, warm evening light, and
  ticket-office window consistent; no readable text anywhere.`
- Show the theatre entrance and ticket office, but do not include readable
  signs, words, dates, or ticket text.
- The tickets can be blank slips or generic paper stubs.
- The mood should shift from urgency to disappointment to sudden hope to
  resigned sadness.
- Avoid making the ticket-office girl rude. She is polite and factual.
- Avoid over-modern app screens in the main storyboard; the modern app bridge
  can be in narration, not necessarily the retell images.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Narrator and Susan hurry toward a warmly lit theatre entrance.
2. Narrator at the ticket office hears that tickets are sold out; Susan looks
   disappointed nearby.
3. A hurried man returns two blank tickets at the same ticket office, and the
   narrator notices with sudden hope.
4. The ticket-office girl politely explains the tickets are for another
   performance; the narrator accepts them sadly.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about trying to buy tickets or reserve a
spot, then discovering a date/performance problem.

Good prompts:

- `你有没有买电影票、演出票或活动票时，发现 sold out？`
- `如果有人退票，你会不会马上去抢？你会检查日期和场次吗？`
- `用英语讲一个“差点买到，结果日期不对”的小故事。`

Expected scaffold:

`The tickets were sold out. Then someone returned two tickets. I hurried to buy them, but they were for ___. I might as well ___.`

Add one app-style version:

`I thought the tickets were for tonight, but they were for next Wednesday's performance.`

Keep the exercise practical. The student should learn to talk about tickets,
availability, dates, and disappointed choices.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the ticketing reversals and modal-verb tone.

Recommended sentences:

1. `The play may begin at any moment.`
   - Focus: `may begin`, `at any moment`
2. `It may have begun already.`
   - Focus: `may have begun`
3. `May I have two tickets please?`
   - Focus: polite `May I have`
4. `I'm sorry, we've sold out.`
   - Focus: `sold out`
5. `What a pity!`
   - Focus: emotion and intonation
6. `Can I return these two tickets?`
   - Focus: `return` as give back
7. `Could I have those two tickets please?`
   - Focus: polite `Could I have`
8. `They're for next Wednesday's performance.`
   - Focus: wrong date / wrong performance
9. `I might as well have them.`
   - Focus: resigned tone

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, and
9.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the reversals:

`may begin -> may have begun -> sold out -> return tickets -> next Wednesday -> might as well`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a full modal-verb table. Start from why each modal appears in
this story.

Suggested explanation:

`这课里 may / could / might 都不是为了背规则，而是在买票现场完成不同任务。The play may begin 是猜测“可能马上开始”。It may have begun already 是猜测“可能已经开始了”。May I have two tickets 和 Could I have those two tickets 是礼貌买票。I might as well have them 是最后没办法了，“虽然不是今晚的票，那也只好买下吧”。`

Use a compact display:

| Line | What it does |
| --- | --- |
| The play may begin at any moment. | Guess: maybe soon. |
| It may have begun already. | Guess: maybe it has already happened. |
| May I have two tickets? | Polite request. |
| Could I have those two tickets? | Polite request. |
| I might as well have them. | Resigned choice. |

Then connect to the story:

`The grammar follows the emotion: worry, polite asking, sudden hope, and finally
sad acceptance.`

## Tone

Urgent, lightly comic, and disappointed at the end.

The child should feel:

- "I understand what a ticket office is."
- "I know `sold out` means no tickets left."
- "I understand why returned tickets do not solve the problem."
- "I can hear that `I might as well have them` is not happy."
- "I can use may / could / might for different real-life jobs."

Avoid:

- making this a generic theatre etiquette lesson
- treating the returned tickets as a lucky happy ending
- skipping the date/performance mistake
- making `return` mean only "come back"
- overloading the grammar with every modal use in English

Lesson 19 production is successful only if:

- The script clearly frames the lesson as a ticketing reversal: sold out, return
  tickets, wrong performance.
- The modern bridge connects the old ticket office to app ticketing, event
  booking, date checks, and sold-out notices.
- `ticket office` is explained as a physical ticket counter.
- `sold out` is treated as a core phrase even though it is not one of the raw
  keyword entries.
- `return` is explained as returning/refunding tickets in this lesson.
- `performance` is explained as one showing of the play, so `next Wednesday's
  performance` is not tonight's show.
- The 4 retell frames show urgency, sold-out disappointment, returned-ticket
  hope, and wrong-date resignation.
- The storyboard includes `visual_consistency` for the narrator, Susan, the
  ticket-office girl, the returning man, and the theatre ticket window.
- The 3 formal vocab blocks are `ticket`, `return`, and `pity`.
- Supporting words include `sold out`, `ticket office`, `performance`,
  `hurry`, `exclaim`, `sadly`, `at once`, `at any moment`, and
  `might as well`.
- The grammar section teaches modal functions through story use: `may begin`,
  `may have begun`, `May I have`, `Could I have`, and `might as well`.
- The living-scene exercise asks the student to describe a ticket/booking
