## Production Warnings

This lesson has action, but it must not become a "drive into thieves" hero
fantasy.

- Do not encourage imitation.
  - Roy acts quickly in the story, but real children and ordinary people should
    not chase thieves, drive at people, or crash vehicles.
  - The modern safe response is: keep distance, remember details, tell an adult
    or security staff, and call the police.
- Keep the bus action controlled and not graphic.
  - Show Roy using the bus to block or damage the getaway car.
  - Avoid showing impact on bodies, injuries, or dangerous close-ups.
- Explain that this is an older action-story style.
  - It is vivid and memorable, but modern safety norms are stricter.
  - Today cameras, license plates, security staff, and police response are the
    normal way to handle theft.
- Keep the title as an emergency shout.
  - `Stop thief!` is not a full polite sentence.
  - It sounds like a short street cry: "捉贼！"
- Do not make the thieves glamorous or funny heroes.
  - The story's energy should come from sequence and consequence.
  - The ending is that both men are arrested.
- Teach `such a fright that...` through feeling and result, not just as a
  grammar formula.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a bus driver who sees a theft, reacts quickly, and helps
make the thieves' car easy for the police to recognize.

Roy Trenton used to drive a taxi. A short while ago, he became a bus driver and
he has not regretted it because he finds the new work far more exciting.
Recently, while he was driving along Catford Street, he saw two thieves rush out
of a shop and run toward a waiting car. One thief was carrying a bag full of
money. Roy acted quickly and drove the bus straight at the thieves. The thief
with the money got such a fright that he dropped the bag. As the thieves tried
to escape in their car, Roy drove his bus into the back of it. While the damaged
car moved away, Roy stopped his bus and called the police. Because the car was
badly damaged, it was easy to recognize. Shortly afterwards, the police stopped
the car and both men were arrested.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`taxi driver -> bus driver -> exciting work -> thieves rush out -> bag full of money -> Roy acts quickly -> thief drops bag -> getaway car damaged -> easy to recognize -> police arrest both men`

The ending should land as: "Roy's quick action made the thieves' car easy to
recognize, so the police could stop it shortly afterwards."

## Safety And Modern Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- `Stop thief!` is an emergency shout people might cry when a thief is running
  away.
- Roy is an adult driver in a story, not a model for children to copy.
- In real life, children should not chase a thief or run into traffic.
- If someone sees a theft today, the safer choices are:
  - move to a safe place
  - remember what the person and vehicle look like
  - tell an adult, shop staff, or security
  - call the police
  - let trained adults handle the danger
- Modern shops and streets often have cameras, license plates, security guards,
  traffic rules, and emergency services.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课很像一段街头动作镜头：两个小偷冲出商店，Roy 开着公交车正好看到。他行动很快，让小偷的车受损，后来警察就很容易认出来。但今天我们不能把这个当成现实建议。孩子看到危险时，不要追，不要撞，不要冒险，应该记住线索、远离危险、告诉大人并报警。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old action-story scene to present-day experiences:

- shop theft, school lost-and-found, or a person running away with something
- security cameras and license plates
- remembering details: clothes, direction, vehicle, color, number plate
- telling a teacher, parent, shop assistant, security guard, or police officer
- the difference between being brave and being unsafe
- public transport drivers having responsibility for passengers and traffic
- why a damaged car can be easy to recognize

The durable pattern is:

`sudden event -> quick observation -> visible clue -> police action -> consequence`

Do not turn this lesson into a crime-action game. Keep it as a story about
sequence, observation, and safe judgment.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is simultaneous actions with `when`, `as`, and `while`, plus
the result pattern `such a... that...`.

Teach these through the story:

- `When he was driving along Catford Street...`
  - Roy is driving; during that action, he sees the thieves.
- `As the thieves were trying to get away...`
  - They are trying to escape; during that same developing action, Roy hits the
    back of their car.
- `While the battered car was moving away...`
  - The car is moving away; during that action, Roy stops and calls the police.
- `such a fright that he dropped the bag`
  - The fright is so sudden and strong that a visible result happens: the bag
    drops.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson feels like a video because several actions happen at the same time.
`When`, `as`, and `while` help us put two moving actions together. Then
`such a fright that...` shows a strong feeling causing a visible result.

Use a compact board:

| Pattern | Story meaning |
| --- | --- |
| When he was driving... | during Roy's driving, he saw the thieves |
| As they were trying to get away... | during their escape attempt, Roy hit the car |
| While the battered car was moving away... | during its movement away, Roy called police |
| such a fright that... | the fright was so strong that the bag dropped |

Secondary language points:

- `used to drive`
  - Roy drove a taxi in the past, but his job changed.
- `has not regretted it`
  - He does not feel sorry about changing jobs.
- `far more exciting`
  - much more exciting.
- `straight at`
  - directly toward.
- `easy to recognize`
  - easy to know which car it is.
- `Shortly afterwards`
  - a short time later.
- passive voice in the ending:
  - `both men were arrested`.

## Teaching `Such A... That...` As Language Feel

Do not teach this only as `such + noun + that` versus `so + adjective + that`.
Start from the feeling of the scene.

Core sentence:

`The one with the money got such a fright that he dropped the bag.`

Student-facing feel:

`这一吓，吓得他包都掉了。`

Explain:

- `so frightened that he dropped the bag`
  - The camera is on the man's state: he was very frightened.
- `got such a fright that he dropped the bag`
  - The camera is on the sudden shock itself: that one fright was so strong
    that a result appeared.

Use this contrast:

| Sentence | Camera focus | Chinese feel |
| --- | --- | --- |
| He was so frightened that he dropped the bag. | his scared state | 他太害怕了，所以包掉了 |
| He got such a fright that he dropped the bag. | the sudden fright event | 这一吓可不得了，包都掉了 |

Practical pattern:

`such a + sudden feeling/event + that + visible result`

Examples for light practice:

- `He got such a fright that he dropped the bag.`
- `It was such a loud noise that everyone looked up.`
- `It was such a funny story that we all laughed.`
- `It was such a difficult question that nobody answered.`

Keep the explanation short and vivid. The goal is for the student to feel the
force of the sentence, not memorize a grammar table.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `rush`
   - Meaning: to move very quickly and suddenly.
   - Bridge: thieves do not simply walk out; they rush out because they are
     escaping.
   - Example: `He saw two thieves rush out of a shop.`
2. `fright`
   - Meaning: a sudden feeling of fear.
   - Bridge: `such a fright` means a sudden shock so strong that the thief drops
     the bag.
   - Example: `The one with the money got such a fright that he dropped the bag.`
3. `battered`
   - Meaning: damaged by being hit many times or hit hard.
   - Bridge: the thieves' car is battered, so it is easy for police to
     recognize.
   - Example: `While the battered car was moving away, Roy stopped his bus.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `Stop thief!` = emergency shout meaning "catch / stop the thief"
- `used to` = did in the past, but not now
- `taxi` = car that carries passengers for money
- `bus driver` = person who drives a bus
- `regret` = feel sorry about a choice
- `far more exciting` = much more exciting
- `Catford Street` = street name; do not over-explain geography
- `thief / thieves` = person / people who steal
- `waiting car` = car ready for the thieves to escape
- `bag full of money` = bag containing a lot of money
- `act quickly` = do something fast
- `straight at` = directly toward
- `drop` = let something fall
- `get away` = escape
- `back of it` = rear part of the car
- `telephoned the police` = called the police
- `badly damaged` = seriously damaged
- `recognize` = know or identify something
- `Shortly afterwards` = a short time later
- `arrested` = taken by police because of a crime

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into safe observation and
reporting.

Recommended scenario:

`You see someone run out of a shop with something that may not belong to them. You do not chase. You move to a safe place, remember details, and tell an adult or call the police.`

Student should practice:

- describing what they saw
- using action verbs like `rush`, `run towards`, and `carry`
- giving safe details: clothes, bag, car color, direction
- saying they should not chase
- asking an adult or police for help
- using `such...that` for a result, if natural

Useful expressions:

- `I saw two people rush out of the shop.`
- `One of them was carrying a bag.`
- `They ran towards a green car.`
- `I did not chase them.`
- `I moved to a safe place.`
- `The car was easy to recognize.`
- `We should tell an adult and call the police.`

The exercise should explicitly reward safe behavior, not direct confrontation.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to action timing and visible result.

Board structure:

`Moving actions`

- `When he was driving..., he saw...`
- `As the thieves were trying to get away..., Roy drove...`
- `While the battered car was moving away..., Roy stopped...`

`Strong cause -> visible result`

- `such a fright that he dropped the bag`

`So... that vs such a... that`

- `He was so frightened that he dropped the bag.`
  - Focus: how frightened he was.
- `He got such a fright that he dropped the bag.`
  - Focus: the sudden fright as an event.

`Comparison`

- `far more exciting`
  - much more exciting.

Use the final contrast:

`Fast action is exciting in the story.`

`Safe action in real life means observe, remember, and report.`

Avoid turning the grammar board into a long exam drill. It should make the
student feel the timing and result of the street scene.
