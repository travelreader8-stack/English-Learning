## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel cinematic and tense, but it must not teach unsafe
behavior.

- Add a safety boundary early.
  - Mrs. Sterling is brave, but running after two thieves is risky.
  - Today, if someone grabs a bag, phone, or wallet, a child should not chase.
  - The safe response is: protect yourself and children, shout for help, move to
    a safe place, call the police / security, and remember direction and
    features.
- Do not glorify direct confrontation.
  - The story ending is comic and lucky.
  - The courseware should not say or imply: "This is what you should do."
- Keep the two men non-specific and non-stereotyped.
  - They are thieves in the story, but avoid visual stereotypes.
  - Do not make the scene violent or frightening.
- Explain the picnic setting.
  - `at the edge of a forest` means near the forest boundary, not deep in the
    forest.
  - A modern child can connect this to a park picnic, country park, forest path,
    campsite, or outdoor family day.
- Make the action-chain writing point explicit.
  - This lesson is vivid because it shows actions one by one.
  - It does not merely say: "She got her handbag back."
  - It shows: rushed up -> strap broke -> ran through trees -> ran after them
    -> out of breath -> caught up -> ran straight at them -> dropped the bag.
- Explain `go through the contents`.
  - Here `go through` means search / look through, not pass through.
- Explain `needs mending`.
  - `mending` means repairing.
  - Today, `needs fixing` or `needs repairing` is more common in everyday
    speech.
- Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a sudden outdoor theft that becomes a fast action sequence.

Mrs. Anne Sterling is having a picnic with her children at the edge of a forest.
Two men rush up and try to steal her handbag. During the struggle, the strap
breaks. With the bag in their possession, the men run through the trees. Mrs.
Sterling is so angry that she runs after them. She becomes out of breath, but
continues running. When she catches up with them, she sees that they have sat
down and are going through the contents of the bag. She runs straight at them.
The men get such a fright that they drop the bag and run away. Later, she says
the strap needs mending, but nothing was stolen.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`picnic at forest edge -> men rush up -> handbag struggle -> strap breaks -> bag in their possession -> men run through trees -> Mrs. Sterling runs after them -> out of breath -> catches up -> men search contents -> she runs straight at them -> they drop bag -> strap needs mending`

The ending should land as:

`The bag was damaged, but nothing was stolen; the funny calm ending comes after a risky chase.`

## One-Sentence Hook

## Picnic At The Edge Of A Forest Bridge

Explain the setting:

- `the edge of a forest` means the boundary area between open space and trees.
- They are not deep in the forest. They are near the forest, which makes the

- Modern equivalents:
  - park picnic
  - country park
  - forest trail
  - campsite
  - outdoor family day

Suggested visual:

`picnic blanket / children / open grassy area -> forest edge -> thieves run into

The setting should begin calm and normal so the sudden action feels stronger.

## Action Chain Writing Bridge

This is the special writing insight for Lesson 44.

Teach:

Good story writing often shows actions in sequence instead of summarizing the
result.

Plain summary:

`Two men tried to steal her handbag, but she got it back.`

Scene writing:

`They rushed up -> the strap broke -> they ran through the trees -> she ran after them -> she was out of breath -> she caught up with them -> she ran straight at them -> they dropped the bag.`

Student-facing explanation:

`好故事不是先告诉你“她很勇敢”，而是让你看到她做了什么。课文没有直接说 Mrs. Sterling was brave，而是写她怎么追、怎么喘、怎么继续跑、怎么直冲过去。`

Use the label:

`Action Chain Writing / 动作链写作法`

Suggested board:

| Summary | Scene |
| --- | --- |
| She got her handbag back. | The strap broke. The men ran through the trees. Mrs. Sterling ran after them. She was out of breath, but she continued to run. |
| The thieves were frightened. | She ran straight at them. The men got such a fright that they dropped the bag and ran away. |

Writing principle:

`Show actions, not just results.`

This should become one of the production highlights, not a side note.

## Modern Bridge

Connect the story to present-day experiences:

- picnic in a park or forest area
- keeping bags, phones, and valuables safe outdoors
- sudden events in public places
- emotional reaction versus safe response
- how to describe a fast event vividly
- how to turn a short result into a scene with action verbs

The durable pattern is:

`calm scene -> sudden event -> broken object -> chase -> breathless effort -> confrontation -> recovery -> dry final comment`.

Do not make the lesson about crime prevention only. Safety matters, but the
literary value is the action chain.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar / structure is action sequencing plus `so...that` and
`such...that`.

### Action Verbs And Phrasal Verbs

Teach the movement chain:

- `rush up to her`
  - suddenly move quickly toward her.
- `run through the trees`
  - move among the trees.
- `run after them`
  - chase them.
- `catch up with them`
  - reach them after chasing.
- `run straight at them`
  - run directly toward them.
- `run away`
  - escape.

Student-facing explanation:

These are not all the same `run`. The story becomes cinematic because each verb
changes the camera:

`rush up` = sudden attack

`run through` = escape route

`run after` = chase

`catch up with` = closing the distance

`run straight at` = direct confrontation

`run away` = thieves escape

### Bag And Possession Chain

Teach the object chain:

- `tried to steal her handbag`
- `In the struggle`
- `the strap broke`
- `with the bag in their possession`
- `going through the contents of the bag`
- `dropped the bag`
- `The strap needs mending`

Key explanations:

- `possession` = having / holding something.
- `contents` = the things inside.
- `go through the contents` = search through the things inside the bag.
- `needs mending` = needs repairing.

### `so...that` And `such...that`

Use the two story sentences:

- `Mrs. Sterling got so angry that she ran after them.`
- `The men got such a fright that they dropped the bag and ran away.`

Teach the contrast:

| Pattern | Example | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `so + adjective + that` | `so angry that she ran after them` | the feeling was strong, so an action followed |
| `such + noun phrase + that` | `such a fright that they dropped the bag` | the fright was so great, so an action followed |

Student-facing explanation:

`so angry` describes how angry she was. `such a fright` names the thing they
felt. Both structures push the story into the next action.

Secondary language points:

- `risk she was taking`
  - danger she was accepting.
- `out of breath`
  - breathing hard, unable to breathe comfortably after running.
- `continued to run`
  - kept running.
- `straight at`
  - directly toward.
- `later`
  - after the event.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `risk`
   - Meaning: danger; the possibility that something bad may happen.
   - Bridge: Mrs. Sterling is brave but does not think of the risk she is
     taking.
   - Example: `Mrs. Anne Sterling did not think of the risk she was taking.`
2. `possession`
   - Meaning: having or holding something; something is with you or under your
     control.
   - Bridge: once the men have the bag in their possession, the chase begins.
   - Example: `With the bag in their possession, both men started running through the trees.`
3. `contents`
   - Meaning: the things inside a container, bag, book, or message.
   - Bridge: the men are searching the things inside the handbag.
   - Example: `They were going through the contents of the bag.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `forest` = a large area with many trees
- `picnic` = outdoor meal
- `edge` = border / side of an area
- `rush up to` = move quickly toward someone
- `steal` = take something that is not yours
- `handbag` = small bag, often carried by a woman
- `struggle` = fight / effort when people pull or resist
- `strap` = long narrow part used to hold or carry a bag
- `broke` = stopped being whole / snapped
- `run after` = chase
- `out of breath` = breathing hard after effort
- `continue` = keep doing something
- `catch up with` = reach someone after chasing
- `go through` = search / look through
- `run straight at` = run directly toward
- `fright` = sudden fear
- `drop` = let something fall
- `mend` / `mending` = repair / repairing

## `Needs Mending` Mini-Bridge

Explain the final line:

`The strap needs mending, but they did not steal anything.`

Meaning:

- The bag strap is broken and needs repair.
- Nothing inside the bag was stolen.
- The line is funny because after a risky chase, Mrs. Sterling comments calmly
  on the practical result: only the strap needs repair.

Modern language bridge:

- `needs mending` = old-fashioned / slightly British-feeling.
- `needs repairing` = more standard.
- `needs fixing` = more everyday.

Examples:

- `The strap needs mending.`
- `My bike needs repairing.`
- `My phone needs fixing.`

Keep this short and tied to the final dry humor.
