# Lesson 14 Plan - Do You Speak English?

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 14. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 14 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 14
- Title: `Do you speak English?`
- Chinese title: `你会讲英语吗？`
- Core bridge: `这不是一篇学法语的课，而是一个语言误判造成的尴尬故事：两个人其实都会英语，却因为在法国、又各自做了假设，沉默了一路。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson depends on a social misunderstanding. The production should make
the misunderstanding clear before teaching grammar.

- Do not present hitchhiking as a recommended modern behavior.
  - `asked me for a lift` should be explained as an older travel scene.
  - Add a safety-aware bridge: today a child should not get into a stranger's
    car or stop for strangers.
  - Modern equivalents can be taxi, ride-hailing, school pickup, asking for
    directions, or needing help in a foreign place.
- Do not make the lesson about learning French.
  - French is only the language environment that causes the narrator's wrong
    assumption.
  - The central issue is: "I guessed what language he spoke, and I guessed
    wrong."
- Keep the final question as the punchline.
  - `Do you speak English?` should feel like the moment the whole silent journey
    suddenly becomes funny.
- Explain `Neither of us spoke` carefully.
  - It is not only "we did not speak"; it emphasizes that neither person said
    anything.
  - This sentence carries the awkward silence.
- Student-facing display:
  - If the raw source punctuation around the final quote looks odd, normalize
    student-facing narration naturally without editing `web/data/lessons.json`
    unless explicitly asked.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a wrong language assumption.

The narrator is driving in the south of France. A young man waves to him and
asks for a lift. Because they are in France, the narrator says good morning in
French. The young man replies in French too. The narrator knows almost no
French, so he says nothing more. The young man also says nothing. They sit in
the car in silence until they have nearly reached the town. Then the young man
slowly asks, "Do you speak English?" The narrator soon learns that the young man
is English himself.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`foreign place -> language assumption -> polite French greeting -> silent car ride -> English question -> comic reveal`

The ending should land as: "The funny part is not that they could not speak.
The funny part is that they both could have spoken English all along."

## One-Sentence Hook

Two English speakers sat silently in a car in France because each person assumed
the other one could not speak English.

## Story Focus

The misunderstanding chain is:

1. The narrator had an amusing experience last year.
2. He left a small village in the south of France.
3. He drove on to the next town.
4. A young man waved to him.
5. The young man asked him for a lift.
6. The young man got into the car.
7. The narrator said good morning in French.
8. The young man replied in French.
9. The narrator knew only a few French words.
10. Neither of them spoke during the journey.
11. Near the town, the young man slowly asked, "Do you speak English?"
12. The narrator learnt that the young man was English himself.

The production should make the silence visible and slightly funny, not boring.

## Culture And Safety Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- The story happens in the south of France, so the narrator naturally expects
  people around him to speak French.
- `ask for a lift` means asking someone to take you in their car for part of a
  journey.
- In older travel stories, hitchhiking appears more often. Today, especially
  for children, this is not a behavior to copy.
- The modern equivalent is not "go hitchhiking"; it is "you are in a place where
  you do not know what language someone speaks."
- The real-life version could happen at an airport, hotel, restaurant, taxi,
  school trip, museum, or tourist site.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这篇课文里的 ask for a lift 是以前旅行故事里常见的搭便车场景。今天我们不会建议小孩子这样做。你只需要抓住真正的笑点：在法国这个环境里，作者以为对方说法语，对方也配合着说了一句法语，于是两个其实都会英语的人，硬是沉默了一路。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old scene to present-day experiences:

- using translation software before realizing the other person speaks English
  or Chinese
- meeting someone at an airport or hotel and guessing the wrong language
- a foreign student or tourist suddenly speaking a language you understand
- two people being polite but unsure what language to use
- a group chat or online game where someone stays silent because they assume
  others will not understand them

The durable pattern is:

`limited information -> quick assumption -> awkward silence -> one simple question changes everything`

Do not make the modern bridge too technology-heavy. Translation apps can appear
as a comparison, but the human pattern matters more.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is past perfect for earlier actions in a past story.

Teach it through the action order:

- `After I had left a small village..., I drove on...`
  - First: I left the village.
  - Then: I drove to the next town.
- `As soon as he had got into the car, I said good morning...`
  - First: he got into the car.
  - Then: I spoke.
- `I had nearly reached the town, when...`
  - First: we were almost at the town.
  - Then: he suddenly spoke.

Student-facing explanation:

`had done` helps you point to an earlier action inside a past story. It is like
placing a small "before that" flag in the sentence.

Keep the grammar compact. The story's punchline should remain central.

Secondary language point:

- `Neither of us spoke during the journey.`
  - Meaning: not me, not him.
  - Use this to show awkward silence.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `experience`
   - Meaning: something that happened to you and can be remembered or told.
   - Bridge: funny travel experience, school experience, embarrassing
     experience.
   - Example: `I had an amusing experience last year.`
2. `lift`
   - Meaning: a ride in someone's car; British English.
   - Bridge: `ask for a lift` = ask someone to take you somewhere by car.
   - Safety note: understand the phrase, do not copy the behavior.
   - Example: `He asked me for a lift.`
3. `language`
   - Meaning: a system people use to speak or write, such as English, French,
     or Chinese.
   - Bridge: `in the same language` explains why the narrator thought the
     French assumption was right.
   - Example: `He replied in the same language.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `amusing` = funny in a light way
- `wave` = wave your hand to get attention
- `reply` = answer
- `journey` = trip from one place to another
- `apart from` = except for
- `at all` = used for emphasis in negatives
- `neither of us` = neither person

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a quiet comic misunderstanding.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "Two English speakers sat in silence because both guessed wrong."
2. Explain the old context: south of France, driving, a man asks for a lift.
3. Add safety bridge: understand the phrase, do not copy hitchhiking.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: narrator leaves a small village in the south of France.
   - Frame 2: young man waves on the road and asks for a lift.
   - Frame 3: both sit in the car silently after a French greeting.
   - Frame 4: near town, the young man asks, "Do you speak English?"
5. Discussion: why the silence is funny only after the final question.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `experience`, `lift`, `language`.
8. Grammar: past perfect as "before that" in a past story.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep the visuals simple, sunny, and story-like.

- Small road in the south of France, village behind, road ahead.
- Friendly but cautious tone for the lift scene.
- Car interior with awkward silence; both characters look polite but unsure.
- Final frame should show the young man slowly asking the English question.
- Avoid making the story look like danger, crime, or thriller.
- Avoid romanticizing hitchhiking.
- Do not overdo French stereotypes; a village road and signs are enough.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Narrator driving away from a small French village.
2. Young man waving by the roadside.
3. Two people in the car, silent after the French greeting.
4. Near town, the passenger asks, "Do you speak English?"

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about wrong assumptions in language or
communication.

Good prompts:

- `你有没有以为别人听不懂你，结果发现他其实听得懂？`
- `如果你在机场、酒店或旅行中不确定别人会不会说英语，你可以怎么礼貌地问？`
- `你能写一个小场景：两个人因为误会语言而沉默，最后一句话解开误会吗？`

Expected scaffold:

`I thought ___ could only speak ___. So I said ___ in ___. Later, ___ asked me, "Do you speak ___?"`

Keep the exercise light and personal. It should not require real foreign travel
experience; school, online game, hotel, restaurant, or tourist scenarios are
enough.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the misunderstanding and action order.

Recommended sentences:

1. `I had an amusing experience last year.`
   - Focus: `amusing experience`
2. `After I had left a small village in the south of France, I drove on to the next town.`
   - Focus: past perfect + long place phrase
3. `On the way, a young man waved to me.`
   - Focus: short event sentence
4. `I stopped and he asked me for a lift.`
   - Focus: `asked me for a lift`
5. `As soon as he had got into the car, I said good morning to him in French and he replied in the same language.`
   - Focus: long action chain
6. `Apart from a few words, I do not know any French at all.`
   - Focus: `apart from`, `at all`
7. `Neither of us spoke during the journey.`
   - Focus: awkward silence
8. `Do you speak English?`
   - Focus: slow final question and punchline

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the suspense:

`left -> drove -> waved -> asked -> got into the car -> replied -> silence -> final question`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from tense terminology. Start from story order.

Suggested explanation:

`这篇课文有几个动作都发生在过去，但有些动作比另一些更早。had done 就像在过去故事里插一个小牌子：这个动作更早发生。`

Use a compact action-order display:

| Earlier action | Later action |
| --- | --- |
| had left the village | drove on to the next town |
| had got into the car | said good morning |
| had nearly reached the town | the young man spoke |

Then connect to the story:

`这些语法不是为了复杂，而是为了让故事顺序更清楚。真正的笑点还是最后一句：Do you speak English?`

## Tone

Light, warm, and slightly comic.

The narrator should not sound foolish or mean. The passenger should not sound
strange or suspicious. Both people made a natural assumption in a foreign
language environment.

The child should feel:

- "I understand why the narrator used French."
- "I can feel the awkward silence."
- "The final English question makes the whole story funny."
- "I can use one polite question to avoid this misunderstanding."

## Acceptance Criteria For Production

Lesson 14 production is successful only if:

- The script clearly frames the text as a language-assumption misunderstanding.
- The culture/safety bridge explains `ask for a lift` without recommending
  hitchhiking.
- The modern bridge connects to airports, hotels, tourist places, translation
  apps, or online scenarios where language assumptions happen.
- The 4 retell frames show village departure, roadside wave/lift, silent car
  ride, and final English question.
- The 3 formal vocab blocks are `experience`, `lift`, and `language`.
- Supporting words include `amusing`, `wave`, `reply`, `journey`, `apart from`,
  `at all`, and `neither of us`.
- The grammar section teaches past perfect through action order, not abstract
  terminology alone.
- The living-scene exercise asks the student to describe a small language
  misunderstanding.
- The final question `Do you speak English?` lands as the punchline.
- The outro includes the full practice order and the concrete read-aloud steps:
  `⓪ 跟读：听课文全文 -> 听原音 -> 录一句 -> 获取反馈`, then
  `① 生活场景`, `② 完形`, `③ 中译英`, `④ 英译中`, `⑤ 默写`.
