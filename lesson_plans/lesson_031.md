## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel human and lightly comic, not like a motivational speech.

- Do not make it a generic "work hard and become rich" lesson.
  - The story is about hard work, skill, growth, memory, and a warm punchline.
  - The final bicycle request is essential.
- Preserve the bicycle loop.
  - Early: Frank's job was to repair bicycles.
  - Ending: his wife asks him to repair their grandson's bicycle.
  - This loop is the emotional and comic center of the lesson.
- Explain `used to` carefully.
  - It means something was true or happened regularly in the past, but is not
    the normal situation now.
  - This is not the same as "use something to do something."
- Explain `workshop` versus `factory`.
  - A workshop is a small work space or small shop / small production place.
  - A factory is a larger production place with many workers.
  - The growth from workshop to factory should be visual.
- Explain `spare parts for aeroplanes`.
  - Do not turn this into an aviation lecture.
  - It means machine parts used to repair or build aeroplanes.
- Handle the number `seven hundred and twenty-eight`.
  - The exact number helps show scale.
  - Use it as a visual contrast with `two helpers`.
- Keep the wife and grandson scene warm.
  - The wife is not mocking him.
  - The joke is that even after a long road to success, family still remembers
    the simple skill he started with.

  the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a man looking back on his long road from a small shop to a
large factory, only to be brought gently back to his first skill.

Yesterday afternoon, Frank Hawkins was telling the narrator about his
experiences as a young man. Before he retired, Frank was the head of a very
large business company. But as a boy, he used to work in a small shop. His job
was to repair bicycles, and he used to work fourteen hours a day. He saved money
for years, and in 1958 he bought a small workshop of his own. In his twenties,
he used to make spare parts for aeroplanes. At that time he had two helpers. In
a few years, the small workshop had become a large factory which employed 728
people. Frank smiled as he remembered his hard early years and the long road to
success. Then his wife came in and asked him to repair their grandson's bicycle.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`young shop worker -> bicycle repair -> long hours -> saved money -> own workshop -> aeroplane parts -> two helpers -> large factory -> success memory -> grandson's bicycle`

The ending should land as: "Success did not erase where Frank began; the bicycle
skill comes back in family life."

## One-Sentence Hook

Frank built a factory with 728 workers, but his family still needed him for the
job he started with: repairing a bicycle.

## Culture And Work Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A `small shop` in this story can be a small repair shop.
- A `workshop` is a small place where practical work or making things happens.
- A `factory` is much larger and can employ many people.
- Repairing bicycles is a practical skill.
- Making spare parts for aeroplanes is more industrial and complex, but the
  story does not need technical aircraft detail.
- `retired` means no longer working full-time because one has reached an older
  age or finished a career.
- The final bicycle request is a warm joke, not an insult.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课不要只讲“努力就会成功”。更有意思的是 Frank 的人生闭环：小时候他在小店里修自行车，每天工作很久；后来他攒钱买了自己的 workshop，再做到生产飞机零件，最后工厂雇了 728 个人。可是故事最后，妻子进来让他修孙子的自行车。这个结尾不是打脸，而是很有人味：你最早学会的本事，成功以后也还在生活里有用。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old shop-to-factory story to present-day experiences:

- learning a basic skill first: fixing a bike, coding a small app, editing a
  video, making a model, cooking, drawing, or repairing something
- starting with a small project before building a larger team or company
- saving money or practicing for years before owning one's own tools / studio
- turning a hobby or skill into a workshop, studio, lab, channel, app, or
  business
- family still asking for the simple practical skill even after career success
- success as a long road, not one sudden moment

The durable pattern is:

`basic skill -> long practice -> small ownership -> larger work -> success -> skill returns in family life`

Do not turn the modern bridge into entrepreneurship propaganda. Keep it grounded
in skill, time, growth, and humility.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is `used to` for past habits and past states, plus past perfect
for a life change completed before a later point in the story.

Teach it through the story:

- `used to work in a small shop`
  - He often worked there in the past.
  - He does not do that now.
- `used to work fourteen hours a day`
  - This was his regular past routine.
- `used to make spare parts for aeroplanes`
  - This was something he did in his twenties.
- `had become`
  - `the small workshop had become a large factory`
  - By the time Frank looked back, the change had already happened.

Student-facing explanation:

`used to` helps us compare Frank's past life with his later life. Past Frank
worked in a small shop and repaired bicycles. Later Frank became the head of a
large company. `had become` shows that the small workshop changed into a large
factory before the moment being remembered.

Use a compact contrast:

| Past | Later |
| --- | --- |
| used to work in a small shop | head of a large company |
| used to repair bicycles | factory made aeroplane parts |
| two helpers | 728 employees |
| repair bicycles as work | repair grandson's bicycle as family help |

Secondary language points:

- `before he retired`
  - before he stopped full-time work.
- `of his own`
  - belonging to him.
- `in his twenties`
  - when he was between twenty and twenty-nine.
- `in a few years`
  - after only several years.
- `long road to success`
  - a metaphor for years of hard work and growth.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `workshop`
   - Meaning: a small place where things are made or repaired.
   - Bridge: bicycle repair workshop, maker space, small studio.
   - Example: `He bought a small workshop of his own.`
2. `employ`
   - Meaning: to give someone a job and pay them to work.
   - Bridge: a company employs workers; a school employs teachers.
   - Example: `The factory employed seven hundred and twenty-eight people.`
3. `retire`
   - Meaning: to stop working full-time after a long career, often because of
     age.
   - Bridge: grandparents or older workers may retire.
   - Example: `Before he retired, Frank was the head of a very large business company.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `success story` = story of someone's path to success
- `experience` = something that happened to a person and can be remembered
- `head of` = leader of
- `business company` = company doing business
- `used to` = did regularly in the past, not now
- `small shop` = small repair or work shop
- `repair` = fix
- `bicycle` = bike
- `save money` = keep money for later use
- `of his own` = belonging to him
- `in his twenties` = age twenty to twenty-nine
- `spare parts` = replacement parts
- `aeroplanes` = airplanes
- `helper` = person who helps with work
- `factory` = large place where things are made
- `grandson` = son of one's son or daughter

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like an elder telling a life story with a warm punchline.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "Frank's success story starts and ends with a bicycle."
2. Explain the bicycle loop before or just after the first retell, but keep the
   final joke alive.
3. Bridge `used to`, workshop/factory, and 728 employees.
4. Retell in 4 frames:

5. Discussion: why the ending is funny and warm.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `workshop`, `employ`, `retire`.

9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Four-Frame Storyboard Intent

Frame 1: Small Shop And Bicycle

- Young Frank works in a small repair shop.
- He repairs a bicycle.
- The image should imply long hours and humble beginnings without looking
  miserable.
- The visual anchor: bicycle repair.

Frame 2: Own Workshop

- Frank saves money and buys a small workshop of his own.
- Show simple tools, workbench, and a small place that belongs to him.
- The mood should be hopeful.
- The visual idea: "a first step of ownership."

Frame 3: Large Factory

- The workshop has become a large factory.
- Show spare parts for aeroplanes in a nontechnical way: metal parts, wings or
  aircraft-related components in the background.
- Show two helpers / workers growing into a larger team.
- The visual contrast: small beginning to large scale.

Frame 4: Grandson's Bicycle

- Older Frank smiles while remembering his success.
- His wife enters with or points to a child's bicycle.
- The mood is warm and funny.
- The image should clearly echo frame 1.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the story into a personal growth and
skill-return scene.

Recommended scenario:

`Someone learned a simple skill when young, used it to build something bigger, and later still uses the same skill to help family or friends.`

Student should practice:

- talking about a past habit with `used to`
- describing a skill that grew over time
- explaining a small beginning and a later result
- using `of his own`, `in a few years`, and `had become`
- ending with a warm practical request

Useful expressions:

- `He used to...`
- `It was his job to...`
- `He saved money for years.`
- `He bought a small workshop of his own.`
- `In a few years, it had become...`
- `But his family still asked him to...`

The exercise should not require the child to invent a business empire. A small
skill-to-growth story is enough.

## Grammar Board
