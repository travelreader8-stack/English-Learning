## Basic Info

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be a light misunderstanding story, not a theft or danger
story.

- Explain `pub` carefully.
  - Do not translate it simply as a modern city bar or nightclub.
  - In this lesson, a `village pub` is a small British countryside eating and
    drinking place, and often a community meeting place.
- Explain `landlord` carefully.
  - Here it does not mean a rental landlord.
  - It means the man who runs the pub.
- Explain `bill` as a meal bill.
  - It is the money the narrator must pay after lunch.
- Keep the dog playful, not criminal or frightening.
  - The dog has carried the bag into the garden.
  - The landlord knows this habit, so he smiles and goes to get the bag.
- Do not make the narrator look dishonest.
  - He cannot pay because he cannot find his bag, not because he wants to avoid
    paying.
- Treat obvious source-text errors responsibly.
  - The raw `web/data/lessons.json` text currently has `My dog had taken in into
    the garden.`
  - Student-facing script, read-aloud data, storyboard narration, and exercise
    text should normalize this to `My dog had taken it into the garden.`
  - Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production unless
    the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.
- The raw quote punctuation around `Did you have a good meal?` may also look
  uneven in the source. Normalize student-facing narration if needed without
  changing shared data.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a small panic that turns into a familiar household joke.

The narrator has lunch at a village pub. After lunch, he looks for his bag. He
had left it on a chair beside the door, but now it is gone. This matters because
he cannot pay the bill without his bag. When the landlord hears this, he smiles,
goes out, and soon returns with the bag. The bag was not stolen. The landlord's
dog had taken it into the garden. The final line explains the whole story:
`He often does this!`

The courseware should make the student understand:

`lunch at village pub -> bag missing -> cannot pay the bill -> awkward worry -> landlord smiles -> dog habit -> light ending`

The ending should land as: "The narrator thought this was a serious problem,
but for the landlord it was a familiar little trouble."

## One-Sentence Hook

He could not pay for lunch because his bag had disappeared, but the pub owner
already knew exactly who the usual suspect was.

## Story Focus

The misunderstanding chain is:

1. The narrator finishes lunch at a village pub.
2. He looks for his bag.
3. He remembers that he had left it on a chair beside the door.
4. Now the bag is not there.
5. The landlord comes in and asks about the meal.
6. The narrator says the meal was good, but he cannot pay the bill.
7. He explains that he has not got his bag.
8. The landlord smiles and goes out.
9. He returns with the bag.
10. The dog had taken it into the garden.
11. The title works because the dog often does this.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`customer panic` versus `landlord's calm familiarity`.

## Culture And Scene Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A British `village pub` is a small countryside place where people can eat,
  drink, talk, and meet neighbours.
- A pub is not the same as a modern nightclub.
- The `landlord` in a pub is the owner or manager of the pub.
- The `bill` is the money you have to pay after a meal.
- In a small village pub, the owner may know the place, the garden, and even the
  dog's habits very well.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`中国小朋友看到 pub，可能会以为是城市里的酒吧。这里更像英国乡村里的小餐馆加社区空间：可以吃午饭，老板也认识这里的人和动物。这个故事的关键不是喝酒，而是吃完饭要付账，包却不见了。客人紧张，老板却笑了，因为他知道：这只狗又把东西叼到花园里去了。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old pub scene to present-day experiences:

- eating in a small restaurant and suddenly not finding your bag or phone
- leaving a backpack beside a door, then finding it moved by someone else
- a family dog carrying shoes, socks, or schoolbags to the balcony or garden
- a younger sibling moving your things and forgetting to say so
- a shop owner who knows a regular little problem in the shop
- an awkward moment when you need to pay but cannot find the thing you need

The durable pattern is:

`missing thing -> worry -> explanation -> familiar habit -> relief`

Do not turn the lesson into a pet-training lecture. Keep it as a light story
about embarrassment, relief, and a funny repeated habit.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is past perfect as "the earlier past".

Teach it through the story timeline:

- `After I had had lunch...`
  - Earlier: I had lunch.
  - Later: I looked for my bag.
  - Explain the double `had`: the first `had` helps form past perfect; the
    second `had` means "eat / have lunch".
- `I had left it on a chair beside the door...`
  - Earlier: I left the bag on the chair.
  - Later: I could not find it.
- `My dog had taken it into the garden.`
  - Earlier: the dog took the bag into the garden.
  - Later: the landlord brought it back.

Student-facing explanation:

`had done` often tells us what happened before another past moment. In this
story, the narrator is already in the past, and he looks back to an even earlier
action: he had had lunch, he had left the bag, and the dog had taken it.

Use a compact timeline:

| Earlier past | Later past |
| --- | --- |
| I had had lunch | I looked for my bag |
| I had left it on a chair | it wasn't there |
| the dog had taken it into the garden | the landlord returned with it |

Secondary language points:

- `haven't got`
  - British-style everyday English for `do not have`.
  - In this lesson: `I haven't got my bag` = I do not have my bag with me.
- `give it back to me`
  - Meaning: return it to me.
  - Good for daily object-return scenes.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `pub`
   - Meaning in this lesson: a small British countryside place where people can
     eat, drink, and meet.
   - Bridge: not a nightclub; closer to a village restaurant/community place in
     this story.
   - Example: `at a village pub`
2. `landlord`
   - Meaning in this lesson: the owner or manager of the pub.
   - Bridge: not a rental-house landlord here.
   - Example: `the landlord came in`
3. `bill`
   - Meaning: the amount of money you must pay after a meal or service.
   - Bridge: restaurant bill, phone bill, electricity bill; here it is the meal
     bill.
   - Example: `I can't pay the bill.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `bag` = a thing you carry belongings in
- `chair` = seat
- `beside the door` = next to the door
- `look for` = try to find
- `meal` = food eaten at breakfast/lunch/dinner
- `immediately` = right away
- `in a few minutes` = after a short time
- `return` = come back
- `give it back to me` = return it to me
- `garden` = yard/garden area
- `take it into` = carry or move it into
- `haven't got` = do not have
- `often` = many times / repeatedly

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a small countryside comedy.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "The meal is finished, the bill should be paid, but the bag is gone."
2. Bridge from `village pub` to a small restaurant/community place.
3. Explain why `landlord` is the pub owner and why `bill` means meal bill.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: narrator finishes lunch at a warm village pub and leaves his bag
     on a chair beside the door.
   - Frame 2: narrator searches for the missing bag and realizes he cannot pay
     the bill.
   - Frame 3: landlord smiles because he knows the likely cause and walks out
     toward the garden.
   - Frame 4: landlord returns with the bag; the dog sits nearby as the familiar
     culprit.
5. Discussion: why the landlord smiles instead of panicking.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `pub`, `landlord`, `bill`.
8. Grammar: past perfect as earlier-past timeline.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals cozy, rural, and lightly funny.

- Use one consistent narrator, one consistent landlord, and one consistent dog.
- Suggested storyboard `visual_consistency`:
  `Keep the same three subjects across all four frames. The narrator is a polite
  adult traveler in his early thirties, slim build, short dark-brown hair,
  wearing a light shirt, muted green jacket, and carrying a small brown leather
  bag. The landlord is the same friendly middle-aged British pub owner with a
  warm round face, greying hair, white shirt, dark waistcoat, and calm amused
  expression. The dog is the same medium-sized golden-brown dog with floppy
  ears and playful eyes. Keep the village pub warm, wooden, cozy, and rural;
  only move the action from pub door to garden and back.`
- The pub should look like a small countryside eating place, not a modern bar or
  nightclub.
- The missing bag should be visible in frame 1 and frame 4, and absent in the
  middle worry moment.
- The dog should look playful, not aggressive.
- Avoid signs, readable menus, labels, or written bills in the image.
- Avoid police, theft, dark suspense, or scary lost-property imagery.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Inside a cozy village pub after lunch: narrator's brown bag rests on a chair
   beside the door.
2. Narrator looks worried near the chair and cannot find the bag while the meal
   bill moment is implied without readable text.
3. The friendly landlord smiles knowingly and steps toward a garden door.
4. The landlord gives the bag back to the narrator while the playful dog sits
   nearby in the garden doorway.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about something that looked lost but was
only moved by a familiar person or pet.

Good prompts:

- `你有没有把书包、笔袋、手机或鞋放在一个地方，后来发现被家里的宠物、弟弟妹妹或同学拿走了？`
- `如果你在餐厅或教室里突然找不到东西，你会怎么解释？`
- `用 had done 说清楚：你之前把东西放在哪里，后来发生了什么。`

Expected scaffold:

`I had left my ___ on ___. Then I couldn't find it. Later, I found out that ___ had taken it to ___. He/She often does this.`

Add one restaurant-style option:

`I couldn't pay the bill because I hadn't got my ___.`

Keep the exercise grounded in daily life. The point is to describe the earlier
action and the later discovery clearly.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the missing-bag timeline and the final reveal.

Recommended sentences:

1. `After I had had lunch at a village pub, I looked for my bag.`
   - Focus: double `had`, `village pub`
2. `I had left it on a chair beside the door and now it wasn't there!`
   - Focus: `had left it`, `beside the door`
3. `As I was looking for it, the landlord came in.`
   - Focus: `was looking for`, `landlord`
4. `Did you have a good meal?`
   - Focus: polite restaurant/pub question
5. `I can't pay the bill.`
   - Focus: `pay the bill`
6. `I haven't got my bag.`
   - Focus: British `haven't got`
7. `In a few minutes he returned with my bag and gave it back to me.`
   - Focus: `returned with`, `gave it back`
8. `My dog had taken it into the garden.`
   - Focus: corrected `it`, `had taken`
9. `He often does this!`
   - Focus: punchline, repeated habit

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 1, 2, 5, 6, 8, and
9.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the timeline:

`had had lunch -> had left the bag -> bag wasn't there -> couldn't pay -> dog had taken it -> often does this`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a full tense table. Start from the missing-bag timeline.

Suggested explanation:

`这篇课文里一直在回头看更早发生的事。故事本身已经发生在过去：I looked for my bag。可是找包之前，他已经吃完饭了：I had had lunch。包不见之前，他已经把包放在椅子上了：I had left it on a chair。老板拿回包之前，狗已经把包叼到花园里了：My dog had taken it into the garden。`

Use a compact display:

| What happened first? | What happened later? |
| --- | --- |
| I had had lunch | I looked for my bag |
| I had left it on a chair | it wasn't there |
| the dog had taken it into the garden | the landlord returned with it |

Then explain the funny ending:

`He often does this` is simple present because it is not only one past action.
It is the dog's regular habit.

## Tone

Cozy, lightly embarrassed, and funny.

The child should feel:

- "I understand what a village pub is in this story."
- "I know `landlord` here is the pub owner."
- "I can see why the narrator is embarrassed."
- "I can follow earlier-past versus later-past events."
- "The ending is funny because the dog does this again and again."

Avoid:

- treating `pub` as adult nightlife
- making the dog a thief
- making the narrator look dishonest
- overexplaining British pub culture
- leaving the source typo `taken in into` in student-facing material
