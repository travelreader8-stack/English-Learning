## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a country preparing for its first Olympic Games by building
a new sports-and-transport area on a four-year timeline.

The Olympic Games will be held in the country in four years' time. Because many
people will visit the country, the government will be building hotels, an
immense stadium, an Olympic-standard swimming pool, new roads, and a special
railway line. The Games will be held just outside the capital, and the whole
area will be called `Olympic City`. Workers will have completed the new roads
by the end of this year. By the end of next year, they will have finished work
on the new stadium. The modern buildings have been designed by Kurt Gunter.
Everybody will be watching anxiously as the buildings go up. People are excited
and looking forward to the Olympic Games because the Games have never been held
in this country before.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`four years' time -> many visitors -> government builds hotels / stadium / pool -> roads / railway -> Olympic City -> roads completed this year -> stadium finished next year -> designed buildings -> people watch anxiously -> first Olympic Games in the country`

The ending should land as: "A huge future event is not just an announcement. It
becomes a city project with buildings, transport, deadlines, and public
expectation."

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is future forms in a project schedule.

Teach these through the story:

- `will be held`
  - Passive future: the Games will happen in this country.
- `will be visiting`
  - Future-in-progress: many people will be visiting during the Games period.
- `will be building`
  - Future-in-progress: the government will be in the process of building many
    things.
- `will have completed`
  - Future perfect: by the end of this year, the roads will already be
    completed.
- `will have finished`
  - Future perfect: by the end of next year, the stadium work will already be
    finished.
- `have been designed`
  - Present perfect passive: the buildings already have a designer.
- `have never been held before`
  - Present perfect passive: from the past until now, this has never happened in
    the country.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson talks like a project manager. Some sentences tell us what will
happen in the future. Some tell us what people will be doing during a future
period. Some tell us what will already be finished by a deadline. The grammar
matches the construction schedule.

Use a compact board:

| Form | Example | Project meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| will be held | The Games will be held... | future event |
| will be building | the government will be building... | future work in progress |
| will have completed | Workers will have completed... | finished before a future deadline |
| will have finished | they will have finished... | finished before next year's deadline |
| have been designed | buildings have been designed... | already designed |
| have never been held | never been held before | first time in this country |

Secondary language points:

- `in four years' time`
  - four years from now.
- `a great many people`
  - many people; a large number of people.
- `just outside the capital`
  - near but not inside the capital city.
- `the whole area`
  - the complete place / district.
- `as the new buildings go up`
  - as the buildings are being built.
- `look forward to`
  - feel happy and excited about something in the future.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `government`
   - Meaning: the group of people / system that runs a country or city and
     makes public decisions.
   - Bridge: the government can organize roads, railways, stadiums, and public
     projects.
   - Example: `The government will be building new hotels and an immense stadium.`
2. `stadium`
   - Meaning: a large sports building or open structure where many people watch
     events.
   - Bridge: Olympic stadium, football stadium, school sports field at a much
     larger scale.
   - Example: `They will be building an immense stadium.`
3. `design`
   - Meaning: to plan how a building or object will look and work before it is
     made.
   - Bridge: an architect designs buildings; a designer plans the shape and
     function before workers build.
   - Example: `The fantastic modern buildings have been designed by Kurt Gunter.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `Olympic Games` = large international sports event
- `hold / be held` = organize / take place
- `in four years' time` = four years from now
- `a great many` = a very large number of
- `visitor` = person who comes to a place
- `immense` = very large
- `Olympic-standard` = meeting Olympic requirements
- `swimming pool` = pool used for swimming competitions
- `railway line` = train route / track
- `capital` = capital city of a country
- `whole area` = complete district
- `Olympic City` = area built for the Games
- `complete` = finish
- `by the end of` = before or at the final point of a time period
- `fantastic` = very impressive
- `modern buildings` = new-style buildings
- `go up` = be built / rise during construction
- `anxiously` = with worry or nervous attention
- `look forward to` = happily expect
- `never been held before` = has not happened before

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into planning a large
school or community event.

Recommended scenario:

`Your school will hold a sports day, science fair, concert, or open day in several months. Many visitors will come. You explain what will be built, prepared, finished by certain dates, and why everyone is looking forward to it.`

Student should practice:

- describing a future event
- explaining why many visitors need preparation
- using `will be building / preparing`
- using `will have completed / finished by...`
- naming places and facilities
- saying people are looking forward to it

Useful expressions:

- `The sports day will be held next month.`
- `A great many people will be visiting our school.`
- `We will be preparing new signs and activity areas.`
- `The workers will have finished the stage by Friday.`
- `By the end of next week, we will have completed the plan.`
- `Everybody is looking forward to it.`

Keep the scene practical and age-appropriate. It should make future perfect
feel like a deadline, not an abstract tense.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to project deadlines.

Board structure:

`Future event`

- `will be held`
- `will be called`

`Future work in progress`

- `will be visiting`
- `will be building`
- `will be watching`

`Future deadline`

- `will have completed ... by the end of this year`
- `will have finished ... by the end of next year`

`Already / never before`

- `have been designed`
- `have never been held before`

Use the final contrast:

`The Games are four years away.`

`But the city must be built step by step before then.`

Avoid teaching future perfect as a detached rule. Show it as a project deadline:
by this date, the work will already be done.
