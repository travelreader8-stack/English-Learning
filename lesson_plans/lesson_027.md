## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about outdoor experience and reading the environment, not
about encouraging unsafe camping.

- Do not romanticize free camping in an open field.
  - In the story, the boys put up a tent in the middle of a field.
  - In modern life, children should camp with adults, in safe and permitted
    places, and check weather and ground conditions.
- Treat `open fire` and `campfire` with a safety bridge.
  - The original story has the boys cooking over an open fire and singing by
    the campfire.
  - Student-facing narration should explain that real camping today must follow
    campsite rules, fire bans, and adult supervision.
- Keep the story adventurous but not dangerous.
  - The boys are surprised and wet, not seriously injured or in disaster.
  - The tone should be a memorable outdoor lesson, not a flood emergency.
- Explain the real cause of the problem.
  - The rain is not the whole problem.
  - The tent was placed where rainwater could form a stream and flow under it.
- Explain `stream had formed`.
  - A temporary stream can appear when heavy rainwater gathers and follows low
    ground.
- Explain `wound its way`.
  - `wound` here is the past tense of `wind`, meaning moved in a curving path.
  - It is not the noun `wound` meaning an injury.
- Explain `right under`.
  - `right` here means exactly / directly, not correct.

  the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a happy camping evening that turns into a wet surprise
because the boys did not understand the ground and rainwater.

Late in the afternoon, the boys put up their tent in the middle of a field.
After that, they cook a meal over an open fire. The food smells good, and after
the meal they tell stories and sing songs by the campfire. Later, it begins to
rain. The boys feel tired, so they put out the fire and creep into their tent.
Their sleeping bags are warm and comfortable, so they sleep soundly. In the
middle of the night, two boys wake up and shout because the tent is full of
water. They leap out of their sleeping bags and hurry outside. It is raining
heavily, and they discover that a stream has formed in the field. The stream
winds across the field and flows right under their tent.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`happy camp -> rain starts -> safe fire out -> deep sleep -> water in tent -> temporary stream -> bad tent location`

The ending should land as: "The wet night happened because water found the low
path, and their tent was on that path."

## One-Sentence Hook

The boys thought they had chosen a good camping spot, but the rain turned the
field into a stream under their tent.

## Culture And Safety Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- Camping means sleeping outside in a tent.
- Older adventure stories often show children camping in fields and cooking
  over open fires.
- Modern camping normally happens in a campsite, park, or controlled outdoor
  area with adults and rules.
- An open fire or campfire can be dangerous, so today it needs permission,
  safety equipment, and adult supervision.
- A field may look flat, but rainwater still finds lower ground.
- A tent should not be placed in a hollow, ditch, stream path, or place where
  water can collect.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课不是要鼓励孩子自己跑到野地里露营。我们要抓住真正的户外知识：帐篷不是随便找一块空地就能搭。下雨时，水会往低处走，可能临时形成一条小溪。如果帐篷正好搭在水流经过的位置，再暖和的睡袋也没用了。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the camping story to present-day experiences:

- school camping trip or scout-style outdoor activity
- family camping in a campsite
- outdoor picnic where weather suddenly changes
- playground, sports field, or parking lot collecting water after rain
- hiking or walking after rain and seeing water channels on the ground
- checking weather forecast before outdoor activities
- choosing a tent spot that is higher, flatter, and away from water paths
- turning off a stove or fire safely before sleeping

The durable pattern is:

`fun plan -> weather changes -> natural rule appears -> preparation matters`

Do not make the modern bridge too technical. The child should remember a simple
idea: water moves, and outdoor plans must respect that.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is time sequence in a past story.

Teach it through the story:

- `Late in the afternoon`
  - Sets the opening time.
- `As soon as this was done`
  - Immediately after putting up the tent, they cooked.
- `After a wonderful meal`
  - The next activity begins after eating.
- `some time later`
  - A later change: rain begins.
- `In the middle of the night`
  - The surprise happens during the night.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson is like a short film. The time phrases tell us when each scene
happens. If the student follows the time words, the story becomes easy to
retell.

Use a compact timeline:

| Time phrase | Event |
| --- | --- |
| Late in the afternoon | put up the tent |
| As soon as this was done | cooked a meal |
| After a wonderful meal | told stories and sang songs |
| some time later | it began to rain |
| In the middle of the night | the tent was full of water |

Secondary language points:

- `put up` versus `put out`
  - `put up a tent` = set up a tent
  - `put out the fire` = make the fire stop burning
- `so`
  - Shows result: they were tired, so they went into the tent.
- `found that...`
  - Used when they discovered the real reason.
- `right under`
  - Exactly under, not "correct under."

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `tent`
   - Meaning: a portable shelter made of cloth or similar material, used for
     camping.
   - Bridge: camping tent, school camping trip, family campsite.
   - Example: `The boys put up their tent in the middle of a field.`
2. `campfire`
   - Meaning: a fire made outdoors at a campsite for warmth, cooking, or
     gathering.
   - Safety bridge: today it must follow campsite rules and adult supervision.
   - Example: `They told stories and sang songs by the campfire.`
3. `stream`
   - Meaning: a small flow of water.
   - Bridge: after heavy rain, water can form a temporary stream across a field
     or road.
   - Example: `A stream had formed in the field.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `field` = open area of land, often with grass or crops
- `put up` = set up
- `open fire` = outdoor fire, safety-sensitive
- `smelled good` = had a good smell
- `wonderful meal` = very good meal
- `put out` = extinguish, stop a fire
- `crept` = moved quietly or carefully; past tense of creep
- `sleeping bag` = warm bag for sleeping outdoors
- `comfortable` = making you feel relaxed and good
- `soundly` = deeply, in a strong sleep
- `leapt` = jumped suddenly; past tense of leap
- `hurried` = went quickly
- `heavily` = strongly, a lot
- `formed` = came into shape / appeared
- `wound its way` = moved in a curving path
- `right under` = directly under

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like an outdoor story with a clear final reveal.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "Their tent was warm and comfortable, but it was in the wrong place."
2. Bridge camping and field camping to modern safe camping.
3. Explain fire safety for `open fire` and `campfire` briefly.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: late afternoon, boys put up a tent in the middle of a field.
   - Frame 2: open fire / campfire meal, stories and songs, warm happy mood.
   - Frame 3: rain begins; boys put out the fire and sleep soundly in warm
     sleeping bags.
   - Frame 4: midnight surprise: tent full of water; outside, stream flows
     right under the tent.
5. Discussion: why the tent spot was the real problem.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `tent`, `campfire`, `stream`.
8. Grammar: time sequence phrases and `put up` / `put out`.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Four-Frame Storyboard Intent

Frame 1: Tent In The Field

- Late afternoon.
- The boys put up a green tent in the middle of a grassy field.
- The sky is mostly calm, perhaps with clouds in the distance.
- The visual question: "Is this really the best place for a tent?"

Frame 2: Campfire Evening

- The boys cook or eat a simple meal near a small controlled campfire.
- They tell stories and sing songs.
- The mood is warm, comfortable, and happy.
- Keep fire safety visually calm and supervised.

Frame 3: Rain And Sleep

- Rain begins later.
- The fire is out.
- The boys creep into the tent and sleep in sleeping bags.
- The tent looks warm inside, but rainwater outside begins to collect.

Frame 4: Stream Under The Tent

- In the middle of the night, the boys leap out because the tent is wet.
- A shallow stream winds across the field.
- The water flows directly under the tent.
- The image should make the final sentence instantly understandable.

## Read-Aloud And Pronunciation Direction

Production should preserve the existing read-aloud structure:

- First listen to the full passage.
- Then listen to the original sentence.
- Then record one sentence.
- Then receive feedback.

This lesson has strong rhythm because of the time sequence. Feedback should help
the student group phrases naturally instead of reading word by word.

Useful sentence groups for read-aloud focus:

- `Late in the afternoon, the boys put up their tent in the middle of a field.`
- `As soon as this was done, they cooked a meal over an open fire.`
- `After a wonderful meal, they told stories and sang songs by the campfire.`
- `But some time later it began to rain.`
- `The boys felt tired so they put out the fire and crept into their tent.`
- `Their sleeping bags were warm and comfortable, so they all slept soundly.`
- `The tent was full of water!`
- `It was raining heavily and they found that a stream had formed in the field.`
- `The stream wound its way across the field and then flowed right under their tent!`

For feedback tone:

- Praise clear time phrases.
- Encourage a small pause after long opening phrases.
- Practice stress on `full of water`, `raining heavily`, and `right under`.
- Explain that `wound its way` is read like the past of `wind`, not the noun
  `wound`.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the story into a modern outdoor
planning scene.

Recommended scenario:

`You are going camping or having an outdoor picnic. It may rain. You need to choose a safe place and explain why.`

Student should practice:

- choosing a campsite or picnic spot
- explaining weather risk
- saying water flows to lower ground
- warning someone politely
- using time-sequence words to retell what happened

Useful expressions:

- `We should not put the tent here.`
- `This place is too low.`
- `If it rains heavily, water may flow here.`
- `Let's move the tent to higher ground.`
- `We must put out the fire before we sleep.`
- `A stream may form after heavy rain.`

The exercise should feel practical and calm, not frightening.

## Suggested You Too Scene

Scene:

`Your family is choosing a place for a tent at a campsite. The ground looks flat, but you notice a low path where rainwater may flow.`

Prompt ideas:

- `你看到一块地很空，但它好像比旁边低一点。你会怎么提醒大家？`
- `如果晚上可能下大雨，你会建议把帐篷移到哪里？`
- `你怎样解释 water flows to lower ground？`

Target output pattern:

`I don't think we should put the tent here. This place is too low. If it rains heavily, water may flow under the tent. Let's move it to higher ground.`

Keep the focus on judgment and explanation.
