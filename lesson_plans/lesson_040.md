## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a polite conversation attempt at a formal dinner that fails
comically.

At a dinner party, the hostess asks the narrator to sit next to Mrs. Rumbold.
Mrs. Rumbold does not look up. Her eyes are fixed on her plate, and she soon
starts eating. The narrator tries to make conversation. He asks about a new play
at `The Globe`, about holidays abroad, and about staying in England. Mrs.
Rumbold answers `No` each time. In despair, he asks whether she is enjoying her
dinner. She finally speaks in a complete sentence: if he ate more and talked
less, they would both enjoy their dinner.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`formal dinner -> hostess arranges seat -> narrator sits beside Mrs. Rumbold -> she only wants to eat -> narrator tries small talk -> three polite future questions -> three short No answers -> despair -> sharp final reply`

The ending should land as:

`He thought conversation would make dinner better. She thought silence would
make dinner better.`

## Dinner Party And Small Talk Bridge

This bridge should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A `dinner party` is not just a family meal. It is a meal with invited guests.
- The `hostess` may arrange seats so guests sit beside people they do not know.
- In that situation, people often try to `make conversation`.
- `Make conversation` means finding light, polite topics so the meal does not
  feel awkward.
- Common safe topics include plays, holidays, travel, school events, sports, or
  shared activities.
- In this story, the narrator chooses polite topics, but Mrs. Rumbold gives only
  very short answers.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课里的 dinner party 不是随便吃晚饭，而是比较正式的聚餐。女主人安排座位，你可能会坐在一个不熟的人旁边。这个时候英语里有一个很重要的动作，叫 make conversation，也就是礼貌地找话题。它不是审问，也不是考试，而是把一个轻一点的话题递给对方，看对方愿不愿意接。`

## Why `Will You Be Doing...?` Feels Polite

This is the key language insight for Lesson 40.

Teach the form:

`will + be + verb-ing`

Examples from the text:

- `Will you be seeing it?`
- `Will you be spending your holidays abroad this year?`
- `Will you be staying in England?`

Explain the tone:

- `Will you do...?` can sound more direct: `Will you see it?`
- `Will you be doing...?` asks about a future arrangement: `Will that be part
  of your plans?`
- It does not push the other person to decide in front of you.
- It sounds like a light social question, so it is useful for dinner-table
  small talk.

Student-facing explanation:

`加上 be doing 以后，问题不是硬问“你到底做不做”，而是问“到时候你是不是会有这个安排”。英语里的礼貌，很多时候就来自这种不把问题直接压到对方面前的说法。`

Use a compact contrast board:

| More direct | Softer small-talk question |
| --- | --- |
| Will you see it? | Will you be seeing it? |
| Will you spend your holidays abroad? | Will you be spending your holidays abroad? |
| Will you stay in England? | Will you be staying in England? |

Add one important nuance:

`Will you be seeing it?` here means `Will you go to see the play?`, not `Will
your eyes see it?`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old dinner-party scene to a teenager's present and future life:

- sitting beside someone unfamiliar at a meal, camp, school event, competition,
  host family dinner, or parents' friends' gathering
- opening a light topic politely
- noticing whether the other person wants to continue
- understanding short answers like `No` as a sign that the topic may not be
  working
- using `Will you be doing...?` to ask about future plans softly
- knowing that politeness includes both speaking and stopping

The durable pattern is:

`unfamiliar social seat -> polite topic -> short answer -> try another topic -> still short answer -> social despair -> punchline`

Do not over-moralize. The lesson should feel memorable because the table scene
is awkward and funny.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is future continuous used for polite questions about future
arrangements.

Teach these through the dinner-table questions:

- `Will you be seeing it?`
  - meaning: `Do you have plans to see the play?`
- `Will you be spending your holidays abroad this year?`
  - meaning: `Are you going to spend your holidays abroad?`
- `Will you be staying in England?`
  - meaning: `Are you going to stay in England?`

Student-facing explanation:

The narrator is not asking like a police officer. He is trying to make
conversation. `Will you be doing...?` makes the question feel like a polite
guess about someone's future arrangement.

Secondary language points:

- `make conversation`
  - make light, polite talk; 找话题 / 寒暄.
- `eyes were fixed on her plate`
  - she kept looking at her plate.
- `in a short time`
  - soon.
- `In despair`
  - because every topic failed.
- `whether she was enjoying her dinner`
  - an indirect question after several direct questions.
- `If you ate more and talked less, we would both enjoy our dinner`
  - a pointed conditional sentence; she means he is talking too much.

Do not teach the final conditional as a heavy grammar lecture. Let the student
feel the meaning first: `多吃点，少说点，我们俩都开心。`

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `hostess`
   - Meaning: the woman who invites and looks after guests at a meal or event.
   - Bridge: in this story, the hostess arranges where the narrator sits.
   - Example: `The hostess asked me to sit next to Mrs. Rumbold.`
2. `make conversation`
   - Meaning: make light, polite talk, especially with someone you do not know
     well.
   - Bridge: this is the heart of the lesson; it is conversation as social
     politeness, not serious information exchange.
   - Example: `I tried to make conversation.`
3. `despair`
   - Meaning: the feeling that nothing is working and you are almost giving up.
   - Bridge: the narrator is not in tragic despair; he is socially desperate
     because every topic gets only `No`.
   - Example: `In despair, I asked her whether she was enjoying her dinner.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `dinner party` = formal meal with invited guests
- `sit next to` = sit beside
- `Mrs.` = polite title for a married or adult woman
- `Rumbold` = character's surname
- `large` = physically large; do not use this as mockery
- `unsmiling` = not smiling; serious
- `tight` = fitting closely to the body, used here for clothing
- `black dress` = the clothes she is wearing
- `look up` = raise one's eyes
- `take my seat` = sit down in my assigned place
- `beside` = next to
- `fixed on` = kept looking at
- `plate` = dish used for food
- `in a short time` = soon
- `The Globe` = theatre name in the story
- `play` = stage drama, not playing a game
- `abroad` = in another country
- `stay in England` = remain in England
- `ate more and talked less` = the final contrast

- `The Globe` is the name of the theatre in the story.
- `globe` as a common noun can mean the earth or a round model of the earth.
- With capital letters, `The Globe` works like a place name.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into polite small talk.

Recommended scenario:

`You are sitting next to someone you do not know well at a school dinner, camp meal, host-family meal, or parents' friends' gathering. You try to open a light conversation politely.`

Student should practice:

- greeting politely
- choosing safe light topics
- asking about future arrangements with `Will you be doing...?`
- noticing short answers
- changing topic once or twice
- stopping politely if the other person does not want to talk

Useful expressions:

- `Will you be joining the activity tomorrow?`
- `Will you be watching the performance?`
- `Will you be spending the holiday here?`
- `Will you be staying for the whole event?`
- `That's nice.`
- `I see. Enjoy your dinner.`

Add a boundary point:

Polite conversation is not just talking. It also means noticing when the other
person does not want a conversation.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to polite future arrangements.

Board structure:

`Small talk at dinner`

- `I tried to make conversation.`
- `Will you be seeing it?`
- `Will you be spending your holidays abroad?`
- `Will you be staying in England?`

`Why be doing?`

- `Will you do...?` = more direct
- `Will you be doing...?` = asking about a future arrangement
- Softer because it sounds like: `Will this be part of your plans?`

`The failed rhythm`

- Question 1 -> `No`
- Question 2 -> `No`
- Question 3 -> `No`
- In despair -> final question

`Punchline`

- `If you ate more and talked less, we would both enjoy our dinner.`
- Meaning first: `Please stop talking and let us eat.`

Avoid teaching future continuous as an abstract tense chart only. The grammar
should explain why the narrator's questions sound like polite dinner-table
conversation.
