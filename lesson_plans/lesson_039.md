## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a patient using formal indirect questions on the telephone
to get the information his doctor would not tell him face to face.

While John Gilbert was in hospital, he asked his doctor to tell him whether his
operation had been successful, but the doctor refused. The following day, John
asked for a bedside telephone. When he was alone, he called the hospital
exchange and asked for Doctor Millington. When the doctor answered, John said he
was inquiring about a certain patient, a Mr. John Gilbert. He asked if Mr.
Gilbert's operation had been successful, and the doctor said it had been. Then
he asked when Mr. Gilbert would be allowed to go home, and the doctor said he
would have to stay in hospital for another two weeks. Finally, the doctor asked
whether the caller was a relative of the patient. The patient answered: "No, I
am Mr. John Gilbert."

The courseware should make the student understand:

`patient in hospital -> asks doctor directly -> doctor refuses -> bedside telephone -> hospital exchange -> asks for Doctor Millington -> inquires about a certain patient -> gets operation result -> asks about going home -> doctor asks if caller is a relative -> "I am Mr. John Gilbert"`

The ending should land as: "He got the answer by asking about himself as if he
were another person."

## Old Hospital Phone And Privacy Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- In an older hospital setting, a patient might ask for a telephone beside the
  bed.
- To call someone inside the hospital, the patient might call the hospital
  exchange, meaning the switchboard / operator who connects calls.
- Today, a patient might use a mobile phone, hospital app, nurse call system, or
  electronic medical record.
- Today, medical information is usually protected by privacy rules.
- A doctor should normally confirm identity and authorization before discussing
  a patient's operation with a caller.
- The story is funny because the old system allows a misunderstanding: the
  patient is the caller, but the doctor thinks the caller is asking about
  someone else.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课里的 hospital exchange 不是换东西，也不是换钱，而是老式医院电话总机。病人床边没有今天这样的手机和 App，他要先打给总机，再让总机接到医生那里。今天医院更重视隐私，医生一般不会随便把病人的手术情况告诉电话里的人。所以这课不是教“套话”，而是一个旧时代电话场景里的幽默反转。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old hospital-phone scene to present-day experiences:

- wanting clear information after a medical test, exam, or important result
- asking directly but not receiving an answer
- using a phone system, customer service, school office, or app to ask for
  information
- privacy and identity verification: who is allowed to know someone's private
  information
- difference between patient, caller, and relative
- formal indirect questions when asking politely or officially

The durable pattern is:

`anxious question -> blocked direct answer -> formal phone inquiry -> mistaken identity -> comic reveal`

Do not turn this into a hospital-policy lecture. Keep the privacy point short
and clear so the humor still works.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is indirect questions and reported questions.

Teach these through the story:

- Direct question: `Was my operation successful?`
  - Story form: `whether his operation had been successful`
- Direct question: `Has Mr. Gilbert's operation been successful?`
  - Story form: `if Mr. Gilbert's operation had been successful`
- Direct question: `When will Mr. Gilbert be allowed to go home?`
  - Story form: `when Mr. Gilbert would be allowed to go home`
- Direct question: `Are you a relative of the patient?`
  - Story form: `if he was a relative of the patient`

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson is full of indirect questions because John is not speaking plainly
by the end. He is asking about himself in a formal, indirect way. The grammar
matches the trick: the sentence is indirect, and the whole phone call is
indirect too.

Use a compact board:

| Direct question | Indirect / reported form |
| --- | --- |
| Was my operation successful? | whether his operation had been successful |
| Has the operation been successful? | if the operation had been successful |
| When can he go home? | when he would be allowed to go home |
| Are you a relative? | if he was a relative |

Secondary language points:

- `refused to do so`
  - refused to tell him.
- `The following day`
  - the next day.
- `asked for`
  - requested.
- `inquire about`
  - ask for information about something, formal.
- `a certain patient`
  - a particular patient; also sounds deliberately formal and slightly
    mysterious here.
- `be allowed to`
  - have permission to.
- `another two weeks`
  - two more weeks.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into polite, privacy-aware
information requests.

Recommended scenario:

`You need to ask a school office, clinic, or customer service desk for information. You ask politely, explain who you are, and respect privacy rules.`

Student should practice:

- asking whether something was successful / completed / ready
- asking when someone will be allowed to do something
- saying who the caller is
- using `if` and `whether` in indirect questions
- respecting private information
- not pretending to be someone else

Useful expressions:

- `I am calling about...`
- `Could you tell me whether...?`
- `I would like to ask if...`
- `Could you tell me when...?`
- `I am the student / patient / parent.`
- `Do you need to check my identity?`
- `I understand this information is private.`

Keep the exercise modern and ethical. The student should learn polite inquiry,
not deception.
