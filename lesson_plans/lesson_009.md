## Production Warnings

This lesson needs cultural context before the story can make sense.

- `Town Hall`:
  - Do not translate it only as `市政厅` and move on. Chinese students may think
    of a government office where people handle paperwork, which makes the New
    Year gathering confusing.
  - Explain it as an old town's public landmark: a central building, often with
    a clock tower or public square, where people could gather for civic events.
  - Make clear that people are not going there to "do government business";
    they are gathering under a shared public clock.
- Then and now:
  - Do not imply that all British or American people still celebrate New Year
    this way today.
  - Explain that the text reflects an older public-life scene. Today people also
    watch phones, TV countdowns, livestreams, big screens, fireworks, and social
    media.
- Clock literacy:
  - Many children are used to digital time. Explain `five to twelve` as `11:55`
    and `two minutes past twelve` as `12:02`.
  - Explain `minute hand` as the long hand that points to minutes on an analog
    clock.
- Title:
  - `A cold welcome` is not about cold weather only. It personifies the clock:
    the big clock "refused" to welcome the New Year.

shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is not mainly about a broken clock. It is about a public countdown
that fails at the most important moment.

In the old town setting, people gather under the Town Hall clock on the last
night of the year. The clock is supposed to strike twelve and publicly mark the
start of the New Year. But at five to twelve, the clock stops. The minute hand
does not move. People wait and wait. Then someone notices it is already two
minutes past twelve. The New Year has arrived, but the big public clock has
missed its job.

For a child today, connect this to:

- a New Year countdown screen freezing at the last moment
- a livestream countdown lagging or getting stuck
- a school/event timer stopping before the final moment
- a phone app freezing, so people check another watch
- a public ceremony becoming funny because the main device fails

The real emotional movement is:

`public ritual -> shared countdown -> clock failure -> confusion -> somebody notices -> everyone laughs and sings`

## One-Sentence Hook

A whole crowd gathers under the town's big clock to welcome the New Year. The
clock is supposed to strike twelve, but it stops at 11:55 and misses the moment.

## Story Punchline

The comic chain is:

1. On Wednesday evening, people go to the Town Hall.
2. It is the last day of the year.
3. A large crowd has gathered under the Town Hall clock.
4. The clock will strike twelve in twenty minutes.
5. Fifteen minutes pass.
6. At five to twelve, the clock stops.
7. The big minute hand does not move.
8. People wait and wait, but nothing happens.
9. Someone shouts that it is already two minutes past twelve.
10. The big clock has refused to welcome the New Year.
11. Everybody begins to laugh and sing.

The ending should land as: "The clock failed at its one big job, but people
turned the awkward moment into laughter."

## Cultural Bridge

This section is essential for Lesson 9 and should appear early in the script,
preferably in the hook or discussion before the student is expected to understand
the joke.

Explain:

- A `Town Hall` is a town's public building and civic landmark, not just a place
  to handle documents.
- A `Town Hall clock` was a shared public time signal before everyone had phones.
- Gathering under the clock on New Year's Eve is like gathering around a public
  countdown screen today.
- The old clock is doing the job that a phone countdown, TV broadcast, big
  screen, or livestream might do now.
- This is an older English public-life scene. Modern people may celebrate in
  many other ways.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`Town Hall clock 对那时的人，就像今天跨年夜的大屏倒计时或手机倒计时。大家不是去市政厅办事，而是在同一个公共地标下面，一起等新年的第一刻。`

## Modern Bridge

Useful modern scenes:

- People watch a New Year countdown on a big city screen.
- A family waits for the TV host to count down to midnight.
- A class event has a timer on the classroom screen.
- A game or livestream countdown freezes.
- A phone shows the real time, while a public screen is stuck.

The transferable idea is:

`We trust one shared clock or screen for an important moment. When it fails, people first feel confused, then laugh together.`

## Vocabulary Strategy

Use exactly 3 formal vocab blocks, but explain analog-clock and countdown
language throughout the story.

### Formal Vocab Blocks

Prefer:

1. `Town Hall`
   - A town's public building / public landmark. Explain why people gather
     there.
2. `crowd`
   - A large group of people gathered in one place.
3. `strike`
   - When a clock rings or chimes the hour, especially `strike twelve`.

### Required Supporting Vocabulary

Explain these in context:

- `gathered`
  - People came together and stayed in one place.
- `last day of the year`
  - New Year's Eve context.
- `in twenty minutes' time`
  - Twenty minutes from now.
- `five to twelve`
  - 11:55, five minutes before twelve.
- `two minutes past twelve`
  - 12:02, two minutes after twelve.
- `minute hand`
  - The long hand on an analog clock.
- `shouted`
  - Said loudly because the crowd needed to hear.
- `refused to welcome`
  - Personification: the clock acts as if it will not welcome the New Year.
- `laugh and sing`
  - The crowd's mood turns from waiting/confusion to celebration.

### Time Word Network

The script should build a clear time network:

1. **Public place**
   `Town Hall / Town Hall clock / crowd / gathered`
2. **Countdown time**
   `strike twelve / in twenty minutes' time / five to twelve / two minutes past twelve`
3. **Clock failure**
   `stopped / minute hand / did not move / refused to welcome`

## Language Focus

Use these language points:

- `a large crowd of people had gathered`
  - They had already come together before the key moment.
- `It would strike twelve in twenty minutes' time`
  - From that moment, the clock was expected to ring at midnight soon.
- `at five to twelve`
  - A natural way to say 11:55.
- `The big minute hand did not move`
  - The visual evidence that the clock had stopped.
- `We waited and waited`
  - Repetition creates the feeling of waiting too long.
- `refused to welcome the New Year`
  - Personification and the title joke.
- `began to laugh and sing`
  - The final mood change.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: a countdown story with time layers.

Do not teach this as a tense chart. Teach it as a scene:

- Before the key moment:
  - `a large crowd of people had gathered`
  - People were already there before the clock was supposed to strike.
- Expected future from that moment:
  - `It would strike twelve in twenty minutes' time`
  - The clock was expected to ring soon.
- Passing time:
  - `Fifteen minutes passed`
  - `We waited and waited`
- Final realization:
  - `It's two minutes past twelve!`
  - The New Year has already arrived.

Student-friendly explanation:

`This story works like a countdown: people are already gathered, they expect the clock to ring soon, time passes, the clock stops, and then someone realizes midnight has already passed.`

## Read Aloud Sentences

Use 7 guided sentences. The goal is to make public-countdown and clock language
natural before the student moves into modern countdown-screen examples.

- `On Wednesday evening, we went to the Town Hall.`
  - focus: `Town Hall` as a public landmark, not just an office.
- `It was the last day of the year and a large crowd of people had gathered under the Town Hall clock.`
  - focus: `last day of the year`, `crowd`, `gathered`.
- `It would strike twelve in twenty minutes' time.`
  - focus: `strike twelve` and `in twenty minutes' time`.
- `Fifteen minutes passed and then, at five to twelve, the clock stopped.`
  - focus: `five to twelve` means 11:55.
- `The big minute hand did not move.`
  - focus: `minute hand` as the long hand of a clock.
- `Suddenly someone shouted. 'It's two minutes past twelve! The clock has stopped!'`
  - focus: `shouted`, `two minutes past twelve`, and the surprise.
- `The big clock refused to welcome the New Year. At that moment, everybody began to laugh and sing.`
  - focus: `refused to welcome` as personification; then the mood change.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. An old town square at night on New Year's Eve, with a Town Hall / clock tower
   as a public landmark and a crowd gathering below. No readable signs or text.
2. A close visual focus on the big public clock approaching midnight, with a
   clear analog minute hand and a waiting crowd.
3. The clock has stopped at five to twelve; people look confused and check a
   watch or small modern-looking timepiece for contrast if appropriate.
4. The crowd realizes midnight has passed and turns the failed countdown into a
   happy moment, laughing and singing together.

No text, letters, captions, readable signs, or visible English words in images.
Avoid making the Town Hall look like a Chinese government service hall. It
should read as an old Western town landmark / clock tower / public square.

## Tone

Warm, ceremonial, and lightly comic. The lesson should have New Year's Eve
atmosphere: a public gathering, shared expectation, a small failure, and a
collective laugh.

The script should keep the cultural background clear but not heavy. The child
should understand the scene quickly and then enjoy the countdown joke.
