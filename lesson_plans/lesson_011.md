## Production Warnings

This lesson needs careful handling of money, borrowing, and fairness.

- Title meaning:
  - Explain the proverb early: `One good turn deserves another` means if someone
    does something kind for you, it is fair to return the kindness.
  - In this story, Tony twists the idea. He gives money immediately, but only so
    the narrator can pay for his dinner.
- Borrowing ethics:
  - Do not present Tony's trick as clever behavior to copy.
  - The lesson should emphasize fair borrowing: if you borrow something, you
    should return it or pay it back.
- Child-facing bridge:
  - Use small, age-appropriate examples: borrowing a pencil, eraser, charger,
    snack money, game item, or paying first and settling later.
  - Avoid encouraging children to lend real money freely or negotiate payment
    without adults.
- `borrow` / `lend`:
  - These are the central language point. Chinese students often confuse the
    direction.
  - `borrow from` means take something from someone temporarily.
  - `lend to` means give something to someone temporarily.
- Student-facing display:
  - Prefer `托尼·斯蒂尔` rather than raw punctuation like `托尼.斯蒂尔` in Chinese
    narration.
  - `twenty pounds` is British money. Explain only as a story amount; do not
    convert or over-teach currency unless useful.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about borrowing, returning, and social fairness.

The narrator is having dinner at a restaurant when Tony Steele comes in. Tony
used to work in a lawyer's office and now works at a bank. He earns a good
salary, but he always borrows money from friends and never pays it back.

Tony has never borrowed money from the narrator. While Tony is eating, the
narrator asks Tony to lend him twenty pounds. We expect Tony to avoid lending
money, because he is the person who usually borrows and never pays back. But
Tony immediately gives the money. The twist is that Tony then says the narrator
can pay for his dinner, because Tony has never borrowed money from him before.

The real punchline:

`Tony is not generous. He turns the narrator's request into another way to make someone else pay.`

## One-Sentence Hook

Tony always borrows money and never pays it back. One day, the narrator asks him
for money first, and Tony gives it immediately, but there is a catch.

## Story Punchline

The social chain is:

1. The narrator is having dinner at a restaurant.
2. Tony Steele comes in.
3. Tony used to work in a lawyer's office.
4. Tony now works at a bank and earns a good salary.
5. But Tony always borrows money from friends and never pays it back.
6. Tony sits at the narrator's table.
7. Tony has never borrowed money from the narrator.
8. While Tony is eating, the narrator asks Tony to lend him twenty pounds.
9. Tony gives the money immediately.
10. The narrator is surprised.
11. Tony says he has never borrowed money from the narrator.
12. Therefore, the narrator can pay for Tony's dinner.

The ending should land as: "Tony gives the money, but he has already turned it
into a bill for someone else."

## Modern Bridge

Do not center the modern bridge only on adults lending money in restaurants.
Connect it to fairness and small everyday borrowing:

- A classmate often borrows a pen, eraser, ruler, or charger and forgets to
  return it.
- A friend says, "Can you pay first? I'll pay you back later," but often forgets.
- A group buys snacks, and one person always avoids paying their share.
- A game friend borrows an item, skin, or account resource but never returns it.
- Someone does a small favour and then immediately asks for a bigger favour in
  return.
- Mobile payment and split bills make `pay back` and `pay for` familiar, but the
  fairness rule is the same.

The transferable idea is:

`Borrowing only works when people remember the direction, the promise, and the return.`

## Vocabulary Strategy

Use exactly 3 formal vocab blocks. For this lesson, choose the words that
explain the borrowing logic, even though the raw lesson keywords include job and
salary words.

### Formal Vocab Blocks

Prefer:

1. `borrow`
   - To take something from someone for a while. Use with `from`.
   - Example direction: `I borrow money from Tony.`
2. `lend`
   - To give something to someone for a while. The thing goes out from me.
   - Example direction: `Tony lends me money.`
3. `pay back`
   - To return money that was borrowed.

### Required Supporting Vocabulary

Explain these in context:

- `turn`
  - In the proverb, a `good turn` means a kind act or favour.
- `deserve`
  - To be fair to receive something because of what happened.
- `lawyer's office`
  - A place where lawyers work; background information about Tony's old job.
- `bank`
  - Tony's current workplace; makes the money joke sharper.
- `salary`
  - Money someone earns from a job.
- `immediately`
  - Right away, without waiting.
- `pay for`
  - Pay the cost of something, such as dinner.
- `twenty pounds`
  - British money in the story.
- `to my surprise`
  - The narrator did not expect Tony to give money so quickly.

### Borrowing Direction Network

The script should make the direction visible:

1. **Money comes to me**
   `I borrow money from Tony.`
2. **Money goes from me to someone else**
   `I lend Tony money.`
3. **Money returns**
   `Tony pays it back.`
4. **Money pays a bill**
   `You can pay for my dinner.`

This direction network is more important than memorizing isolated translations.

## Language Focus

Use these language points:

- `I was having dinner ... when Tony Steele came in`
  - A background action is happening, then Tony enters.
- `worked in a lawyer's office years ago, but he is now working at a bank`
  - Past job versus current job.
- `He gets a good salary`
  - He has enough income; his borrowing is a habit, not because the story says
    he has no job.
- `always borrows money ... and never pays it back`
  - Repeated unfair habit.
- `He has never borrowed money from me`
  - Sets up why Tony's final line is possible.
- `I asked him to lend me twenty pounds`
  - The narrator reverses the usual borrowing direction.
- `To my surprise`
  - Marks the unexpected response.
- `so now you can pay for my dinner`
  - The twist: Tony turns the favour into his own benefit.

## Grammar Point

Recommended grammar point: restaurant scene timing plus borrowing history.

Do not teach this as a full tense table. Teach it as scene logic:

- Background action:
  - `I was having dinner at a restaurant`
  - The narrator is already in the middle of dinner.
- Interruption:
  - `when Tony Steele came in`
  - Tony enters during that background action.
- Simultaneous action:
  - `While he was eating, I asked him...`
  - The narrator asks during Tony's meal.
- Past background:
  - `Tony worked in a lawyer's office years ago`
  - His old job.
- Current situation:
  - `he is now working at a bank`
  - His current job.
- Experience up to now:
  - `He has never borrowed money from me`
  - This fact matters at the moment of the joke.

Student-friendly explanation:

`The grammar helps the joke: what was happening at the table, what Tony usually does, and what he had never done to this narrator before.`

## Read Aloud Sentences

Use 8 guided sentences. The goal is to make the borrowing direction and the
final twist easy to say before the student moves into modern fairness examples.

- `I was having dinner at a restaurant when Tony Steele came in.`
  - focus: background action plus interruption.
- `Tony worked in a lawyer's office years ago, but he is now working at a bank.`
  - focus: old job versus current job.
- `He gets a good salary, but he always borrows money from his friends and never pays it back.`
  - focus: `good salary`, `borrows money from`, `never pays it back`.
- `Tony saw me and came and sat at the same table.`
  - focus: simple action chain.
- `He has never borrowed money from me.`
  - focus: `has never borrowed` as setup for the ending.
- `While he was eating, I asked him to lend me twenty pounds.`
  - focus: `While he was eating`, `lend me twenty pounds`.
- `To my surprise, he gave me the money immediately.`
  - focus: `To my surprise`, `immediately`.
- `'I have never borrowed any money from you,' Tony said, 'so now you can pay for my dinner!'`
  - focus: the quotation and punchline; make `pay for my dinner` land clearly.

## Living-Scene Exercise

Use `mode: "all_fills"` for Lesson 11, consistent with Lessons 1-10.

The exercise should transfer the story into modern borrowing / returning /
fairness scenarios. The goal is to practice direction words, not to encourage
children to lend money casually.

Suggested word bank:

- `borrow`
- `lend`
- `from him`
- `pay me back`
- `pay it back`
- `salary`
- `immediately`
- `pay for`
- `good turn`
- `deserves another`
- `to my surprise`

Suggested three fill scenes:

- Borrowing direction:
  `I forgot my pen, so I asked Jack if I could ___ one ___.`
  Expected: `borrow`, `from him`
- Lending direction:
  `My friend asked me to ___ her twenty yuan, and she promised to ___ tomorrow.`
  Expected: `lend`, `pay me back`
- Fair payment:
  `Sam did one ___ for me and said one good turn ___. Then he asked me to ___ his drink.`
  Expected: `good turn`, `deserves another`, `pay for`

Production note: because this lesson hinges on direction, the final `you_too`
JSON should use a word bank that fits the exact sentences. Keep the core
contrast: borrow from / lend to / pay back / pay for.

## Visual Direction

Use 4 retell frames:

1. A restaurant dinner scene: the narrator is eating when Tony Steele enters.
   Keep it ordinary and modern enough to understand, but not too contemporary if
   the script keeps the British pound detail.
2. Tony's background: he used to work in a lawyer's office and now works at a
   bank; show this as simple visual contrast without readable signs or text.
3. Tony's borrowing habit: friends look frustrated because Tony borrows money
   and does not pay it back. Keep it light, not hostile.
4. The restaurant twist: the narrator asks Tony for money, Tony gives it, then
   points to the dinner bill with a confident expression. The narrator is
   surprised.

No text, letters, captions, readable bills, readable signs, or visible English
words in images. Avoid showing large sums of cash. Keep the humour social and
clear, not greedy or mean-spirited.

## Tone

Dry, social, and lightly satirical. This should feel like a small restaurant
story about a person who always manipulates borrowing rules.

The script should not moralize for too long, but it should make the fairness
line explicit: borrowing requires returning, and a favour should not become a
trick.
