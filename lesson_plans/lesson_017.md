# Lesson 17 Plan - Always Young

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 17. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 17 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 17
- Title: `Always young`
- Chinese title: `青春常驻`
- Core bridge: `这不是一课关于“35岁老不老”的课，而是一课关于演员真实年龄和角色年龄不同：演员可以通过服装、声音、动作和舞台表现，暂时变成另一个年龄和身份的人。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about acting and role identity, not about age anxiety.

- Do not imply that thirty-five is old.
  - The story says Jennifer is at least thirty-five only to create contrast
    with the young roles she plays.
  - Keep the tone playful, not judgmental.
- Do not make the clothes the whole lesson.
  - `bright red dress`, `long black stockings`, `short socks`, and
    `orange-coloured dress` are stage costumes.
  - Explain that costumes help the audience believe the role.
- Explain `play` carefully.
  - Here it means a theatre play, not "to play a game".
- Explain `appear` as stage appearance / role presentation.
  - It can mean "come into view", but in this lesson it also means how an
    actress appears as a character.
- Treat the final line as witty stage language.
  - `It must be terrible to be grown up!` is Jennifer's playful answer, not a
    serious complaint about adulthood.
- If production introduces the helpful word `role`, make clear that it is a
  support word, not a keyword from the original text.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about the difference between a real person and a stage role.

Jennifer is an actress. In real life, she must be at least thirty-five. In
spite of this, she often appears on the stage as a young girl. Soon she will
take part in a new play. This time, her role is a girl of seventeen. She must
wear a bright red dress and long black stockings. Last year, another role made
her wear short socks and a bright orange-coloured dress. When people ask her
how old she is, she replies as if growing up is the terrible thing.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`real person -> actress -> stage role -> costume -> audience believes the role -> witty ending`

The ending should land as: "Jennifer is always young on stage because she can
step into young roles again and again."

## One-Sentence Hook

How can a thirty-five-year-old actress become a seventeen-year-old girl on
stage? By stepping into a role.

## Story Focus

The acting chain is:

1. Jennifer is the narrator's aunt.
2. She is an actress.
3. She must be at least thirty-five years old.
4. In spite of this, she often appears on the stage as a young girl.
5. She will take part in a new play soon.
6. This time, she will be a girl of seventeen.
7. Her costume must make the audience see the young role.
8. Last year, another costume helped her play another young role.
9. If anyone asks her age, she gives a playful answer.
10. The title works because the stage lets her stay "always young" as a
    performer.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`Jennifer in real life` versus `Jennifer in the role`.

## Culture And Scene Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- An `actress` is a female actor.
- A `stage` is where theatre actors perform in front of an audience.
- A `play` is a theatre story performed by actors.
- A `role` is the character an actor plays.
- A `costume` is clothing worn for a role.
- `grown up` means an adult, or someone who has grown from a child into an
  adult.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`现在的小朋友可能不常看传统舞台剧，但很容易理解电影、动画配音、学校短剧和 cosplay。一个成年人可以给动画里的小孩配音，一个演员可以在电影里演学生，一个同学也可以在学校短剧里演老人、国王或侦探。真实的人是一回事，角色是另一回事。Lesson 17 讲的就是这个：Jennifer 本人不是17岁，但她在舞台上可以扮演17岁的女孩。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the theatre scene to present-day experiences:

- a grown-up voice actor speaking as a child in an animation
- an actor playing a student in a TV show or film
- a school play where students act as parents, detectives, kings, or old people
- cosplay, where clothing helps someone become a character for a short time
- a drama club rehearsal with costumes and stage directions
- a video creator acting different characters in one short video

The durable pattern is:

`real identity -> role identity -> costume and performance -> audience accepts the role`

Do not turn the lesson into a history of theatre. Keep it close to the child's
experience of pretending, acting, dressing for a role, and speaking in a role.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is the contrast between `must be` as deduction and
`have to / had to` as external requirement.

Teach it with the story:

- `She must be at least thirty-five years old.`
  - Meaning: I am sure / I guess strongly that she is at least thirty-five.
  - This is deduction.
- `Jennifer will have to take part in a new play soon.`
  - Meaning: the new play requires her to do it.
  - This is an obligation or arrangement.
- `She had to wear short socks...`
  - Meaning: the role required that costume last year.
  - This is a past obligation.
- `She must appear in a bright red dress...`
  - Meaning: the stage role requires this appearance.
  - This is a role requirement.

Student-facing explanation:

`must be` can mean "一定是", because the speaker is making a strong guess.
`have to` means "不得不 / 必须", because something outside you requires it.

Use a compact contrast:

| Sentence | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| She must be at least thirty-five. | I am almost sure about her age. |
| She will have to take part in a new play. | The play requires her to do it. |
| She had to wear short socks. | The old role required that costume. |

Secondary language point:

- `in spite of this`
  - Meaning: although this is true, something surprising still happens.
  - In this lesson: she is at least thirty-five, but she still plays young
    girls.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `appear`
   - Meaning in this lesson: show oneself on stage as a role.
   - Bridge: appear on screen, appear in a video, appear on stage.
   - Example: `She often appears on the stage as a young girl.`
2. `stage`
   - Meaning: the place where actors perform in a theatre.
   - Bridge: school stage, theatre stage, performance stage.
   - Example: `on the stage`
3. `bright`
   - Meaning: strong, clear, easy-to-notice in colour or light.
   - Bridge: bright red, bright orange, bright screen.
   - Example: `a bright red dress`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `actress` = female actor
- `play` = theatre performance, not a game here
- `take part in` = join and do something as part of it
- `role` = the character an actor plays
- `costume` = clothes for a role
- `stockings` = long close-fitting clothing for legs
- `socks` = shorter clothing for feet
- `in spite of this` = despite this
- `at least` = not less than
- `grown up` = adult
- `Darling` = a warm or theatrical way to address someone

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a small backstage tour.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "Can someone who is thirty-five play a seventeen-year-old on stage?"
2. Bridge from theatre to animation, film, school play, and cosplay.
3. Explain real age versus role age.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: Jennifer backstage, clearly an adult actress preparing for a
     role.
   - Frame 2: Jennifer on stage appearing as a young girl.
   - Frame 3: Jennifer preparing for the new play as a girl of seventeen, with
     the red dress and black stockings as costume details.
   - Frame 4: Someone asks her age, and Jennifer answers playfully about being
     grown up.
5. Discussion: why costume and acting can change how the audience sees a
   person.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `appear`, `stage`, `bright`.
8. Grammar: `must be` deduction versus `have to / had to` requirement.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals theatrical, warm, and not age-mocking.

- Use one consistent Jennifer across all four frames. She should remain the
  same adult actress even when she appears as a younger stage role.
- Suggested storyboard `visual_consistency`:
  `Jennifer is the same professional adult actress in every frame: a graceful
  woman in her mid-thirties, kind expressive eyes, oval face, dark wavy
  shoulder-length hair, slim build, poised theatre-performer posture. Keep her
  facial features, hair, build, and warm theatrical presence consistent across
  backstage and stage scenes; only the costume and role presentation change.`
- Use a theatre backstage and stage setting.
- Jennifer should look like a professional actress, not a caricature.
- The young role should be shown through costume, posture, and stage light, not
  through making her look like a child in an unrealistic way.
- Make costumes visible: bright red dress, black stockings, short socks, orange
  dress.
- Use a clear difference between backstage reality and stage performance.
- The final frame should feel witty and charming, like a performer answering in
  character.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Backstage: Jennifer, an adult actress, looking at a script and costume rack.
2. On stage: Jennifer appearing as a young girl in a performance.
3. Costume detail: bright red dress, long black stockings, and rehearsal for
   the seventeen-year-old role.
4. Punchline: someone asks her age; Jennifer smiles and says growing up must be
   terrible.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about acting a role in a school play or
short video.

Good prompts:

- `如果你要在 school play 里扮演 detective，你必须穿什么、怎么说话？`
- `如果一个成年人给动画里的小孩配音，真实年龄和角色年龄有什么不同？`
- `选择一个角色：king, detective, old man, robot, student from the future。用英语说你 must / have to 做什么。`

Expected scaffold:

`In real life, I am ___. In the play, I appear as ___. I have to wear ___. I have to speak ___.`

Add one contrast sentence:

`I am not really ___, but I play ___.`

Keep the exercise playful. The student should feel how English describes role
identity without confusing it with real identity.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the real-age / role-age contrast and the final
joke.

Recommended sentences:

1. `My aunt Jennifer is an actress.`
   - Focus: `actress`
2. `She must be at least thirty-five years old.`
   - Focus: `must be`, `at least`
3. `In spite of this, she often appears on the stage as a young girl.`
   - Focus: `In spite of this`, `appears on the stage`
4. `Jennifer will have to take part in a new play soon.`
   - Focus: `will have to`, `take part in`
5. `This time, she will be a girl of seventeen.`
   - Focus: role age
6. `In the play, she must appear in a bright red dress and long black stockings.`
   - Focus: `must appear`, costume words
7. `Last year in another play, she had to wear short socks and a bright, orange-coloured dress.`
   - Focus: `had to wear`
8. `Darling, it must be terrible to be grown up!`
   - Focus: punchline tone, `grown up`

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and
8.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the contrast:

`real age -> in spite of this -> stage role -> costume requirement -> playful answer`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a modal-verb chart. Start from the story.

Suggested explanation:

`这篇课文里有两个很像的“必须/一定”。She must be at least thirty-five 是说：我推测她一定至少35岁。She had to wear short socks 是说：角色要求她不得不穿。一个是“我判断”，一个是“外部要求”。`

Use a compact display:

| Expression | Function | Story meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| must be | strong guess | I am sure about her age. |
| will have to | future requirement | The new play requires her to join. |
| had to | past requirement | The old role required that costume. |
| must appear | role requirement | The costume/role requires this look. |

Then connect to acting:

`演员的真实身份和角色身份不同，所以英语里会不断出现：她本来是谁，她必须在舞台上呈现成谁。`

## Tone

Warm, theatrical, and playful.

The child should feel:

- "I understand what a stage play is."
- "I know that an actor's real age and role age can be different."
- "I can explain why costume matters."
- "I can hear the difference between `must be` and `have to`."
- "The ending is a joke, not a sad sentence."

Avoid:

- jokes about age or appearance
- making Jennifer look foolish
- turning the lesson into fashion vocabulary only
- overexplaining theatre history
