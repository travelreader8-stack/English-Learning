# Lesson 23 Plan - A New House

This is the source-of-truth design card for producing Lesson 23. A development
thread should read `AGENTS.md`, then `LESSON_PRODUCTION_PLAN.md`, then this file
before producing any Lesson 23 assets.

## Basic Info

- Lesson: 23
- Title: `A new house`
- Chinese title: `新居`
- Core bridge: `这不是单纯的“搬新家”，而是远方亲人回来前的 surprise：姐姐住在遥远的 Nigeria，不知道家里已经在英国乡下建成了一栋非常 modern、在当地显得 strange 的新房子。`

## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about surprise, distance, and a modern house in an old
district, not about luxury housing.

- Explain `Nigeria` compactly.
  - It is a country in Africa, far from England.
  - The point is distance and absence, not a full geography lesson.
- Explain why the sister will be surprised.
  - She left before the house was completed.
  - She may not expect a very modern house in the country.
- Do not make `modern` automatically mean better.
  - In the story, modern means new in style, different from traditional houses.
- Do not make `strange` mean bad or ugly.
  - It means unfamiliar or unusual to some people because it does not match the
    surrounding district.
- Explain `country` as countryside.
  - Here it means a rural area, not "nation".
- Explain `district` as local area.
  - It is the surrounding area or neighbourhood, not a school district unless
    using that as a modern bridge.
- Keep the house welcoming.
  - The narrator has told his sister she can stay with them.
  - The story should feel like family welcome plus architectural surprise.

If production requires editing `web/data/lessons.json`, stop and ask for a
shared-data edit because it is outside the normal per-lesson write set.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a sister who will come home to find that home has changed.

The narrator had a letter from his sister, who lives in Nigeria. In her letter,
she said she would come to England next year. If she comes, she will get a
surprise. The family is now living in a beautiful new house in the country. Work
on it had begun before the sister left, and the house was completed five months
ago. The narrator has told her that she can stay with them. The house has many
large rooms and a lovely garden. It is very modern, so it looks strange to some
people. It must be the only modern house in the district.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`sister far away -> letter says she will visit -> house was being built before she left -> house completed -> modern house in countryside -> surprise`

The ending should land as: "The new house is not strange because it is bad; it
is strange because it is the only modern house in an old-looking district."

## One-Sentence Hook

His sister thinks she is coming back to the old home, but next year she may find
a modern house in the countryside instead.

## Story Focus

The surprise chain is:

1. The narrator receives a letter from his sister.
2. She lives in Nigeria.
3. She says she will come to England next year.
4. If she comes, she will get a surprise.
5. The family is now living in a beautiful new house in the country.
6. Work on the house had begun before the sister left.
7. The house was completed five months ago.
8. The narrator has told her she can stay with them.
9. The house has large rooms and a lovely garden.

The production should make the contrast visible:

`what the sister remembers` versus `what the family lives in now`.

## Geography And Scene Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- `Nigeria` is a country in Africa.
- `England` is where the narrator and the new house are.
- The sister is far away, so she does not see the house being finished.
- `country` means countryside in this lesson.
- A modern house in the countryside can look unusual if the surrounding houses
  are old or traditional.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课不需要把尼日利亚讲成一节地理课。孩子只要知道：姐姐住在非洲的 Nigeria，离 England 很远。她离开以后，家里的房子继续建，五个月前建好了。她明年来英国时，会发现家已经不是她记忆中的样子，而是一栋在乡下看起来很现代、很特别的新房子。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the new-house scene to present-day experiences:

- going back to an old neighbourhood and seeing a new glass building
- returning to school after a holiday and finding the classroom renovated
- visiting grandparents and discovering a modern extension or smart home
- a new cafe or library opening in an old street
- a house that looks normal to the owner but strange to neighbours because it
  uses a different style
- a relative coming back after years away and being surprised by family changes

The durable pattern is:

`far away -> old memory -> new change -> surprise -> different people judge the change differently`

Do not make the lesson about showing off a rich house. Keep it about distance,
time, change, and how modern design can feel unfamiliar in an old place.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is timeline language: reported future, past perfect, and
passive voice used to explain the house's building history.

Teach it through the story:

- `she said that she would come to England next year`
  - The sister wrote in the past about a future visit.
  - `would come` is future-in-the-past / reported speech.
- `If she comes, she will get a surprise`
  - A real possible future condition.
  - If she comes -> she will be surprised.
- `Work on it had begun before my sister left`
  - The building work started before the sister left.
  - Past perfect shows the earlier event.
- `The house was completed five months ago`
  - Passive voice focuses on the house being finished.
  - We do not need to say who completed it.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson is really a timeline. The sister left, the house work had already
begun, the house was completed later, and next year she may come back. English
uses different forms to place each event on the timeline.

Use a compact timeline:

| Time | Sentence |
| --- | --- |
| before sister left | Work on it had begun |
| five months ago | The house was completed |
| yesterday's letter | she said she would come next year |
| next year | If she comes, she will get a surprise |

Secondary language points:

- `stay with us`
  - Meaning: live in our home as a guest for a while.
- `It must be...`
  - Strong guess: it is almost certainly the only modern house in the district.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `modern`
   - Meaning in this lesson: new in style, design, or technology.
   - Bridge: modern house, modern classroom, modern phone.
   - Example: `It is a very modern house.`
2. `strange`
   - Meaning: unusual or unfamiliar; not necessarily bad.
   - Bridge: a modern building in an old street may look strange at first.
   - Example: `It looks strange to some people.`
3. `district`
   - Meaning: an area or local part of a town/country.
   - Bridge: neighbourhood, local area, school district as a familiar modern
     comparison.
   - Example: `the only modern house in the district`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `Nigeria` = country in Africa
- `England` = country where the narrator lives
- `letter` = written message
- `country` = countryside in this lesson
- `surprise` = something unexpected
- `work on it` = building work on the house
- `begun` = past participle of begin
- `completed` = finished
- `stay with us` = live with us as a guest
- `large rooms` = big rooms
- `lovely garden` = beautiful garden
- `must be` = strong guess

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a family letter story with a visual reveal.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "A sister far away writes that she will visit, but she does not know
   home has changed."
2. Bridge Nigeria and England as distant places; keep geography compact.
3. Explain countryside, modern house, and why neighbours think it looks strange.
4. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: narrator receives a letter from his sister in Nigeria.
   - Frame 2: flashback/timeline: work on the house had begun before the sister
     left.
   - Frame 3: the completed modern house in the English countryside, with large
     rooms and a garden.
   - Frame 4: contrast between the modern house and older/traditional houses in
     the district; the sister's future surprise is implied.
5. Discussion: why modern can look strange in an old district.
6. Passage reading.
7. Vocab: `modern`, `strange`, `district`.
8. Grammar: timeline with `would come`, `had begun`, `was completed`, `If...
   will`.
9. Living-scene transfer.
10. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep visuals warm, familial, and architecture-focused.

- Use one consistent narrator, one implied/letter-based sister, and one
  consistent modern house across frames.
- Suggested storyboard `visual_consistency`:
  `Keep the same narrator and house across all frames. The narrator is a warm
  adult man in his thirties, short brown hair, simple sweater, calm family
  expression. The sister appears as a distant relative through letter or
  imagined future arrival: adult woman in her thirties with similar family
  features, travel coat, carrying a small suitcase. The modern house is the same
  clean-lined countryside house with large windows, pale walls, simple geometric
  shape, many large rooms suggested through windows, and a lovely garden. Keep
  the surrounding district rural with older traditional houses nearby; no
  readable text anywhere.`
- If a geography hint is useful, it can appear as a very simple no-text visual:
  a letter travelling from far-away Africa/Nigeria direction to England, but do
  not make geography the main frame unless needed.
- Show modern design as different, not ugly.
- Avoid luxury real-estate advertising style.
- Avoid readable text on letters, maps, signs, addresses, or envelopes.
- The final frame should clearly show the modern house standing out among older
  countryside houses.

Four storyboard frames:

1. Narrator at home reading a letter from his sister far away.
2. Earlier timeline: builders or construction work had begun before the sister
   left.
3. The completed modern house in the countryside with large rooms and a lovely
   garden.
4. The modern house looks unusual among older houses in the district; the
   sister's future surprise is suggested.

## Living-Scene Exercise

The student-facing transfer should be about someone returning after a period
away and finding a familiar place changed.

Good prompts:

- `你有没有离开一段时间，再回来发现家、小区、学校或教室变了？`
- `如果一个亲戚明年来你家，他/她会看到什么 surprise？`
- `一个东西很 modern，但别人觉得 strange，你会怎么解释？`

Expected scaffold:

`If ___ comes next year, he/she will get a surprise. The ___ was completed ___. It is very modern, so it looks strange to some people.`

Add a school/home version:

`Work on it had begun before ___. Now it has ___ and ___.`

Keep the exercise about time, change, and different reactions to new design.

## Read-Aloud Guidance

Choose 5-8 sentences that carry the distance, timeline, and modern-house reveal.

Recommended sentences:

1. `I had a letter from my sister yesterday.`
   - Focus: opening situation
2. `She lives in Nigeria.`
   - Focus: distant place
3. `In her letter, she said that she would come to England next year.`
   - Focus: `said that she would come`
4. `If she comes, she will get a surprise.`
   - Focus: `If... will...`
5. `We are now living in a beautiful new house in the country.`
   - Focus: `in the country` = countryside
6. `Work on it had begun before my sister left.`
   - Focus: `had begun before`
7. `The house was completed five months ago.`
   - Focus: passive `was completed`
8. `It is a very modern house, so it looks strange to some people.`
   - Focus: `modern`, `strange`
9. `It must be the only modern house in the district.`
   - Focus: `must be`, `district`

If a shorter read-aloud set is needed, prioritize sentences 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8,
and 9.

The read-aloud section should help the student hear the timeline:

`letter from sister -> would come next year -> will get a surprise -> had begun before she left -> was completed five months ago -> modern house in the district`

## Grammar Handling

Do not start from a giant tense chart. Start from the house timeline.

Suggested explanation:

`这课是在用英语给房子排时间线。姐姐昨天来信，说她明年 would come。她如果来了，就 will get a surprise。为什么？因为她离开之前，房子的工程 had begun；五个月前，房子 was completed。英语里的这些形式不是为了复杂，而是帮我们说清楚：谁先发生，谁后发生，谁还没发生。`

Use a compact display:

| Story time | English |
| --- | --- |
| before my sister left | Work on it had begun |
| five months ago | The house was completed |
| in yesterday's letter | she said she would come |
| next year | If she comes, she will get a surprise |

Then connect vocabulary:

`modern` explains the house's style; `strange` explains other people's reaction;
`district` explains the local area where the house stands out.

## Tone

Warm, curious, and lightly surprised.

The child should feel:

- "I understand why the sister will be surprised."
- "I know Nigeria is far from England in this story."
- "I understand country means countryside here."
- "I know modern can look strange when the area around it is old."
- "I can follow the timeline: had begun, was completed, would come, will get."

Avoid:

- making the lesson about showing off wealth
- making modern design automatically better than old houses
- treating strange as ugly
- spending too long on geography
- turning the grammar into an abstract tense lecture
