## Production Warnings

This lesson should be about a real parking nuisance plus a mythological joke,
not about scary monsters or revenge.

- Explain the house layout early.
  - Jasper has a house in the city.
  - His `gate` is the entrance to his property.
  - His `garage` is where he needs to park his own car.
  - When someone parks outside his gate, he cannot drive his own car into the
    garage.
- Do not assume Chinese children know this housing pattern.
  - Many children live in apartment buildings, not houses with private gates and
    garages.
  - Bridge it to a blocked garage entrance, private parking space, school gate,
    community entrance, or fire lane.
- Explain `No Parking` as a sign and a rule.
  - The problem is not that Jasper dislikes cars.
  - The problem is that other people block his entrance.
- Explain `Medusa` with the Chinese name.
  - `Medusa` = `美杜莎`.
  - She is a famous figure from Greek myth.
  - In the myth, people who look at her can be turned to stone.
  - She is often shown with snakes for hair.
- Explain `Gorgon` compactly.
  - A Gorgon is a kind of monster in Greek myth.
  - In this lesson, `Medusa, the Gorgon` means "the Gorgon called Medusa."
- Keep the myth bridge light.
  - Mention that Medusa appears in well-known modern stories and films, such as
    `Percy Jackson`, only as a memory hook.
  - Do not turn the lesson into a full mythology lesson.
- Do not make the visuals frightening.
  - The stone head can be ugly and funny, not horror-like.
  - No violence, petrified bodies, or scary close-ups.
- Normalize obvious student-facing capitalization.
  - The raw source has `he has just bought...` and `jasper hopes...`.
  - Student-facing script, headings, reading text, and exercises can use `He`
    and `Jasper` naturally.
  - Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production unless
    the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a normal city problem solved with a very abnormal joke.

Jasper White is one of the rare people who believes in ancient myths. He has
just bought a new house in the city. Ever since he moved in, he has had trouble
with cars and their owners. At night, when he comes home, he often finds that
someone has parked a car outside his gate. Because of this, he has not been
able to get his own car into his garage even once. He puts up `No Parking`
signs, but they do not have any effect. Then he puts an ugly stone head over the
gate. It is Medusa, the Gorgon. Jasper hopes that she will turn cars and their
owners to stone, but none of them has been turned to stone yet.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`city house -> blocked gate -> garage problem -> No Parking signs fail -> Medusa stone head -> myth joke -> no magic yet`

The ending should land as: "Jasper's solution is funny because he uses an
ancient myth against a modern parking problem."

## One-Sentence Hook

No one obeyed Jasper's `No Parking` signs, so he tried a Greek monster instead.

## Culture And Myth Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- `ancient myths` are very old stories about gods, heroes, monsters, and magic.
- `Greek myth` means myths from ancient Greece.
- `Medusa` is called `美杜莎` in Chinese.
- Medusa is a famous Gorgon. She is often shown with snakes for hair.
- In the myth, looking at Medusa can turn a person to stone.
- Modern movies, books, and games sometimes use Medusa. For example, many
  children may know or later meet her through `Percy Jackson`.
- Jasper puts a stone head of Medusa over his gate because he wants the parking
  problem to be "turned to stone" too.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这里的 Medusa 要直接告诉孩子中文名：美杜莎。她是希腊神话里很有名的蛇发女怪，传说中人看到她会变成石头。后来很多电影、游戏、小说都借用过这个形象，比如 Percy Jackson。Jasper 把美杜莎石头像放在大门上，不是真的在讲恐怖故事，而是一个很夸张的笑话：No Parking 没用，那就让美杜莎来吓一吓乱停车的人。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old house-and-garage scene to present-day experiences:

- a private garage entrance being blocked by another car
- a school gate blocked by cars at pickup time
- a community entrance or underground garage ramp blocked by parking
- a fire lane or emergency access lane blocked by parked cars
- a private parking space occupied by someone else
- signs that say `No Parking`, `Private Parking`, or `Keep Clear`
- legal / modern responses: call property management, contact the owner, call a
  parking service, put up a legal barrier or clearer sign
- absurd / story response: put up Medusa and hope for magic

The durable pattern is:

`real rule ignored -> normal warning fails -> exaggerated symbolic solution -> comic ending`

Do not turn the modern bridge into a full traffic-law lesson. The child only
needs to understand why blocking a gate or garage is frustrating and why
Jasper's solution is funny.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is present perfect for a problem that continues to the present,
plus passive voice for the myth joke.

Teach it through the story:

- `has just bought`
  - Jasper bought the house recently.
- `ever since he moved in, he has had trouble`
  - The trouble started when he moved in and continues now.
- `has not been able to`
  - He still has not succeeded.
- `has put up`
  - He has already tried signs.
- `have not had any effect`
  - The signs still have not solved the problem.
- `has been turned to stone yet`
  - Passive voice plus `yet`: up to now, no one has been changed into stone.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson uses present perfect because Jasper's parking problem is not just a
one-time event. It started after he moved in and still matters now. The final
sentence uses the same grammar for the joke: up to now, Medusa has not turned
any cars or owners to stone.

Use a compact timeline:

| Time / result | Sentence |
| --- | --- |
| recently | has just bought a new house |
| from moving in until now | has had trouble |
| up to now, not even once | has not been able to get his car into the garage |
| already tried | has put up No Parking signs |
| still no result | have not had any effect |
| still no magic | none has been turned to stone yet |

Secondary language points:

- `one of those rare people who...`
  - Jasper belongs to a small unusual group.
- `ever since`
  - from that time until now.
- `because of this`
  - because of the parking problem.
- `even once`
  - emphasizes that it has never happened.
- `turn... to stone`
  - change someone or something into stone.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `myth`
   - Meaning: an ancient story about gods, heroes, monsters, or magic.
   - Bridge: Greek myths; Medusa / 美杜莎 as a famous myth figure.
   - Example: `Jasper believes in ancient myths.`
2. `garage`
   - Meaning: a place where a car is kept, often beside or under a house.
   - Bridge: apartment parking space, underground garage, private parking spot.
   - Example: `He has not been able to get his own car into his garage.`
3. `effect`
   - Meaning: a result or influence.
   - Bridge: a sign has an effect if people change their behaviour after seeing
     it.
   - Example: `The No Parking signs have not had any effect.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `rare` = not common
- `ancient` = very old
- `believe in` = think something is real or true
- `move in` = start living in a new home
- `trouble` = problem or difficulty
- `owner` = person who owns something
- `gate` = entrance to a house or yard
- `No Parking` = do not park here
- `put up` = place or hang something where people can see it
- `ugly` = not pleasant to look at
- `stone head` = head-shaped statue made of stone
- `Medusa / 美杜莎` = famous Gorgon in Greek myth
- `Gorgon` = monster type in Greek myth
- `turn... to stone` = change into stone
- `yet` = up to now, used in negatives/questions

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a city-life problem with a mythological punchline.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "A No Parking sign did not work, so Jasper tried Medusa."
2. Bridge the house layout: gate, garage, blocked entrance.
3. Explain `No Parking` signs and why they should have worked.
4. Introduce Medusa / 美杜莎 and the stone-turning myth, briefly and lightly.
5. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: Jasper's new city house, gate, garage, and a car blocking the
     entrance.
   - Frame 2: Jasper puts up `No Parking` signs, but cars still block the gate.
   - Frame 3: Jasper places an ugly stone head of Medusa / 美杜莎 over the gate.
   - Frame 4: comic ending: cars are still there; none has been turned to stone.
6. Discussion: why this is funny, not scary.
7. Passage reading.
8. Vocab: `myth`, `garage`, `effect`.
9. Grammar: present perfect continuing problem, passive `has been turned`.
10. Living-scene transfer.
11. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Four-Frame Storyboard Intent

Frame 1: Blocked Gate

- Jasper returns to his new city house at night or evening.
- A car is parked outside his gate.
- The garage entrance is clearly behind the gate, blocked by the parked car.
- The visual question: "How can Jasper get his own car in?"

Frame 2: No Parking Signs Fail

- Jasper has put up signs outside the gate.
- Another car is still parked there.
- Jasper looks frustrated and confused.
- The visual contrast: "The rule is visible, but it has no effect."

Frame 3: Medusa Over The Gate

- Jasper places or points to an ugly stone head over the gate.
- The head should suggest Medusa / 美杜莎 with snake-like stone hair.
- Keep it comic and mythic, not horror.
- The visual idea: "Maybe myth can do what signs cannot."

Frame 4: No Magic Yet

- Cars and owners are still normal.
- The Medusa head looks fierce but useless.
- Jasper looks disappointed or wryly hopeful.
- The image should imply: "None of them has been turned to stone yet."

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the story into a modern blocked-
entrance or rule-ignored scene.

Recommended scenario:

`Someone has parked in front of a garage, gate, fire lane, or private parking space. A sign has not worked. You need to explain the problem and suggest a sensible solution.`

Student should practice:

- explaining why a parked car is causing trouble
- using `No Parking` and `garage`
- saying a sign has no effect
- suggesting a normal modern solution
- understanding the story's absurd myth solution as a joke

Useful expressions:

- `Someone has parked outside the gate.`
- `We can't get the car into the garage.`
- `The No Parking sign has not had any effect.`
- `We should call the property manager.`
- `We should ask the owner to move the car.`
- `Jasper wants Medusa to turn them to stone, but that is only a joke.`

The exercise should not encourage revenge, vandalism, or scaring people. Keep
the practical response legal and calm.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to the continuing problem.

Board structure:

`Present perfect: past action, present result`

- `has just bought`
  - recent action
- `has had trouble`
  - problem started before and continues now
- `has not been able to`
  - still no success
- `has put up`
  - already tried a solution
- `have not had any effect`
  - still no result

`Passive voice in the joke`

- `has been turned to stone`
  - focus on what happens to cars and owners
- `none of them has been turned to stone yet`
  - up to now, the myth has not worked

Use the final contrast:

`The signs have not had any effect.`

`Medusa has not had any effect either.`

Avoid a full table of all perfect tenses. The child should connect the grammar
to "this problem is still going on."
