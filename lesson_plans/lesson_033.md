## Production Warnings

This lesson should feel tense but calm, serious but not frightening.

- Do not romanticize dangerous boating or swimming.
  - Make clear that in modern life, going out alone in a small boat during bad
    weather is unsafe.
  - Mention life jackets, weather forecasts, phones / GPS, and asking for help
    as the modern safety bridge.
- Do not make the girl look like a superhero.
  - The story is about danger, survival, direction, and rescue.
  - Her strength matters, but the tone should stay realistic.
- Explain the old story context.
  - The text comes from an older world with fewer instant communication tools.
  - Today a child may expect phone location, emergency calls, and rescue
    services.
- Treat `light ahead` as the turning point.
  - The light is both a real light on the cliffs and the hope that she is near
    shore.
- Keep the final hospital scene gentle.
  - The ending should feel safe and relieved, not medically dramatic.
- Explain the geography words visually.
  - `coast`, `shore`, `rock`, and `cliff` are easy to confuse if taught only as
    translations.
  - The vocabulary page should include a small teaching diagram.
- Do not edit `web/data/lessons.json` during normal lesson production unless
  the user explicitly asks for a shared-data fix.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a girl surviving a night in the sea by moving from
darkness toward a light.

Nearly a week passes before the girl can explain what happened. One afternoon
she sets out from the coast in a small boat and is caught in a storm. Towards
evening, the boat strikes a rock, so she jumps into the sea. She spends the
whole night in the water and swims eight miles to the shore. Early the next
morning, she sees a light ahead. Because the light is high up on the cliffs,
she knows she is near land. When she arrives at the shore, she struggles up the
cliff toward the light. That is all she remembers. A day later, she wakes up in
hospital.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`coast -> small boat -> storm -> rock -> sea -> whole night -> eight miles -> light ahead -> cliffs -> struggle up -> hospital`

The ending should land as: "The light was not just a detail; it gave her a
direction out of the darkness."

## One-Sentence Hook

A girl was lost in the sea all night, but one light high on the cliffs helped
her find the shore.

## Geography And Safety Bridge

This section should appear early in the script, before detailed vocabulary.

Explain:

- `coast` is the area of land beside the sea. It is larger than one exact point.
- `shore` is the edge where water meets land, especially the place where she can
  finally reach land.
- `rock` here means a dangerous rock / reef in the sea, not a small stone on a
  road.
- `cliff` is a high, steep rock face near the sea.
- `ahead` means in front of her, in the direction she is moving.
- The light is high up on the cliffs, so she understands that land is close.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`这课最好先看一张海边小地图：女孩从 coast 出发，船在海上撞到 rock，她跳进 sea，游到 shore。可是岸边不是平坦沙滩，而是高高的 cliff。她看到 cliff 上有一盏 light ahead，于是知道自己接近陆地了。`

Modern safety bridge:

`现在如果出海，不能只靠勇气。要查天气，穿救生衣，带可以联系人的设备，不要一个人冒险。课文里的女孩很坚强，但我们学习的是方向、求生和语言，不是模仿危险行为。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old sea-rescue scene to present-day experiences:

- weather apps and forecasts before boating
- life jackets and basic water safety
- phones, GPS, emergency calls, and location sharing
- rescue lights, lighthouses, buildings, or signals on shore
- keeping calm and moving toward a visible safe point
- telling a traumatic experience only after rest and recovery

The durable pattern is:

`danger -> no clear direction -> find a signal -> move toward safety -> recover -> explain what happened`

Do not turn the lesson into a disaster documentary. Keep it focused on the
story route, the light, and the girl's recovery.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is time order and past-perfect memory.

Teach these through the story:

- `what had happened to her`
  - The accident happened before she explained it.
- `after spending the whole night in the water`
  - She swam to the shore after a long night in the sea.
- `On arriving at the shore`
  - This means "when she arrived at the shore" or "as soon as she arrived."
- `the light she had seen`
  - She saw the light first, then later struggled toward that same light.
- `found herself in hospital`
  - She woke up and realized where she was.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson uses time phrases to make a survival route clear. First she sets
out, then the storm comes, then the boat hits a rock, then she swims all night,
then she sees the light, then she climbs, and only later can she explain what
had happened.

Use a compact board:

| Phrase | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| had happened | happened before she explained it |
| after spending... | after doing something for a long time |
| On arriving... | when / as soon as she arrived |
| had seen | saw earlier |
| found herself... | woke up and realized she was there |

Secondary language points:

- `set out from`
  - started a journey from a place.
- `was caught in a storm`
  - suddenly got into a storm and could not easily escape it.
- `Towards evening`
  - near evening / as evening came.
- `covered a distance of eight miles`
  - travelled or swam eight miles; not "covered" with something.
- `high up on the cliffs`
  - high above her on the cliff face.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `coast`
   - Meaning: the land area beside the sea.
   - Bridge: a coastal city, a road along the coast, setting out from the
     coast.
   - Example: `She set out from the coast in a small boat.`
2. `cliff`
   - Meaning: a high, steep rock face, often beside the sea.
   - Bridge: a light high up on the cliffs helps her know where land is.
   - Example: `The light was high up on the cliffs.`
3. `struggle`
   - Meaning: to try very hard to do something difficult.
   - Bridge: tired after swimming all night, she struggles up the cliff.
   - Example: `The girl struggled up the cliff towards the light.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `darkness` = no light; also the danger and confusion in this title
- `explain` = tell clearly what happened
- `set out` = start a journey
- `small boat` = a small boat, not a ship
- `storm` = very bad weather with strong wind and rain
- `towards` = moving in the direction of something
- `rock` = dangerous rock / reef in the sea in this lesson
- `shore` = the edge of the water where land begins
- `whole night` = all night
- `cover a distance` = travel a distance
- `eight miles` = a long distance to swim
- `light ahead` = a light in front of her
- `hospital` = place where she wakes up safe and receives care

## Scene And Script Direction

The script should feel like a survival route from darkness to light.

Recommended sequence:

1. Hook: "She was in the sea all night, but one light showed her the way."
2. Explain the title: literal darkness and emotional danger.
3. Bridge old story context with modern boating safety.
4. Explain the geography map: `coast`, `rock`, `shore`, `cliff`, `light ahead`.
5. Retell in 4 frames:
   - Frame 1: the girl sets out from the coast in a small boat; weather begins
     to change.
   - Frame 2: storm at sea; the boat strikes a rock; she jumps into the sea.
   - Frame 3: after swimming all night, she sees a light ahead high on the
     cliffs.
   - Frame 4: she struggles up the cliff toward the light and later wakes in
     hospital.
6. Discussion: why the light is the turning point.
7. Passage reading.
8. Vocab: `coast`, `cliff`, `struggle`, with the geography diagram.
9. Grammar: time order and past-perfect memory.
10. Living-scene transfer.
11. Outro: practice order, including read-aloud steps.

## Visual Direction

Keep the visuals cinematic but safe and educational.

- Main girl:
  - Teen girl or young woman, realistic and not superhero-like.
  - Same face, hair, and clothing across frames.
  - Wet and exhausted later, but not shown in a frightening or graphic way.
- Setting:
  - Coast, small boat, stormy sea, rock / reef, shore, high cliffs, distant
    light, hospital.
- Mood:
  - Frame 1: ordinary start with warning signs in the weather.
  - Frame 2: danger and storm, but not disaster spectacle.
  - Frame 3: quiet dawn / early morning hope with light on cliffs.
  - Frame 4: relief and safety after struggle.
- Safety:
  - No graphic injury, drowning panic close-up, or horror lighting.
  - The hospital scene should be calm and reassuring.
- Geography:
  - The cliff and light must be visually clear.
  - The route from sea to shore to cliff should be understandable.

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into safety, direction,
and asking for help.

Recommended scenario:

`You are on a school trip near the sea. The weather starts to change, and a friend wants to go closer to the water. You explain the danger, choose a safe direction, and tell an adult.`

Student should practice:

- noticing weather and danger
- using direction words
- saying where something is
- choosing a safe action
- asking an adult for help

Useful expressions:

- `The weather is getting worse.`
- `We should not go out in a small boat.`
- `The shore is over there.`
- `The light is ahead.`
- `Let's move towards the safe place.`
- `We should tell an adult now.`
- `We need help.`

The exercise should connect to the story without asking the student to act out
dangerous swimming.
