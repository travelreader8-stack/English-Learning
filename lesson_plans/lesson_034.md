## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about a man who thinks the police may want him for a serious
reason, but discovers they have found his stolen bicycle from twenty years ago.

Dan Robinson has been worried all week because he received a letter from the
local police. The letter asks him to call at the station. Dan wonders why he is
wanted by the police, so he goes to the station nervously. There, a smiling
policeman tells him that his bicycle has been found. The bicycle was picked up
five days ago in a small village four hundred miles away. It is now being sent
to Dan's home by train. Dan is very surprised and amused because he never
expected that bicycle to be found. It was stolen twenty years ago when he was a
boy of fifteen.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`police letter -> worry all week -> call at the station -> smiling policeman -> bicycle found -> small village 400 miles away -> sent by train -> surprised and amused -> stolen 20 years ago`

The ending should land as: "Dan was worried because he thought the police
wanted him, but the police only wanted to return a bicycle from his childhood."

## One-Sentence Hook

Dan thought the police wanted him for trouble, but they had found the bicycle he
lost twenty years ago.

## Police-Notice And Modern Bridge

This section should appear early in the script.

Explain:

- A letter from the local police can make someone nervous because they may not
  know why the police are contacting them.
- `local police` means the police in Dan's own area.
- `call at the station` is an older expression meaning visit / go to the police
  station.
- Dan is not in trouble. The smiling policeman gives good news.
- A stolen bicycle can sometimes be identified later if there is a record,
  frame number, report, or other clue.
- In modern life, this might happen by phone, text message, app notification,
  email, or a police call instead of a formal letter.
- Today a bicycle might also have GPS tags, security cameras, registration
  records, or online lost-and-found posts.

Suggested student-friendly bridge:

`Dan 收到警察局来信，第一反应不是高兴，而是紧张：警察为什么找我？这很真实。现在我们可能收到电话、短信或 App 通知，但那种“是不是我惹麻烦了”的感觉没有变。结果到了警察局，警察笑着说：你的自行车找到了。更好笑的是，那辆车不是昨天丢的，而是二十年前他十五岁时被偷的。`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old police-letter scene to present-day experiences:

- receiving an unexpected official message
- feeling nervous before knowing the reason
- losing a bicycle, scooter, phone, bag, or school item
- reporting a lost or stolen item
- police stations, lost-and-found offices, school offices, and delivery notices
- old records suddenly matching a found object
- items being returned by delivery service, courier, or train
- childhood objects becoming funny memories later

The durable pattern is:

`unexpected notice -> nervous imagination -> real explanation -> relief -> funny old story`

Do not make this a police-procedure lesson. Keep it as a human reaction story:
Dan worries first, then laughs.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is passive voice, especially when the story focuses on what
happened to Dan and the bicycle rather than who did each action.

Teach these through the story:

- `he was asked to call at the station`
  - The letter asked him; the passive focuses on Dan receiving the request.
- `he was wanted by the police`
  - Dan thinks the police want him for some reason.
- `he was told by a smiling policeman`
  - The policeman gives the news, but the focus stays on Dan.
- `his bicycle had been found`
  - The bicycle was found before Dan heard the news.
- `the bicycle was picked up`
  - Someone found / collected it, but the finder is not important.
- `It is now being sent to his home by train`
  - The bicycle is in the process of being sent now.
- `the bicycle to be found`
  - Dan never expected that result.
- `It was stolen twenty years ago`
  - The bicycle was stolen in the past; who stole it is unknown or unimportant.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson uses passive voice because the important question is not "Who did
every action?" The important question is "What happened to Dan and what happened
to the bicycle?" Dan was asked, Dan was told, the bicycle had been found, the
bicycle was picked up, and now it is being sent home.

Use a compact board:

| Passive phrase | Story meaning |
| --- | --- |
| was asked | the letter asked Dan to go |
| was wanted | Dan thought the police wanted him |
| was told | the policeman gave Dan news |
| had been found | the bicycle was found before Dan arrived |
| was picked up | the bicycle was found / collected |
| is being sent | it is on the way now |
| was stolen | someone stole it in the past |

Secondary language points:

- `has been worried all week`
  - Dan has felt worried from earlier in the week until now.
- `not worried anymore`
  - the worry has ended.
- `four hundred miles away`
  - very far from Dan's home.
- `by train`
  - using a train as the way to send it.
- `a boy of fifteen`
  - a fifteen-year-old boy.

## Grammar Board

Keep the grammar board tied to passive voice and time.

Board structure:

`Passive voice chain`

- `he was asked`
- `he was wanted`
- `he was told`
- `his bicycle had been found`
- `the bicycle was picked up`
- `It is now being sent`
- `the bicycle to be found`
- `It was stolen`

`Why passive?`

- Focus on Dan: what happened to him.
- Focus on the bicycle: what happened to it.
- The person who stole it or found it is not the main point.

`Time contrast`

- `all week`
- `Last Tuesday`
- `yesterday`
- `Five days ago`
- `now`
- `twenty years ago`
- `when Dan was a boy of fifteen`

Use the final contrast:

`Dan was worried about the police.`

`The police had found his bicycle.`

Avoid an abstract passive-voice lecture. The grammar should help the student
see why the story sounds like an official notice and a found-item report.
