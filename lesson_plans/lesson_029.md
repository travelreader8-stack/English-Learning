## Production Warnings

This lesson has many unfamiliar places and concepts. Production must help the
student memorize them as a route map, not as a random list of names.

- Explain the title joke early.
  - `taxi` normally means a car service.
  - In this lesson, the "taxi" is a small aeroplane.
  - The modern bridge can be `air taxi`, small-plane service, rescue flight, or
    remote-area transport.
- Do not make the plane feel like a toy or fantasy vehicle.
  - It is an unusual but professional small aeroplane service.
  - Captain Fawcett is skilled, but he is not reckless.
- Explain `Pilatus Porter` compactly.
  - It is the name of a small Swiss aeroplane.
  - The child does not need aircraft specifications.
  - The important point is: it can carry passengers and land in unusual places.
- Make a memory map.
  - Use the sequence:
    `who -> plane -> ability -> places served -> refused place -> safety reason`
  - This is the main scaffolding for recitation.
- Add a simplified geography bridge.
  - `Birmingham` is a city in England.
  - `Welsh mountains` are in Wales, west of England.
  - `Rockall` is a lonely remote rock/island in the Atlantic Ocean.
  - The map does not need to be precise, but it should show that Rockall is far
    out at sea and much more dangerous than the earlier destinations.
- Explain `lonely` carefully.
  - In this lesson, it often means remote / isolated, not emotionally sad.
- Explain `deserted`.
  - A deserted car park is empty and unused, not a desert.
- Explain `ploughed field`.
  - A field after farming soil has been turned over.
- Emphasize the final safety judgment.
  - The story is not "this plane can go anywhere, so it should go anywhere."
  - The important ending is that Captain Fawcett refuses the Rockall trip
    because it is too dangerous.

## Lesson Positioning

This lesson is about an unusual transport service and professional judgment.

Captain Ben Fawcett has bought an unusual taxi and has begun a new service. The
taxi is actually a small Swiss aeroplane called a Pilatus Porter. It can carry
seven passengers, and the most surprising thing is that it can land in unusual
places: on snow, on water, or even on a ploughed field. His first passenger was
a doctor who flew from Birmingham to a lonely village in the Welsh mountains.
Since then, he has flown passengers to many unusual places, including the roof
of a block of flats and a deserted car park. Then a businessman asks to fly to
Rockall, a lonely island in the Atlantic Ocean. Captain Fawcett refuses because
the trip is too dangerous.

The courseware should make the student understand:

`unusual taxi -> small Swiss aeroplane -> seven passengers -> unusual landings -> useful remote service -> strange request -> safety refusal`

The ending should land as: "A good pilot is not the person who says yes to every
request. A good pilot knows when to say no."

Teach the lesson as a route map:

`who -> what plane -> what it can do -> where it has gone -> where it refused to go -> why`

Use this exact memory chain:

`Captain Fawcett -> Pilatus Porter -> seven passengers / land anywhere -> doctor to Welsh mountains / roof / deserted car park -> Rockall -> too dangerous`

Break the text into four recitation blocks:

1. The service identity
   - `Captain Ben Fawcett`

   - `small Swiss aeroplane`

2. The plane's ability
   - `carry seven passengers`
   - `land anywhere`

## Modern Bridge

Connect the old air-taxi story to present-day experiences:

- air taxi or small-plane charter service
- helicopter rescue or medical transport
- remote villages where roads are slow or difficult
- island transport where boats or planes are needed
- drones or special vehicles that can reach unusual places
- a ride-hailing idea, but in the air instead of on the road
- a professional refusing a risky request even when the tool is powerful

The durable pattern is:

`special tool -> unusual service -> impressive ability -> professional limit`

Do not make the modern bridge about luxury private jets. Keep it about transport
to hard-to-reach places and responsible safety decisions.

## Main Language Focus

The main grammar is present perfect for a service that has developed up to now,
plus `can` for ability and safety boundaries.

Teach it through the story:

- `has bought`
  - Captain Fawcett has bought the unusual taxi.
- `has begun`
  - He has begun a new service.
- `Since then, he has flown...`
  - From that time until now, he has flown passengers to many places.
- `has just refused`
  - He has recently refused a strange request.
- `can carry`
  - The plane has the ability to carry seven passengers.
- `can land`
  - The plane can land in unusual places.
- `did not take him because...`
  - Ability does not remove judgment; he can fly, but should not take this trip.

Student-facing explanation:

This lesson uses present perfect to show the history of Captain Fawcett's new
service: he has bought the plane, has begun the service, has flown to unusual
places, and has just refused a dangerous request. The word `can` shows ability,
but the ending shows safety judgment.

Use a compact contrast:

| Idea | Sentence |
| --- | --- |
| service has started | has bought / has begun |
| service has history | Since then, he has flown... |
| plane ability | can carry / can land |
| professional boundary | did not take him because the trip was too dangerous |

Secondary language points:

- `however`
  - introduces the surprising point about landing anywhere.
- `or even`
  - makes the last item more surprising.
- `Since then`
  - from that time until now.
- `Once... on another occasion`
  - two example events.
- `because`
  - gives the reason for refusal.

## Vocabulary Plan

Formal vocab blocks should be exactly 3:

1. `service`
   - Meaning: work done for other people, often regularly or as a business.
   - Bridge: taxi service, delivery service, medical service, air-taxi service.
   - Example: `He has begun a new service.`
2. `passenger`
   - Meaning: a person who travels in a vehicle but does not drive or fly it.
   - Bridge: taxi passenger, bus passenger, plane passenger.
   - Example: `This wonderful plane can carry seven passengers.`
3. `dangerous`
   - Meaning: not safe; likely to cause harm or serious risk.
   - Bridge: a road, trip, storm, landing, or request can be dangerous.
   - Example: `Captain Fawcett did not take him because the trip was too dangerous.`

Supporting vocabulary to introduce in context, not as full vocab cards:

- `taxi` = normally a car service; here a small aeroplane service
- `Captain` = pilot title / person in command
- `unusual` = not common
- `aeroplane` = airplane
- `Swiss` = from Switzerland
- `Pilatus Porter` = name of the small Swiss aeroplane
- `land` = come down to the ground or water safely
- `snow` = frozen white water from the sky
- `ploughed field` = farm field with soil turned over
- `Birmingham` = city in England
- `lonely village` = remote / isolated village
- `Welsh mountains` = mountains in Wales
- `block of flats` = apartment building
- `roof` = top of a building
- `deserted car park` = empty car park
- `request` = something someone asks for
- `businessman` = person who works in business
- `Rockall` = remote rocky island / rock in the Atlantic Ocean
- `Atlantic Ocean` = ocean west of Britain

## Living-Scene Exercise Direction

The living-scene exercise should transfer the lesson into a modern special-
service and safety-judgment scene.

Recommended scenario:

`You operate a special transport service. Your vehicle can go to unusual places, but one customer's request is too dangerous. You need to explain what your service can do and why you must refuse.`

Student should practice:

- explaining an unusual service
- saying what a vehicle can do
- describing passengers and destinations
- refusing politely for safety reasons
- using `because` to give a reason

Useful expressions:

- `This is an unusual service.`
- `It can carry... passengers.`
- `It can go to unusual places.`
- `I cannot take you there.`
- `The trip is too dangerous.`
- `Safety is more important.`

The exercise should make refusal feel responsible, not rude.
